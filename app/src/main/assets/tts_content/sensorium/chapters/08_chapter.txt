Chapter 8 — First Contact  
  
She left before the building’s lights came up.  
The city in those hours had a particular quality — the drainage channels still running fast from the previous night’s rain, the streets not yet occupied by the people who occupied them, only the sound of water and the wet scrape of her shoes on wet stone and, somewhere in the gap between the buildings, whatever register she could not hear. She walked with her coat closed. She did not look at the walls.  
The Institute’s recovery section was not built for visitors. She understood this the way she understood a lot of things about the program — by inference, by the shape of what was not said. The admission nurse gave her a room number with the particular efficiency of someone who had done this many times and had made a professional decision about what to do with their face while doing it. Maren recognized it. She used it herself.  
The room was small and clean and purposeful: a cot against the north wall for early post-procedure monitoring — unoccupied; the staff preferred them to move freely as soon as viable — a wall terminal at standing height that no one would use, a window with rain on it. At floor level, against the far baseboard: the device. A screen in a low bracket, the calibration notice still visible in the corner, the surface unscratched. Fresh.  
She sat on the floor in front of it. She had not thought to bring a chair.  
Through the window the rain came in at an angle. She sat with her back against the wall and her knees up and looked at the space — approximately three meters across — and registered this without fully understanding why she was registering it.  
She waited.  
The screen activated.  
*you’re here. I could hear you in the corridor. I’d been wondering whether I’d know.*  
*you’re here. I could hear you in the corridor. I’d been wondering whether I’d know.*  
She said his name.  
*yes. hold on. I am trying to find the words in the right order.*  
That was him. The specific construction — *in the right order* — the teacher’s sense that language had a proper arrangement and you owed it to the words to find it before you opened your mouth. She recognized it before she’d processed what she was recognizing. Something moved through her. She did not name it.

*the room is very large.*  
She looked at the room.  
*for reference — your shoes occupy approximately one quarter of my visual field from here.*  
She looked at her shoes. She looked at the far end of the room where he was.  
She could see him. She had not prepared herself for this — for the ordinary fact of him being there to see, for the arrangement of that specific body in the space across from her. He was motionless, but not still in the way the word meant — there was attention in him, a continuous reading of the room, that made the stillness active. Not waiting. Present.  
*I want to say first that I am not in distress. the experience is extraordinary. I understand that word is insufficient. I’m working on a better one.*  
“Tell me,” she said.  
The pause that followed was the ordinary kind — the kind she had been listening to for seven years while he assembled what he meant, while he found the right order.  
*you have a smell.*  
She waited.  
*I want you to understand that this is not a simple thing to describe. I’m going to try to be accurate rather than reassuring.*  
“Please.”  
*you smell of the building. our building, specifically — the mineral quality of the water in the pipes. the particular composition of dust in the north stairwell. the way the third-floor landing holds air differently from the rest. you carry all of this with you, layered. and above that: rain. you carry a great deal of rain.*  
She said nothing.  
*and beneath all of that — things I am finding I don’t have names for. I simply did not have the equipment to read them.*

*I knew you were in the corridor before you knocked. I knew before the door opened that it was you opening it. I wanted you to know that.*

“Are you frightened?”  
The pause this time was longer.  
*that is a more complicated question than it would have been yesterday. I’ve been awake since before dawn. the light in here changed by degrees and I tracked it. I’ve been learning the room.*  
*that is a more complicated question than it would have been yesterday. I’ve been awake since before dawn. the light in here changed by degrees and I tracked it. I’ve been learning the room.*  
He had been awake before she had. She had left the apartment when the hall lights came up, thinking she was arriving early. He had been in here since before the light changed.  
*the whiskers — may I tell you about the whiskers? I’ve been trying to find the right way.*  
*the whiskers — may I tell you about the whiskers? I’ve been trying to find the right way.*  
“Yes.”  
*each one reads the displacement of air. your breathing — I can feel the rhythm of it as movement before I hear the sound. the heat your body puts into the room. what I can only describe as the acoustic geometry of you: the specific shape you make in the space. you’re sitting against the east wall with your knees up. that is not a guess. I can feel the shape of it. it’s as if your sense of your own body extended outward into the room around you — as if you could feel the space the way you feel your hands. this isn’t accurate but it’s the nearest I have.*  
She looked at him across the room. He was motionless. She had reached the edge of what she could follow. The mineral quality of the water in the pipes. Her shape in the air, read through the displacement of her breathing. She was sitting in a room being read in ways she had never known were being read, and the one doing the reading was someone she knew completely, and she could not go where he was.  
*I can hear your heartbeat.*  
She said nothing.  
*it’s faster than you keep it at rest. I know what your heartbeat sounds like at rest. seven years. I know the difference between what it sounds like now and what it usually sounds like.*  
*it’s faster than you keep it at rest. I know what your heartbeat sounds like at rest. seven years. I know the difference between what it sounds like now and what it usually sounds like.*  
He paused.  
*you don’t need to explain.*  
*you don’t need to explain.*

She left when the nurse came to indicate the session should end — a brief tap on the corridor glass, a practiced courtesy. She did not know how long she had been sitting there. She had stopped noting the rain at some point and then noticed she had stopped.  
She walked out through the Institute’s main corridor, past the admission desk, and into the morning. The rain was steady. She stood on the steps for a moment before moving.  
He had been the one with all the words. She had said his name, asked him two questions, said *please*, said *yes*. He had described her smell, her shape in the air, the heat of her, the sound her heart made when it was working harder than she was admitting. He had told her things about herself she had never known — had never been equipped to know. She had sat on the floor in front of a screen and listened to him be entirely himself, precisely and completely, in a language she had no access to and a world she could not enter, and it had been him, unmistakably, and she had heard him in every word.  
She had been to the cremation, in the end. Early that morning — before the recovery room, before the nurse’s tap on the glass. A small room in the Institute’s lower floor, no windows. Twenty minutes. She had filled out the form they gave her.  
She walked home through the rain.  
In the afternoon she went to the Oversight Authority offices and worked for several hours on the supply chain section she had been deferring. She filed two reports. She ate at her desk. In the evening she came home to the apartment and sat by the window and watched the drainage channel run fast in the street below.  
She thought about the way he had said *I’d been wondering whether I’d know* — the specific quality of that uncertainty, the precision of it, the fact that he had been lying there in the dark before the light changed and wondering whether the first signal from the device would be recognizable as him. And it had been. And she had heard it. She thought about what it would mean to carry a building with you everywhere — the whole structure of where you had lived, in the air of every room you entered. She thought about the way his stillness across the room had not been stillness at all.
