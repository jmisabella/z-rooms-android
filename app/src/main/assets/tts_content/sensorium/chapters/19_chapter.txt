Chapter 19 — The Plea  
  
The drive was in her pocket. She had not removed it since crossing the Institute’s forecourt bridge that morning.  
She went to him that afternoon.  
The room: the seam in the ceiling panel where the addition met the original construction, the catch pan with its slow percussion, the folding chair too large for the space. She sat forward with her elbows on her knees and the floor-level terminal at the far wall, screen dark. She waited.  
*maren.*  
“Yes,” she said.  
He was still across the room — not passive, not waiting. That active stillness she had learned to read, the continuous reading of the space. His attention on her.  
“I found something,” she said. “I need to tell you it’s there.”  
She did not say what. “It changes the shape of the choice I have to make. I don’t know yet what I’ll do. But I found it.”  
The screen held while he received this. She counted the drips into the catch pan without meaning to.  
*okay.*  
A pause.  
*does it help.*  
“I don’t know yet,” she said.  
*you’re carrying it.*  
“Yes,” she said.  
He was still. The rain moved along the addition’s roof seam and found the joint and the catch pan received it. She thought about the word *gap*, alone on the terminal in the lobby that morning — the screen holding it, and then clearing. She looked at the floor-level terminal.  
He was still. The rain moved along the addition’s roof seam and found the joint and the catch pan received it. She thought about the word *gap*, alone on the terminal in the lobby that morning — the screen holding it, and then clearing. She looked at the floor-level terminal.  
He was still there.  
*I don’t want you to become this.*  
She sat with it. The room sat with it. The catch pan took its next drop.  
She understood him.  
He was still there enough to see her clearly. He had used what remained for this.  
She did not answer. She moved her hand from her lap and set it open beside the terminal, palm up on the floor.  
His head shifted — a fraction, toward the warmth of it. Held there.  
They stayed like that.  
Outside: rain, unchanged. The addition’s roof held it and passed it to the seam. The room was full of the sound of water finding its way.  
After a while she said his name quietly. The way you say something just to have it in the air.  
*good*, the screen said.  
*come back.*  
“Yes,” she said.  
She sat until the nurse tapped the corridor glass. She stood. She looked at the terminal. She said goodnight to it.  
The screen held his last lines.  
Outside on the steps she stood in the rain without moving. The drive was in her pocket. She held his words, did not try to put them down. She moved when she was ready, and not before.
