  
Chapter 1 — Arrive  
  
The drainage channels were running fast. She crossed the third one at the narrow step she’d learned to use in the dark — two blocks from the apartment — and came up on the far pavement with her coat past damp.  
The buildings on the east side were in the usual state: declining, slowly, in the manner of things that had been declining so long the decline had become structural, had become the building. The original walls were still there, more or less. What was missing was everything that would have kept them as they were. Window casings gone loose and never reattached. Mineral staining in long vertical smears where some seam had been losing water for a decade or longer. The corner unit at the far end of the block was doing something complicated with its foundation that she had been noting for approximately a year. It had not resolved.  
Lights on in the upper floors. The rain fell steadily, without drama, the way it always fell here. The street smelled of wet concrete and something older underneath it — the slow exhale of two hundred years of walls holding water, the rot at the margins where the drainage hadn’t quite reached.  
At the base of the foundation, three rats were moving along the wall. She watched them the way she had learned to watch such things: not quite directly. They crossed to the drain channel and went where they went. She walked on.  
She came in late. She could smell that he’d cooked — something with garlic, something that had been warm an hour ago and was now the memory of warm. He’d left it on the stove with a cloth over the pot.  
He’d cleared the table. She put her bag down in the space.  
He was at the far end of the room with a stack of papers, marking. The lamp was on his side, which meant her side of the table was in the lower light, which was how she preferred it. He had arranged this without her having to say so. He had stopped saying anything about it approximately six years ago; he’d just started doing it.  
She served herself from the pot and sat.  
For a while there was only the rain and the scratch of his pen.  
He read something aloud. A student’s sentence: *the machines inherited the sky and we were left with the names we used to have for it.* He said it the way he said things he found — not to her, exactly. Just: into the room. He picked up his pen again.  
He read something aloud. A student’s sentence: *the machines inherited the sky and we were left with the names we used to have for it.* He said it the way he said things he found — not to her, exactly. Just: into the room. He picked up his pen again.  
She ate. She did not look up.  
“That’s good,” she said.  
“Yes.” He made a note in the margin. “He thinks too fast for his sentences. But he’s getting better.”  
She finished eating. She opened her report.  
The rain.  
He read another one, later. This one he didn’t comment on. She didn’t either. There were sentences like that — ones that didn’t need anything said about them.  
She got to the section of the report that had been bothering her for three days. The section she kept writing around. She read the last sentence she’d written, which was not the sentence she meant, and stopped.  
“There’s a word,” she said, “for when an investigation has gone the right way but arrived at the wrong place.”  
He was quiet for a moment. She hadn’t looked up; she could hear him thinking.  
“Pyrrhic is close but it implies a victory.” He set his pen down. “What you’re describing is more like — the investigation found what it was looking for and the finding made things worse.”  
“Yes.”  
“That’s not one word. It might be. It might be in a language that isn’t this one.”  
She looked up at him.  
“I’ll keep looking,” he said. He picked up his pen.  
“Thank you.”  
“I’ll tell you if I find it.”  
She went back to the report. The section still didn’t want to be written, but the weight of it had shifted, distributed into the space between them. She wrote one sentence. Then another. They weren’t the right sentences yet, but they were sentences, which was a start.  
At some point she became aware that her hands were open on the table, loose, not braced against anything. She could not have said when this happened.  
Much later he put down his papers and stretched and said he was going to sleep, and she said she’d be in soon, and he said alright. He turned off his lamp and moved through the dark apartment toward the back room — the particular ease of someone who knew exactly where everything was. She heard him in the bathroom, the familiar sequence of sounds. Then quiet.  
She sat in the remaining light.  
The city outside the window was doing what the city did: draining, cycling, persisting in the particular way of something that had been persisting a very long time and had stopped noticing it was tired.  
She closed the report.  
She had not found the word. She had not needed to, quite.
