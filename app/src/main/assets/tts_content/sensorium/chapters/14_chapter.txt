Chapter 14 — Nadia  
  
She went the following evening.  
The address was in the second outer ring — past the point where the city’s power grid ended and the buildings relied on salvage or nothing. Some of both. She came in on foot from the canal-side, which added twenty minutes but kept her off the two streets where she’d have been visible from the functioning district. She had not told anyone she was coming. She had not written it in her log.  
The rain was steady and lateral, the kind that found the gap between collar and neck regardless of how you positioned yourself. She stopped once under an overhang — a former storefront, the shutters long gone — and looked at the building across the narrow street. Three stories. Windows lit on the upper floor, dark on the second, a single lamp behind board-reinforced glass at street level. Someone had patched the roof seam on the west side; the patch was newer than the building and darker in the rain. Someone was taking care of it.  
She crossed the street. A door on the south face: no bell, no marking, the kind of door whose identity was absence. She knocked twice the way the contact had told her.  
A man opened it. Younger, forties, with the build of someone who had once worked physically and still carried it in the way he moved. He looked at her face and then at her hands. He stepped aside.  
The first floor was a pass-through. Someone had a small cook fire in the back room — she could smell it, hear a conversation she wasn’t meant to catch. She followed the man up two flights, past a landing where water stains mapped the wall in geological layers. At the top, a door already open.  
The room had no windows on the outer wall. Two boarded, she could see the seams — a third, on the interior side, faced whatever the building faced across a narrow shaft. The source of light was two salvaged lamps, both adequate, both angled toward the center of the room where a table sat with two chairs already positioned at it.  
She was already there.  
—————  
Nadia did not stand when Maren came in. She watched her cross to the chair and sit, the way you watched something you had been expecting.  
She was older than Maren had assembled from the network’s margins. Late forties, maybe past fifty — not the years themselves but a quality of having processed something over a long time and arrived somewhere on the other side of the processing. Her hands were on the table, still. She wore nothing that suggested anything about herself except practical choices made in a practical environment. The only thing Maren had anticipated, from what she’d read in the investigation’s gaps, was precision. The precision was there. The rest of her was quieter than a resistance leader had any business being.  
“You’ve been working toward this for a while,” she said.  
Not a question. A fact, offered so they could agree on it and begin.  
“About six weeks,” Maren said.  
“I know what you’re carrying,” she said. “I know why you came without your partner.”  
Maren waited.  
“I’m not going to ask about your investigation,” she said. “That’s not why you’re here.”  
“Then what am I here for,” Maren said.  
She looked at Maren for a moment. Not assessing — she had already assessed. The quality of someone who has asked a question so many times that asking it once more is not an effort, just an act.  
“I want to ask you something. About Eli.”  
Maren said nothing.  
“Is that Eli in the rat, or a pattern that believes it is Eli?” she asked.  
She asked it the way a physician asks a question the patient already knows the answer to and has been managing not to hear. Not cruelly. Not rhetorically. The question was clinical in the best sense — clinical meaning: concerned with the actual thing, not the thing we prefer.  
Maren did not answer.  
She had been living with the question since the room with the dripping ceiling panel, since the screen going dark mid-sentence. Since the footsteps. Since *sorry. came back. something — I don’t have the word.* She had been living with it before that, since the early transfer three years ago, since she had written *expected sensory adjustment* and gone home and not slept. She had it now — the exact words, from this woman across a table in a room with no outer windows — and she found that having the exact words changed nothing about the not-answering.  
“Make your argument,” Maren said.  
Nadia did not make the argument as though she were making an argument. She made it the way someone describes a place they know well — the turns you take, what you’ll find there.  
“Consciousness,” she said, “is not substrate-independent. This is the Institute’s assumption — the premise under everything Harlan says when he addresses a room and explains the process. He assumes the pattern is the person. He assumes the pattern persists, unchanged in any meaningful way, if you write it to a new system. He assumes the human brain is replaceable as a substrate, the way you might transfer a message from one medium to another without losing it. This is the assumption the transplant is built on. It has never been proven. It cannot be proven, because the only evidence is the testimony of the copy, which has every reason to believe itself continuous with the person it replaces.”  
She paused. Not for effect. The pause of someone who has measured the next sentence and is about to set it down with care.  
“The experience of being a specific person is produced by the specific, irreplaceable biological configuration that developed that person. Your history, your memory, your accumulated self — these emerge from a particular brain, over decades, through a particular body’s interaction with a particular world. They are not separable from that substrate. They appear separable, because the mapped pattern contains them. But the map is not the territory.”  
“The transplanted communicate,” Maren said. “They remember. They use the same language, the same habits, the same —“  
“Yes,” she said.  
She said it simply, the word dropping into the sentence like something with weight.  
“The copy is faithful. Detailed. Subjectively convinced. It will tell you — and it will mean it — that it is the person. It contains everything the person contained at the moment of transfer. It has every reason to believe itself continuous with the person. This is consistent with what I’m telling you, not a refutation of it. The question is not whether the copy experiences itself as the original. The question is whether that experience corresponds to something real. The person’s brain was destroyed. The person died on the table. Someone else woke up in the rat, carrying everything the person had, and has been living inside the claim of continuity ever since.”  
“You’re asking me to believe that Eli —“ Maren said.  
“I’m asking what you believe,” she said. “Not what the program’s model says. Not what your employer’s policy is. What you believe when you are in that room with him.”  
Maren looked at her hands on the table.  
“I believe he’s there,” Maren said. “I hear him in it.”  
“I believe you hear something,” she said. “I’m asking whether what you hear is the man or the echo.”  
They sat with that. The rain worked at the boarded windows.  
“One of my people said something, coming back from an expedition to the northern city. Three years ago. We sent four people overland — the route had been passable, once. We wanted to know if there was anyone left to reach.  
“They found the city intact. Structurally. Buildings standing, streets navigable, the infrastructure running on its own logic, the way infrastructure does when no one has been maintaining it for long enough. No people. The team spent three days.  
“They came back with a report they hadn’t expected to agree on. Rats everywhere. Not in the way of a city where humans have vacated and the rats have moved in to fill the absence. In the way of something already oriented. Already settled. Not afraid.”  
She stopped. One of the salvaged lamps made a small sound, the filament adjusting.  
“One of the team told me that wherever he walked, the rats watched him. Not scattered — watched. He said, ‘*they watched me the whole time I was there.*’”  
“One of the team told me that wherever he walked, the rats watched him. Not scattered — watched. He said, ‘*they watched me the whole time I was there.*’”  
Maren waited.  
“The Institute knows about the northern city,” she said. “They have the expedition reports. They have reports from others — the eastern coast, the river delta settlements. They call it completion. They say the transplant program reached those populations first. They say the silence that followed is not loss.  
“I want you to know what completion looks like, so that when Harlan uses that word — and he will use that word — you have something to hold it against.”  
—————  
They talked for another hour. Maren put her counter-arguments and put them honestly, because she was a program defender professionally and by prior conviction and because she could not argue well from a position she didn’t hold. “The evidence for substrate independence isn’t settled,” she said. “The degradation arc is documented but its endpoint is unknown. Even if the copy argument were correct, a faithful copy that believes itself the person is still —“  
“Is still what,” she said. “Is still preferable to death? Is still the person in any meaningful sense?”  
“I don’t know,” Maren said.  
“That’s the most honest answer I’ve heard from someone in your position in a long time,” she said.  
It did not feel like a compliment. It felt like an accurate description of where things were.  
“I’m not asking you to agree with me. I’m not asking for conversion. I’m asking you to keep looking at what you’re already looking at.”  
“That I’m doing,” Maren said.  
The session ended without resolution, which was not the same as ending badly. Maren had anticipated an ideologue — someone with the close-held certainty of the long-wronged — and had found instead a woman who held her position the way a scientist holds a hypothesis: provisionally, openly, with the evidence she had and without the evidence she hadn’t. The argument was complete. It was not closed. She would need to come back; she knew this before she stood up from the chair.  
—————  
She went down the two flights, past the geological stairwell, out the south door into the rain. The man who had let her in was not there. The narrow street was empty.  
She stood for a moment under no overhang in the lateral rain and knew what she was not doing.  
She had been building a case for six weeks, methodically, the way she built cases — through supply chains and logistics patterns and the specific grammar of how a distributed network organized its patience. She had enough. She had more than enough. She had a location, a face, a formal thread she could take to Soren and a report she could write tonight that would close the operational case and hand the conclusion to the Authority and be done with it. She knew what it would cost not to do this. She knew that people had died in attacks this network had enabled — not by Nadia’s hand directly, and not without her decisions somewhere in the margins of the work.  
She was not going to turn Nadia in. She knew this the way she knew the decision had already been made when she wrote the address in her notebook instead of her log. She noted it as a fact, without commentary, without justification, and then she walked. The word she had waited for had not arrived. The drainage channel along the canal-side street was running high and dark from the outer district runoff. She crossed it. Beneath her coat the notebook was where she had put it, the address still in the wrong place, and she walked with that weight and did not try to redistribute it.
