Chapter 2 — Home  
  
She came home to fennel.  
He had found it somewhere — she had no idea where — because she had said something about it six weeks ago, or seven: the supply had been intermittent for months, she had stopped expecting it. She hadn’t been asking for anything.  
He had cleared the table again. She set her bag in the space.  
“Ten minutes,” he said, not turning from the stove.  
She hung her coat and sat and opened her report.  
He turned away from the stove a few minutes later — toward the wall — and it was brief, and his hand went to the counter edge, and then he was back to the pot. She turned a page of the report. Neither of them said anything. They had had fourteen months to make this adjustment.  
He served. She picked up her spoon. The fennel came through clean in the broth, the particular smell of it, and she was aware that she had mentioned it once and he had gone looking and had not said anything about that and would not. She ate.  
His lamp on his side, her side in lower light — the arrangement he had made without asking and had stopped remarking on years ago. He had a book beside his bowl, marked-up, pencil in hand.  
Later he read something aloud. A student’s sentence: *the idea that there was a better time is doing a lot of work for a lot of people.* Not to her. Into the room. He set the book down and wrote a note in the margin.  
She didn’t look up. “Young?”  
“Third year. She’s been angry at nostalgia for a semester. Finally figuring out what to do with it.” He kept writing. “She’ll work herself out of it, probably. Some of them do.”  
“That’s too bad.”  
He laughed. Short, genuine. She went back to the report.  
The rain. The scratch of his pen.  
She put the report down.  
He was bent over the page, the pen moving in the small careful way he’d always worked — not hesitant, just exact. He was frowning at something he’d written, not dissatisfied, just: still with it. She watched him. She wasn’t watching for anything in particular. She was just there, with him, in the apartment that smelled of fennel and rain, for a moment not managing anything at all.  
He looked up. He saw her.  
He held it for a moment — a second, maybe two — then looked back down at the page and kept writing. She looked back at the report.  
The weight that had been sitting at the base of her sternum since morning had redistributed. She had not done anything to move it.  
“Dael had a good session this morning,” he said, a little later, not looking up. “Six or seven full sentences. He was telling me about the ventilation runs in the east corridor — apparently the sound travels through the building structure in a way he hadn’t expected. He seems interested in it.”  
“That’s good.”  
“He asked after you.” He turned a page. “I said you’d been working.”  
“I have.”  
“I know.” He said it without emphasis, which was how he said things he wasn’t complaining about.  
She finished the section. Not well, but adequately. She could revise it in the morning.  
She left the report where it was. His lamp was still on — on his side of the table, where he had placed it years ago without ceremony, and where it would stay.
