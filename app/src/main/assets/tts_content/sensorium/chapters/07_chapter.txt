Chapter 7 — The Procedure  
  
The call came at four in the morning. By the time Maren arrived, the eastern facility was lit with emergency lamps hauled in from somewhere and arranged into a poor approximation of the lighting that no longer worked. They were not the same color as working lights. Everything in the space looked slightly wrong.  
She walked the pre-procedure wing first.  
Six rooms. Three candidates dead — two in the first room, one in the corridor between rooms three and four. The corridor didn’t fit yet; she’d return to it. Two more had been pulled out and transferred to the Institute’s main facility in the first chaotic hour after discovery: alive, their procedures suspended, their appointment dates — which they had arranged their lives around for months — gone. The sixth had been in prep sedation when the team came through and was physically unharmed. The room had not been entered.  
She didn’t know yet why that room had not been entered. She wrote it down.  
The equipment was destroyed in the way things got destroyed when someone understood how they worked: not simply broken but rendered irreparable, specific components removed and likely taken. The power array for the neural mapping system — one of four in the city — was gone. Not damaged. Gone.  
Soren was at the intake end when she came through. He handed her the access log without comment.  
She read it. The delivery corridor had been accessed at 0112. The main entry at 0109 — three minutes before. Two different points of access.  
“Two operational signatures,” she said. “Three in the equipment damage.”  
He looked at her.  
“The array was taken, not destroyed. That’s different hands from the structural damage.” She indicated the far room. “Someone was precise. Someone else wasn’t.”  
“Three people. Two came in by delivery. One through the front.”  
“With standard facility clearance.” She handed him back the log. “That’s not the fringe.”  
Soren said nothing, which was agreement.  
She walked the corridor a second time. The space between rooms three and four — the place that didn’t fit. She stood with it. The third candidate hadn’t been in either room. She had been in the corridor. She had been moving, which meant she had known something was coming and had tried to reach the stairwell. She had not reached the stairwell.  
She walked the corridor’s length and then she found the other thing: the emergency override on room four’s internal lock had been activated from inside, before the team came through. The sixth candidate, the unharmed one. Someone inside that room had locked the door from inside.  
Someone inside that room had known they were coming.  
She stood with this for a moment. Then she put it at the back of her thinking and kept moving. The Council would be meeting in three hours.  
—————  
She caught Soren at the exit. Rain had started, coming in sideways off the eastern drainage channel, and they stood just inside the doorway.  
“The supply signature,” she said. “The timing components. The couplings.”  
“Canal service road.”  
“Yes.” She had written it down three weeks ago. The same material signature. The same hands.  
“The Council is going to want someone in a room by the end of the week,” Soren said.  
“I know.”  
“You have enough to move on the supply chain.”  
She did. They both knew it.  
“I want to understand the front-door access first,” she said. “The supply chain leads back to what we’ve already found. The access pattern is something else.”  
He had a particular quality in these moments — not impatient, not agreeable, simply waiting to see which direction this went. Four years had given her this catalog. He accepted what she said. They went out into the rain.  
—————  
Two days later, she came home to Eli. He was sitting at the table.  
He hadn’t cleared it. His papers were still spread — the fragment he’d been working, a student assessment half-marked. He was sitting in the middle of all of it, not working, just sitting with his hands still.  
She put her bag down beside the table rather than in the usual space. She sat across from him.  
“I need you to hear something,” he said.  
She waited.  
“I’ve scheduled the consultation. The assessment and the procedure planning. I have a date.”  
“When,” she said.  
“Three weeks.”  
She looked at his hands on the papers. The ink stain under his left thumb. She had been looking at that stain for seven years.  
“I know what it costs,” he said. “I’d rather pay it.”  
She tried to find the answer. She could feel the shape of what she wanted to say — the real argument, the one that had been sitting just behind her throat since the man on the observation table had gone still mid-sentence and come back and the screen had said *sorry. smell. it was very strong. *She had the word she had used for that: *adjustment*. She had watched the way the word covered something, the way it functioned as a door, and she had kept the door closed for three years, and now he was asking her to use it for him.  
She couldn’t say it. To say it required believing it, and believing it meant believing it was going to happen to him, and she was not ready — had not been ready for three years — and now there was no more time and she was still not ready.  
“Okay,” she heard herself say.  
He looked at her. He knew the difference between her agreement and her surrender. He didn’t push.  
“I’m not asking you to be there,” he said. “I want to say that separately. I’m not asking.”  
She nodded.  
He reached across the table — not reaching for her, just settling a paper that didn’t need settling. She watched his hands.  
“The fragment,” he said. “I’d like you to keep it.”  
“I’ll keep it.”  
They sat with it for a while. Outside the window, the rain. His lamp threw its light toward his side of the table, and her side was in the lower register, and this had always been the arrangement.  
—————  
She worked.  
The consultation happened. The assessment happened. The neural mapping took several days — she knew the sequence; she had observed it from the other side of a window for eleven years. She ran her investigation and filed her reports and followed the access pattern from the eastern facility, which was leading somewhere she could not yet name. She did not attend the consultation. She did not attend the mapping sessions. She did not attend.  
The procedure was scheduled for a Thursday.  
On Thursday, she was at her desk in the Oversight Authority offices by six in the morning. The facility was on the other side of the city. The session would start at nine. She worked. She wrote the section of the supply chain report she had been deferring. She took a call from a registrar at the Institute who had information that was marginally useful. She ate at her desk. Soren came by at two in the afternoon, left a summary she had asked for, didn’t linger. She kept working.  
At four-seventeen, her device signaled.  
She picked it up. A notification from the facility’s administrative contact — the kind sent to designated family. *Procedure complete. Remains scheduled for cremation. Recovery commenced.*  
She read it twice.  
She set the device on the desk.  
He had not asked her to come. He had known she would not be there — not because he did not want her, but because seven years had taught him the exact shape of what she could and could not carry. He had not asked her to carry differently. He had made room for the shape she was, without naming the shape, without pointing at the making. That he had done this even at this — even on this day, even at the last thing — was not a small thing. It was the kind of love that didn’t announce itself. It was the kind that cost more to hold than to have given.  
She put the device in her pocket.  
She finished the report she was writing.  
Outside the window, it was raining. The drainage channel below the building was running fast and grey-brown. A rat moved along the channel wall — small, purposeful — and was gone before she had fully registered it.  
She filed the report. She put on her coat.  
She went home to the apartment.  
He had cleared the table before he left. Her side was open. She stood in the doorway for a moment, looking at the space he had made.  
The lamp on his side of the table was off. She did not turn on another.
