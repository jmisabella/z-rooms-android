Chapter 13 — The Freeze  
  
She had the location at half past three in the afternoon.  
The contact had given it to her the way he’d given her everything else: as a fact, without ceremony, as if he’d been carrying it long enough that handing it off was simply a way of setting it down. *She’ll decide if she wants to talk to you.* And then an address, and a time, and nothing more. She thanked him. He nodded the way people nodded when they were done.  
The contact had given it to her the way he’d given her everything else: as a fact, without ceremony, as if he’d been carrying it long enough that handing it off was simply a way of setting it down. *She’ll decide if she wants to talk to you.* And then an address, and a time, and nothing more. She thanked him. He nodded the way people nodded when they were done.  
She wrote the address in her notebook. Not her log. Her notebook was not searchable; it did not go into the formal record. She had been using it for things she was still working out. She told herself this. The address went in below two lines about supply chain timing and above a sketch of a distribution node she’d never followed up on.  
She looked at it for a moment. Then she closed the notebook.  
The question she did not ask herself: *when did I decide?* The decision was already made when she wrote it down there instead of in the log. The decision was already made before the contact finished giving her the address. She was not certain when it had happened, only that it had, and that she recognized it now the way she recognized the finish of a thing that had been going for a while. The way the rain recognized a drain.  
She put the notebook in her bag and looked at the reports on her desk, which needed finishing, which she would finish.  
—————  
Soren came by at a quarter to five.  
He brought his own file — he’d been working the salvage service thread, the man identified in both attacks, the reregistered holding company. Clean, patient work. He set the file on the edge of her desk.  
“Good progress on the service company,” she said.  
“There’s a second address registered to the holding company. Eastern district, canal-side. I wanted to ask — your log shows you were near there last week. The Wednesday afternoon block.”  
“I was. I went back to the canal contact from August. She’d mentioned a secondary broker she hadn’t named the first time.”  
He waited. He was good at waiting.  
“It didn’t produce much. A name I already had. I didn’t log it because I was going to combine it with the salvage thread when they connected. They haven’t connected yet.”  
He looked at her. Not long — the length of a confirmation, not an accusation. She held it.  
“I’ll flag the canal address for this week.”  
“Let me know what’s there.”  
He picked up the file. Heading toward the door: “The report for the tenth is overdue. In case you hadn’t noticed.”  
“I noticed.”  
He left. She waited until she heard him at his own desk before she looked at her bag, where the notebook was, where the address was. She thought: he noticed. She thought: he let it go.  
She thought: he always lets things go at exactly the right moment, and she used to do that too, and she did not know whether she still could.  
She finished the reports. She put on her coat. She left.  
—————  
The room was on the third floor of a building the program had been using for four years. The board had approved the rat-accessible addition three years ago and called it a temporary adjustment, and the seam between the original room and the addition still showed in the ceiling panel — a line where the materials had never quite matched, where water found its way through in the heavy rains and came down in a slow, irregular drip into the catch pan someone had left under it. The drip had been there since her second visit. She had stopped noticing it except when the room was quiet enough.  
Eli was at the screen when she came in. She saw him before he could have seen her — he would have heard her in the corridor, she knew that, had known it since the second or third visit — but she had stopped pretending that the asymmetry didn’t exist. She came in; he was there.  
She had brought her own chair the first time. Now there was one kept here, a folding chair slightly too large for the space, angled toward the terminal the way she’d angled it.  
She sat forward, elbows on her knees. The terminal screen at floor level along the far wall: the width of her palm, mounted in a bracket, its surface clear in the way things were clear when someone maintained them carefully. She did not know who maintained it. She had never seen anyone.  
The screen activated: *you’re later than yesterday.*  
“I was finishing something,” she said.  
*was it finished?*  
“It will be.”  
The screen held a pause — the thinking kind, the gathering kind. She knew the shapes of his pauses the way she knew the shapes of his sentences, which was to say she had been reading both long enough that the shapes were simply his.  
*I want to tell you something I’ve been trying to find language for.* A pause. *it’s going to sound — I don’t know how it’s going to sound. I’ll try.*  
*I want to tell you something I’ve been trying to find language for.* A pause. *it’s going to sound — I don’t know how it’s going to sound. I’ll try.*  
“Tell me.”  
*it takes longer now. and shorter. both.*  
She waited. He was working through it.  
*a thought arrives and there is space around it I didn’t have before. like a room with the furniture moved out. which sounds frightening but it doesn’t feel frightening it feels—*  
A pause. She was leaning forward, her elbows on her knees, the screen close. She was leaning in the way she did when she was following — when the thought was going somewhere she could track.  
*the word I want isn’t in any language I taught. I’ve been looking for three days. the closest is something like — when a held note ends and the room holds the shape of it before the sound is completely gone. there’s a word in one of the old—*  
The pause was different.  
Not the gathering kind. Not the selecting kind. The screen was still on, the last phrase still there — *one of the old — *— and he was at the far end of the room, near the corner where the baseboard met the east wall, and his body had shifted. A fraction. Then more. A slow rotation she watched complete itself without understanding it. His head angled toward the wall. His sides stopped moving at the rate they had been moving.  
She waited.  
She knew, sitting very still, that she was watching something she had already named in a report once before — *expected sensory adjustment* — a word she had used at a desk three years ago about a man she knew professionally, and she had understood when she wrote it that the word was not quite what was happening, and she had not written anything else. She was sitting in the same quality of not-understanding now. The language she had was not the language for this.  
The screen went dark.  
She said his name.  
The room. The rain. The three-second drip from the ceiling panel, hollow and irregular, the way drips were.  
She did not stand. She did not move toward him. This was not a decision she made — her body making the same choice it had made the last time and the time before that, whatever instinct understood that moving would not reach what had gone. She was very still. She counted the drips. She kept her hands open in her lap.  
“Eli.”  
Quietly. The way you said something not because you expected it to do anything but because it was still what you had to say. He was there, in the corner, his attention aimed at something the room did not appear to contain. Not absent. The opposite: completely, entirely focused on a frequency she could not locate or name. His stillness was not the stillness of waiting. He was not waiting.  
The footsteps came then. Small and fast, a sound she had learned to separate from the building’s sounds — barely audible, already receding. Past the terminal. Along the baseboard. Into the slot at the far left corner, a gap in the trim darker than the wall around it.  
Gone.  
—————  
She sat in the empty room.  
She did not know how long. The rain. The three-second drip, irregular, hollow. The terminal screen, dark, its bracket catching no signal from anywhere near this room.  
At some point she stopped watching the corner slot and looked at the terminal instead. As if looking were a form of waiting that could accelerate waiting.  
It was not.  
She said his name again. Once, into the empty room. Not because she expected anything. She said it because the room was empty and he had been in it and she had been in the middle of a sentence she had been following, and she said his name because there was nothing else in her to say.  
She had her report open on her device. She looked at the last line she’d written, before the session started, and she did not add to it.  
The drip. The rain. The terminal screen, waiting.  
She thought about the sentence he’d been in. *The word I want isn’t in any language I taught.* He had been trying to give her something he didn’t have a word for — something about what thought felt like now, in the new intervals, in the space the furniture had been moved out of. He had been in the middle of finding the old language for the new experience. Something from the old languages, one of the ones he taught — he had been reaching for it when whatever it was had reached for him first, and the reach had been faster and stronger, and whatever it was, it needed following.  
She thought: he said *the old* and then he was gone.  
She thought: he said *the old* and then he was gone.  
She sat in the empty room and she did not fill it with anything.  
—————  
Eventually: small claws on concrete. Faint, then absent, then the creak of the slot. A pause. Then the terminal screen activated.  
*sorry. came back. something — I don’t have the word. a pressure. it needed following.*  
She read it three times.  
She remembered the whiskers description — the way each vibrissa read air displacement at a different frequency, the way the room was assembled before he turned toward it. She remembered *you have always had them. I simply did not have the equipment to read them.* She remembered all the things he had found language for — the scale of the room, the acoustic geometry of her body in the space, the barometric pressure read forty minutes before the rain stopped. He had found the right words for all of it. He always found the right words. The first weeks she had almost been able to follow him.  
*I don’t have the word.*  
She looked at the incomplete phrase still in the terminal’s buffer, the last thing there before the screen went dark: *one of the old—*  
She looked at the incomplete phrase still in the terminal’s buffer, the last thing there before the screen went dark: *one of the old—*  
He had been talking about language. About thought arriving differently now, about the space the furniture had left. He had been in the middle of a word that would have given it shape — a word from one of the old languages he taught, something preserved in a fragment, something worth handing on — and before he could place it something had taken him out of the room, and whatever he had taken with him, the word was among it.  
When he came back: *I don’t have the word.*  
When he came back: *I don’t have the word.*  
She sat with that for a while. Then she wrote in the visit log:  
*Subject demonstrated expected sensory response during visit 7. Communication resumption: approximately 34 minutes. No distress noted.*  
She had not been counting the time. She did not know where 34 had come from. She did not change it.  
She said goodnight to the terminal. The screen showed nothing — he had gone again, or settled, or whatever the word was for what happened when the session ended and she was no longer there to speak to.  
She let herself out.  
—————  
Outside, the rain. She walked home through it without adjusting her collar.  
The streets were wet and particular in the way they were always wet and particular, the drainage channels running high and fast, the orange tinge from the outer district runoff visible under the nearest functioning lamp. She walked through the light and past it. She was not thinking about the report. She was not thinking about Soren or the notebook or the address she had written in the wrong place. She was thinking about the word she had not finished hearing, and the thing that had finished it first.  
She needed a word for what she had just watched. She was an investigator; she needed to describe things; she reached for the language and found, waiting for her, a language she had not chosen.  
*The sensorium.* The rat’s sensorium, overwhelming the human cognition mid-sentence. *The sensorium is malleable —* she could hear him saying it, the briefing room, the strip lighting, the chairs slightly too low, Harlan’s settled certainty. *We are not bound to one set of inputs. The pattern adapts; the sensorium is merely the interface.*  
She noticed what she was doing.  
She had reached for a word and found Harlan’s word. The clinical term of the man who had, she was increasingly certain, buried research that might have given Eli another choice — that word was the one waiting for her when she tried to name what she had just watched. His framework. His vocabulary. His argument for why what had taken Eli out of the room mid-sentence was not loss but adaptation, calibration, the natural adjustment of the pattern to its new substrate. She was using his language to describe the moment that undid her.  
She could not find another word. She had been in the room when the word was almost found — he had been reaching for it, he had almost had it, something from one of the old languages — and now she did not have it either, and all she had in its place was the Director of the Institute’s clinical terminology.  
*The sensorium.* The word sat in her like something that didn’t fit the space it was in.  
*The sensorium.* The word sat in her like something that didn’t fit the space it was in.  
She walked home. Inside, she did not turn the light on. She sat by the window in the dark and looked at the street below, which was wet and empty and looked the way it always looked, for a long time.  
She did not sleep.
