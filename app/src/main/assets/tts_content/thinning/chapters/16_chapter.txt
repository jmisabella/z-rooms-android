_Chapter 16_

"You'd not think it," Ruth said, "but the funeral ones gave the least trouble of anything, once I had the way of them. Nobody moves. You set the light and it stays where you set it. There's no mother behind a cloth and no bride going stiff on you the second you raised the box, and no child to be held. They hold themselves." She had the funeral box open on the table and was down in it to about the middle, and she got the next one up off the gone-through stack between the side of her thumb and the side of her first finger, the lift she had now, and turned it to the window and tilted it to find the thing she was finding in it. "The trouble was the face. A face with nothing behind it doesn't sit the way a sleeping one does, whatever the family would have liked it to, and the light only answers the half of that. The half it answered I answered. The rest I left."

Claire wrote down _funeral — nobody moves_ and did not write the rest of it.

The box had been out when she came in. Ruth had got it down from wherever she kept it and squared it on the table in the south light before Claire was through the front room — the way she had had the wedding box out, and the baby box after it, each in its turn and waiting on the table when Claire arrived. There was an order Ruth was keeping to and had been for some while, the registers coming one after the other, and she did not say what the order was or why the funerals came where they came in it, and Claire did not ask.

She had opened the notebook to a clean page and ruled the date at the top and underlined it in the small clean way, and to get to the clean page she had turned past the babies, which were in the notebook still in the fast hand, un-carried, the hand in the frame and the three before one lived sitting where she had put them the session before last, gone the flat grey the shorthand went when it had sat; and past the page from the visit she had ruled a date on and not come for, the date on it and nothing under the date; and she had not counted the turning.

She had started to count the turnings to the window instead. Not the photographs. The turnings — the lift and the tilt and the hold while Ruth's eyes did the near of a picture and then the far, because the near and the far had gone their separate ways in them and a thing held off at arm's length and angled to the glass was the one place left where the two of them met on a picture. She had a number for it and it told her nothing, because she did not know how many were in the box or how many of those Ruth would want the window for, and it went up by the ones as the box went down, and she kept it while the box went down between them and the rest of her took what Ruth gave.

"That's a burying at the high ground," Ruth said, of the one she had. "Winter. The ground was too hard to put anyone in it, so they didn't — they stood the box above and waited for the thaw to do the digging, which is how it went the hard winters, you'd have three or four waiting up there for the ground to come back. So it's all standing. You didn't get sitting at a winter one. Nowhere to put a chair you'd get the use of." She held it where she could see it and did not pass it over yet. "Look at the mouths on them, the whole row. Every one's got the breath coming off it. It was the cold and the plate being slow together — you can't hold your breath the length of a slow plate in a cold like that, nobody can, so it comes out a smear off every face down the line." She set it on the gone-through stack, squared. "People asked me to take it off them, after. I left it on. It was the day they had."

Claire wrote _the breath off the mouths_ and did not underline it.

"Most of them didn't want the dead in the picture at all, mind." Ruth had the next one up, the side of the thumb, to the window. "They'd not have it in the house. They'd bring me one from before — a wedding, a sitting, ten years back or twenty — and I'd copy it and bring it up large, and that was the funeral picture, the old face and not the new one, and they hung it for the day and after. It suited me well enough. I'd sooner copy a good one of an afternoon than stand a morning in a cold room making a poor one of the other kind." She tilted the picture a degree further into the light. "This one's a copy. You'd not know unless I told you. There's a grain comes up in a copy if you look, but you'd have to know to look."

"There's less call for them now, even the copies. I'd have done one or two of a season up to a few years back, and then it went off, the way the work all went off." She had the next one up off the stack, the side of the thumb, and held it a moment unworked. "A family at the top end lost the old man, the year before last — a good age, in his bed — and they brought me the one to copy from, him at a daughter's wedding, and I had it done and standing ready, and they never came back up for it. They'd had him back by then. The way people have them back now." She set it down on the gone-through stack, squared, not looked at. "It's in the back still, the copy. I'd not throw it out. A good plate of a wedding, if nothing else."

Once she would have asked what the families did with them after — whether the copied ones got looked at again or only made and hung and let go grey on the wall, whether anyone ever asked her for a second copy years on, the after of the thing, which was the part she had used to go for. She did not ask it. She wrote _copied — brought up large_ and let the after be the after.

The light from the south window had gone along the table while they worked, off the boxes against the wall and onto the gone-through stack, and the stack had not got tall the way it got tall on the long afternoons, because Ruth was a while with each one now, the window and the near and the far of it, and because Claire was writing less under each than she had used to and Ruth did not fill the gap with more. She gave Claire a christening that had turned into a burying — the child christened on the one morning and gone by the next, and the same gathering near enough in their same good clothes for the both, so that she had two plates of one set of people a day apart, the one of them holding a living child and the other not, and she had given the family the christening and kept the burying, because they had not asked for it and she had not offered it. She said it flat, a thing that had finished happening a long time ago, and got the next one up off the box, and Claire wrote _christening to burying, a day_ and the name that came with it went past her into the room and she did not bring it back.

The turnings to the window were up past where she had them before and she let the number go and started it again.

Ruth looked down into the box by her chair and Claire watched her measure what was left in it against what she had left in herself for the day, and find the day the shorter of the two, sooner than she used to find it.

"That's me," Ruth said. She squared the gone-through ones and set them by the not-reached ones and laid her hands flat over both, the worsened right one and the left, in the way she laid them on a thing she had decided to leave where it was. "There's more in it than we'd reach. There always is. They'll keep." She did not put the box away. She moved it the few inches that put it square to the edge of the table, out, for the next time, and left the lid off it.

"Get your coat. The cold's already off the table and it'll be up off the floor before you're at the door, and I'll not be the one tells you you've gone blue twice in the one year." She did not get up. She had not got up to see Claire out in over a year. The look came up then off the squared box and off her own flat hands, the whole of the attention arriving and staying, the look of a woman who had spent her life looking at people through a glass and had not worn it out, only moved it back behind the eyes; and it stayed on Claire a beat past the looking, the way it had stayed on her across the gone-through stacks for over a year, and it took in what it took in and did not say what that was.

"I'm not going anywhere between now and whenever you're next by."

That was the whole of it. There was no shorthand for the line and there never had been, nor for the look that came with it, and Claire took down neither. She ruled the date for the next time at the top of a clean page and underlined it in the small clean way and clipped the pen to the notebook and put it in the inside pocket of her coat against her ribs, and her hand did the ruling and the line and the clip the way it did them, even and clean, and she did not count the clean pages it had ruled the date past to get there.

"Next time," Ruth said.

"Next time."

"All right."

Claire took her coat off the chair. The funeral box stayed where Ruth had set it, square to the edge of the table, the lid off, the breath-smeared row and the copied face and the christening-into-burying squared down inside it among the ones she had not reached, out in the last of the south light for the next time. Ruth was in the chair, smaller in it than a year ago, and did not get up. Claire went out through the bright room and the front room, and the latch took the door behind her.
