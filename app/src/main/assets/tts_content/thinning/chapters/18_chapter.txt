_Chapter 18_

Claire stood in the line for the meal with the folded sack in her hand, and the light lay full along the street as it lay through the long held middle of the day — even, unmoving, hours of it still to come before it would begin its going — which was not the part of the day she was in, mostly, anymore; but the meal came in by day and went out by day, and there was no getting it except in the held light, and so she was in the light for it.

There were four ahead of her and a few come up behind since she had joined it. The line stood along the front of a place that had been a workshop of some kind once and was the giving-out place now, the door open on the dim of it where the woman had the sacks and the table and the measure. Almost no one else was on the street. The few who came for their part came up quiet, took it, and went, and there was not the talk in a line there would once have been; there was the shuffle forward by the one, and the woman's voice saying the small things the work needed saying and no more.

She kept to where the line put its bit of shade down. She had got so the held light was the one she was least easy in of any of them. She was easiest now in the dark that came after it, and after that in the going light at the end of the day, and the held bright middle was the one that found her out, that lay on her the same as it lay on the stones and the shut fronts and gave her no part of itself to stand back into. She had come for the meal because the meal had to be come for, and she stood in the light because the line stood in it, and she waited.

She had come up the short way, along the bottom, and not up through the streets — because the way that went up went up and along to the white house, and she was not going up to the white house, and there was no use in walking the start of a thing she was not walking the rest of. So she had kept to the bottom, where the street ran level and did not turn up, and come to the line that way.

The line went forward by the one as the woman gave out the day's. Claire had the number of the measures going — the scoop down into the open sack and the level wrist and the fold and the next one up — and it went up by the ones and told her nothing, because she did not know what the day's meal came to, or how far the woman meant to make it go, or how many were still to come up the bottom street behind her for their part. She kept it while the line stood and moved and stood, while the rest of her stood in the light and waited her turn in it.

There was a man up near the front she knew the look of before she had the man. He had the look the others had — not the front of it, the way the boy in the greenhouse had worn the front of it, and not the whole of it, the way the man on the step had had the whole; somewhere along in it, the color gone out from under the skin, the day gone slack on him, the standing-in-a-line a thing his body did while the rest of him was off somewhere the line was not.

She had him placed after a moment. He had had the mill, down where the water came through at the bottom of the town, the grinding of the meal in the years there had been the grain for it and the head of water to turn the stone; and the mill had gone quiet some while back, the grain not coming and the water not enough, and the meal came in now from off, parceled out.

He was saying a thing, to the woman ahead of him in the line or to no one, in the flat half-voice of it. "—never quiet, you couldn't get it quiet in the season, you'd run it the day round and the night after and three of us could scarce keep it fed. The dust off the stone that thick you'd not see the man across from you. You'd not hear your own voice in it." His hand came up while he said it and turned a little in the air, the heft of a thing in it that was not in it — the lift of a full sack onto the shoulder, or the run of the meal off the stone through the fingers, the motion gone through him without the thing it was made for — and then his hand came down, and he did not finish it, or he finished it somewhere she did not get.

Claire saw the hand do the old work in the empty air and knew it for what it was, and the knowing went no further in her than the knowing. He looked round the line while the hand was still coming down, the slack look going over the few of them and over her, and it stopped on her — no more than a thing stopping where it has found its like, no warmth in it and no warning, the one of them taking the measure of the other and finding it the same — and then it went off her and along the line. The woman gave him his sack and he took it up in both arms and went out into the held light and down the bottom way, slow in it. Claire did not follow him with her eyes and did not give the thing a name, and the count of the measures was gone — she had lost it somewhere in his hand coming up and turning — and she did not take it up again.

The line brought her up to the table. The woman looked at her and said the small thing she said and no more, and Claire held the sack open and the measure went down into it, the one scoop and the level of the wrist that struck it off, and that was the day's; and she folded the mouth of the sack over and stood out of the line, out of the place in it she had kept.

Up at the top of the street, the other way, where it went up and along, the white house stood in the same light, the wide upstairs windows taking the best of it that there was in the town. Ruth would be in it. The box would be out. Ruth set the box out at the end of a session, square to the edge of the table with the lid off it and the ones she had not reached squared down inside, out for the next time; and she had set it out the last time, for a next time, and the next time was the walk up the street and along that Claire had come the bottom way to keep clear of the start of. So the box stood out up there in the south light for a day that had its date ruled clean at the top of a page in the notebook against her ribs and nothing put down under the date, and a page before that ruled the same and nothing under it, and the one before that — the dates going down the clean pages and the box standing out for them.

The things that might have pressed stood where they stood and did not press. The box up in the light and the clean dates running down the pages; and her sister the other way, down where the land dropped, the coffee out in the thermos the same as ever and the bare bed cleared and the cold let into it, and the thing her sister had said the once standing in the house there now the way the bench stood. She knew where each of them was, the box and the pages and the down-the-other-way, as she knew her own dark rooms, and she went among them and put a hand to none of it; and no want came up in her toward any of it, there in the light, with the sack folded over in her hand. There was nowhere in the bright street for it to go, and it did not come.

She folded the top of the sack down again and turned back the way she had come, along the bottom, where the street ran level and did not go up. The light lay full on it ahead of her and full on the white house at the top of the other way and on the box out in it, and she went down through the held middle of the day that was not her part of it now, with the meal got and the walk done, toward the rooms and the dark that came after the light, the part that fit her; and the box stood out up there behind her in the bright with the lid off it and nothing put down on the page for it.
