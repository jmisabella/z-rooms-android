_Chapter 17_

Claire laid the drawn canes by at the end of the staging, the long ones underneath and the short across the top the way Helen had said, and went back down the bare part of the bed for the next of them.

The house was coming empty the length of it. Helen had the spent plants out of the far half and most of the near, pulled by the root and the soil knocked off them and the dead of them heaped in the barrow at the bright end where the door stood open for the haul; and behind her the bed lay open and dark and broken fine, the holes the roots had left in their rows, the bare run of the soil and the canes drawn out of it and the light down the whole length where the standing crop had been.

The heaters were off and had been since before she came in, and the warm the day had lent the house had gone into her unmarked; what she kept was the thin run of cold along the cleared bed where the door stood open for the barrow, the one cold thing the held light had not put out. The cup was on the staging by her hand, gone cool, and she had not drunk it.

"They're done for the year," Helen said. "There's nothing more coming off them, and up they'd only sit and go soft and bring something in. So they come out, and the house gets the cold empty, and I'll have it clean to start in when the light's worth the starting." She drew a plant and the soil came off the roots of it. "The canes you keep. You'll not get a cane into the town now for the asking, nor the wire, nor the most of it. So they come up clean and they dry over the cold and they're next year's, on the end there where you're laying them."

The smell off the heap was the spent of them, the soft stems and the roots with the bed still on them, an earth smell with the end of a thing in it, and it came up the house on the draught from the door. There was no one come to help with the clearing. There had not been for a good while, and Helen had the house to clear the way she had the rest of it, and cleared it.

Claire counted the canes as she laid them by. The number of them went up by the ones as Helen drew them and brought them up the house and Claire laid them at the end, and it stood for nothing, because she did not know how many the house had in it or how many would come up whole. Each one came up into her hand and got its place, the long under and the short across, whole; and her sister was down the house at the next of them, and the count came up by the ones and Claire kept it.

"Bran's at the near work," Helen said, after a while. "He's not been down to the lower place in a month. They've not sent." She straightened. "I don't know that it's mended. I'd think there's no one left down there now to do the sending. It suits him well enough. He's home of an evening."

"That's good."

"It is."

She worked up the house as she cleared it, the standing crop going down ahead of her and the empty going long behind, and she came up the bed toward the near end where Claire was, a few feet nearer with each plant she took; and the voice came first, full and plain, and then the shape of her under it, an arm's length off and then nearer than that.

The last of the standing crop was down to a few, and Helen took them, and behind her the house was the whole empty length of it, the bare bed and the light along it and the canes laid by. And the thing that came then was not the count, and did not go up by the ones. It was the old wanting — that her sister should have the thing, should be given it, into all of that going empty, the having of it, the way the house was being given its clean cold for the next of the year. It went out the short way to her, the arm's length and a little more, the way the looking went; and it carried now the once — the time it had gone the whole way and not turned — and the turnings-back it had done since, and it got as far as the day full on her, and the shape of her bent to the last plant, and the voice coming up over it, and it stayed there. It did not go the last of the way to her. Claire did not send it across, and she did not bring it back, and it stood in the held light at the near end of the bare bed, and the last plant came up out of the soil.

"That's the house," Helen said. She knocked the bed off the roots of the last one and laid it on the heap, and the barrow was full. "Leave the rest of the canes. I'll have them down before the cold's up off the floor — the door's been open the length of the clearing and it'll be up off it soon. Get on home while the light's in the walk."

"In a minute," Claire said.

"All right."

She took the barrow up by the handles and ran it out the open end into the day, and the cold came in along the cleared bed where the door was, and the house stood empty the length of it behind her in the held light, the bare bed and the holes the roots had left and the canes laid by at the end for the next year. The count of them was gone, and Claire did not start it again.
