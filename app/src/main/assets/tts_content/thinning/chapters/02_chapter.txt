_Chapter 2_

It was still dark when Claire left the house. Not the deep dark of the middle of the night — the kind of dark that was getting ready to be morning, that had the cold of morning in it already. She walked the way she walked when she walked to Helen's, which was a different walk than the one to Ruth's. The route went down and around rather than up. The greenhouses were on a piece of flat ground at the edge of the town where the land started to drop toward the valley.

She had not been to Helen's since the week before last. Possibly the week before that. She had not been keeping a count of it the way she kept a count of her visits to Ruth's. The visits to Helen's did not have the shape of an appointment. They had the shape of Claire deciding, sometime in the long night or the early morning, that she would go.

She had decided in the night.

The greenhouses showed before the rest of Helen's place did, because of the lights. Helen kept lights on in them through the long nights at low levels for the temperature work, and Claire could see them from the bend in the road — three rectangles of pale interior light against the dark, and the dark itself going slowly blue at the top. Helen would be in one of them. Probably the middle one. The middle one held the things that needed the most attention this part of the year, and Helen would be checking those first.

Claire came up the path along the side of the property. The house sat back from the road; the greenhouses were beyond it, separated by a packed-dirt yard and a low fence that Helen's husband had put in years ago for reasons Claire did not remember and that no longer enclosed anything Claire could identify. The dog they used to have was gone. There was no new dog. Claire did not know when the dog had died and had not asked.

The light in the kitchen window was on. That meant Helen had been up at least half an hour. The light in the bedroom window was off. That meant Helen's husband was either still asleep or had already left for wherever he went on the days he worked. Claire could not have said which day this was.

She went around the house to the yard. She did not go to the door of the house. She would not have, this early.

The middle greenhouse door was the one that was used; the others had doors too but Helen went in and out of the middle one because the middle one had the work in it. Claire pushed it open and the warm came out at her, that specific greenhouse warm that smelled of wet earth and green and the slightly metal smell of the heaters. The door closed behind her on its weight.

Helen was at the far end with her back to Claire. She was bent over a tray of something. She did not turn around. She said, without turning, "Coffee on the bench."

"All right."

"It's been there a while. It's still warm. Don't drink it if you don't want it warm."

"I want it warm."

There was a thermos on the bench by the door. There was a cup beside the thermos, the same enameled cup Claire had used the last time she had been here and the time before that. Helen had set the cup out. Claire poured the coffee. The thermos was nearly full; Helen had made enough for two and had not drunk much of it yet, or had made enough for two and had drunk her share of it and refilled it from the kettle in the kitchen before coming out. Both were possible. Claire did not ask.

She drank the coffee standing by the bench. The greenhouse was warm in a way the air outside was not, and the warm was working on her face and her hands in the order they thawed — face first, then the back of the neck, then the hands. She had not realized how cold the walk had been. The dark had hidden it from her.

Helen was working with her hands inside the tray. Claire could not see what she was doing from here. The light in the greenhouse came from two sources — the low pale lights Helen had on through the night, and the gradually arriving light from outside through the long roof, which was still mostly dark and would be for another hour. The light on Helen's back was the indoor light. It was not flattering. Helen looked older in it than she did in daylight, which was something Claire had registered before and had not done anything with.

"You can come over here if you want," Helen said.

"I will in a minute. I'm thawing."

"All right."

Helen kept working. The thing she was doing involved moving something from one part of the tray to another with a small implement. Claire watched her hands without watching her face, because Helen was bent forward and the face was not available anyway. Helen's hands worked the way Helen's hands always worked. They did the thing they were doing and did not do anything else. There was no wasted motion in them and there had not been since Claire was a child.

Claire finished the coffee. She put the cup back on the bench. She walked down the long aisle of the greenhouse to where Helen was working.

"What are you doing."

"Thinning these. They came up too thick. They'll all die if I leave them and if I pull half of them out the other half will live. So I'm pulling half of them out."

"Which half."

"The weaker half. Mostly. Sometimes I'm wrong about which half is the weaker half and I pull out one that would have been all right. You can't tell from up here. You have to commit." Helen pulled one and dropped it into a small bucket at her hip. "You can help if you want. Or you can stand there."

"I'll stand there."

"All right."

Claire stood there. The tray was about three feet long and Helen was working from one end to the other in a slow pattern Claire could not have described in words but recognized as the pattern Helen always used. Helen reached, looked, took, dropped. Reached, looked, took, dropped. Sometimes she did not take; sometimes her hand hovered and then moved on. Claire watched the hand hovering and not taking.

"Bran's gone over to the lower place," Helen said. "He'll be back tonight or tomorrow. There's a thing he's fixing for them."

"All right."

"I told him you might be by."

"You didn't know I'd be by."

"I told him you might be."

Helen had said it without looking up. Claire watched her hands continue at the same rhythm. The hands did not stop when Helen spoke and they did not start when Helen finished. They had not registered the speaking at all.

"How's Ruth," Helen said.

"She's all right. Her hands are getting worse but she didn't say so. She got up slowly the other day. She told me about a teacher she used to photograph who cried during her last picture."

"Did she say what the teacher was crying about."

"No."

"Did you ask."

"No."

"All right." Helen's hand went into the tray and came out and the bucket received what it received. "She wouldn't have known. Ruth's good at telling you the story she has and not telling you the story she doesn't. She always has been."

"You don't know Ruth."

"I've met Ruth twice. I know what she's like from you."

Claire had forgotten that Helen had met Ruth. She did not remember when. She did not ask when, because asking would have been admitting she did not remember, and she did not want to admit that to Helen this morning for reasons she did not examine.

The light in the greenhouse was getting slightly brighter at the roof. Not enough to call dawn. Enough that Claire could begin to see Helen's face when Helen turned a little to reach the far side of the tray. Helen's face was the face Helen had — long, weathered around the eyes, the kind of face that registered weather and work and not much else from the outside. There was a smudge of soil along her jaw where she had pushed her hair back at some point. Helen would not know about it. Claire would not tell her.

"You walked over," Helen said.

"Yes."

"Was it cold."

"Yes."

"I'd have come and gotten you in the cart if you'd said."

"I know."

"You wouldn't have said."

"No."

The walk had been cold and she had wanted it cold and not the cart, and she did not say so, and Helen did not ask. Helen worked. The small implement made the small sound it made.

"You eaten," Helen said.

"No."

"There's bread in the kitchen. I made it yesterday. You can go get some if you want. Door's unlocked."

"In a minute."

"All right."

The bucket at Helen's hip was a third full. Claire counted, without meaning to, how many Helen had pulled in the time she had been standing here. Twenty, maybe twenty-five. The number meant nothing to her — she did not know how many were in the tray to begin with — but she had counted them anyway. She had been counting things lately. Steps on her walk home from Ruth's. The pages she had filled in the notebook in a session. Things that did not need counting. The counting was a thing her mind was doing while the rest of her did what she was doing, and she did not stop it because there was no reason to stop it and stopping it would have required attending to it, which would have been worse.

"You're somewhere else," Helen said.

"I'm here."

"All right." Helen did not look up. "I didn't say you weren't here."

There was a pause. Helen's hand hovered over the tray and did not take.

"I had a long walk," Claire said.

"I know."

"I'm here."

"I know that too."

Helen's hand went into the tray. The thing she had pulled was small and pale-rooted and Claire watched it fall into the bucket and lie on top of the others. There was nothing in Helen's voice that Claire could have identified as anything in particular. The voice was the voice Helen had. It said what it said and it did not say more than that.

Claire wanted to sit down. She looked around for somewhere to sit and there was a low stool near the second tray over and she went and got it and brought it back and sat down on it about four feet from where Helen was working. She set the empty enameled cup on the floor beside her. Helen registered the moving of the stool without looking — Claire could see the small registering in the way Helen's shoulders did not turn — and then went back to what she was doing.

They were quiet for a while. The greenhouse was quiet in the way the greenhouse was quiet, which was not silent — the heaters were on, and water was moving somewhere in a slow system Helen had set up that Claire did not understand the mechanics of, and there was the small sound of Helen's implement in the soil. None of these were quite sounds Claire would have noticed somewhere else. Here they were the texture of being in this room with this person.

Claire said, "How are the things on the south side."

"They're all right. They're slower than they should be. The light's been wrong this week."

"Wrong how."

"It came up later than it should have for what time of year it is. Two days running. I'm watching it."

"Has it done that before."

"It's done a lot of things before. This is one of them."

Helen finished the tray. She straightened. The straightening was slow in a way that was about the work, not about her body — she was being careful with her back the way someone who worked with her back was careful with it. She had been doing this kind of careful since Claire could remember.

"I'm going to start on the next one," Helen said. "You can stay or you can go get the bread."

"I'll get the bread."

"All right. Cut a piece off the loaf on the counter. There's butter in the dish. Don't bring it back out here, eat it in the kitchen. I don't want crumbs in the trays."

"All right."

Claire got up. She picked up the empty cup. She walked back down the aisle and pushed the door open into the cold. The cold came at her face the way the warm had come at her face when she came in, in reverse — face first, then the back of the neck, then the hands. The dark had gone slightly blue. The light was beginning to come up at the eastern edge of the sky over the valley.

She went into the kitchen through the back door, which was unlocked the way Helen had said it would be. The kitchen was warm and had the smell of bread in it that bread had the day after it was made. The loaf was on the counter under a cloth. Claire moved the cloth and cut a piece off and put butter on it from the dish and ate it standing by the counter. She did not sit down. The kitchen was Helen's kitchen and it was Helen's husband's kitchen and Claire did not sit down in it when she was alone in it.

There was a photograph on the windowsill above the sink. Claire had seen it before. It was a picture of Helen and Claire and their mother and father in front of a house Claire did not remember living in. Claire was very small in the picture. Helen was eight years older than Claire and looked eight years older than Claire. The mother and father were the age the mother and father had been. They were not old in the picture. They had not become old, and there was no later picture in which they had — no old man and old woman this one could have grown into. The picture was the age they had been when they had been alive and that was all the age they had.

Claire looked at it for a moment and then she finished the bread and put the cloth back over the loaf and washed the butter off her hand at the sink. The picture was on the windowsill where it had been for as long as Claire had been coming to Helen's. Helen kept it there. Helen looked at it every morning while she made the coffee. Claire had never asked her about it and Helen had never said anything about it and the picture was on the windowsill.

She went back out to the greenhouse.

Helen was at the second tray. She did not turn around. Claire stood inside the door for a moment in the warm and watched her sister's back at the work. The light in the roof was brighter now. Helen's hair, which she had been pushing back with her hand all morning, had soil along the side of it where she had touched it. Claire watched the back of her sister's head and her sister's shoulders working the slow pattern of the thinning and did not go closer.

"I'm going to head back," Claire said.

"All right."

"Thank you for the coffee."

"You're welcome."

"Tell Bran I said hello."

"I will."

"Next week, maybe."

"All right. Or whenever."

"Whenever."

Helen did not turn around. Claire stood at the door for another moment. She did not say anything else. She pushed the door open and went out into the cold and let the door close behind her on its weight.

The walk back was longer than the walk out had been, or felt longer, which was the same thing in this kind of light. The dark had gone all the way blue and the sky was getting ready to be the kind of blue it was during the long day, which was a blue that did not change for a long time once it arrived. Claire walked with her hands in the pockets of her coat. She had not brought the notebook. She had not, on her walks to Helen's, ever brought the notebook. The notebook was for Ruth.

She came up to the bend in the road where the greenhouses had been visible. They were not visible from this side; the road had risen enough that they were behind the shoulder of the hill. She stood for a moment and looked back the way she had come, and then she turned and went on.
