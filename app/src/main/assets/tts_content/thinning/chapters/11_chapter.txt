_Chapter 11_

The account was where it had been since the morning before, squared to the edge of the table in the long light, and Claire had the first pen in her hand and the notebook open beside it to the babies, and she had read down the page twice now in the good light without the first sentence of them arriving on the sheet.

The light was the held light, the flat bright hold of the long middle of the day, and it came across the table the way it came, not moving, the same square of it on the boards it had been on when she sat down, so there was no reading the hour off it the way there was off the going light or the coming one. It would hold like that a good while yet. There was light to work by and would be for hours.

The fair sheets were on the left where they went, the worked ones, thick to about the depth they had been, squared; and the empty ones were on the right, squared, waiting in the way paper waited, which was not at all. The stack on the left had not got taller. She knew its depth the way she knew the room. She had carried the school pictures over in the end — the teacher who had cried and the girl with the hands, done on a sheet one evening and set on the stack — and after the school pictures came the weddings, which were in the notebook still and not on the stack, the bride on the steps and the building that was not there and Aldous whose farm was out past where Helen was and whose wife's name was gone from Ruth; and after the weddings came the babies, which she had had at Ruth's two days before, or three, and which were the open page under her hand now, the hand in the frame and the three before one lived and the faster plates the year after. Turning to the babies she had turned past the weddings, the shorthand of them gone the flat grey the ink went when it had sat, and she had not stopped on them, and she did not stop on them now.

There was a tap at the basin in the other room that did not close all the way and had not for some days, and it let a drop go at its own rate, and she had the count of them going without having meant to begin it, the way she had had the steps once and the panes of the window once, a number that went up by ones and stood for nothing, because she did not know the rate of it or how long it had run before she marked it, and she kept it while the sheet stayed white under the pen.

The first sentence was the one that took it. After the first sentence the rest came along behind, because the rest was only the carrying-over, the notebook into the longer hand, and that was work but it was not the kind that stopped you. A date, a place, the two of them at the table, the box on the floor by Ruth's chair where she could reach it without rising. She had the sentence. She had had it when she sat down. She set the pen on the sheet at the place the first word went, and the place stayed white under the pen, and the pen did not move, and the drop went into the basin in the other room, and she let the count of it go up.

She got up. Not to go anywhere; there was nowhere in the rooms to go. The stove was cold and she did not light it; the cold had not come down into the rooms yet and would not while the light held. There was the cup from the day before on the table with the ring of tea dried in the bottom of it that she had not moved, and another behind it, older, and she moved neither. In the other room there was bread under a cloth, two days made, the same loaf as the day before and the day before that, what had come in, and she cut a piece off it and ate it standing by the basin where the tap let its drop go, and did not taste much of it, and that was the eating done. The water stood in the basin under the drip with a ring of its own at the old line and a dark above it where the new was coming, and she watched the next drop go and lost the count somewhere in it and did not start it again.

She thought about the weather, which was the long bright hold and would be for hours and then would go the way it went. She thought, without holding it, of a thing she had meant to ask Ruth and had not — whether the families had brought their own cloth for the draping or whether Ruth had kept one — the kind of thing she used to put after the thing Ruth gave her, and it came up and she looked at it and it went nowhere, because she was not at Ruth's and would not be for some days, and she let it be the question it was. The things that might have pressed did not press. They were in the rooms with her the way the cups were, and she went around them the way she went around the cups, without the going-around being a thing she did on purpose, and the afternoon held its light, and the bread sat under the cloth, and the account sat squared on the table with the two sessions owed to it and the stack not grown.

Helen came up once, lightly, the way she came up now — not a thought about her, only her sister's plain voice for a moment, _I heard you the once,_ said over the seedlings with the work not stopping for it, and the no that was in it that was not a cruel no — and Claire's attention went toward it and toward the morning it had been said in, and turned. She had not walked down to the greenhouses in a while. She would, or she would not.

The light had not moved off the table; it held where it had been. She sat back down in the chair with the worn place and took up the pen, and this time the first sentence went down — the date, and the two of them at the worktable, and the babies Ruth had given her — went down in the ordinary hand without trouble, and after it she put down the hand in the frame, the grown hand that came in from the left edge under the child's back to hold it still through the length of the holding, the hand that belonged to no one in the picture and was in it anyway. She had got that far, two sentences and the start of a third, when the pen stopped on the sheet and did not go on. It was not that she could not. The light was good and the notebook was open and the next of it was there to be put down, the three of the one family before one of them lived past the picture, and she did not put it down. She set the pen across the sheet. The two sentences and the half of one sat on the fair sheet by themselves, the first thing on it, and the rest of the babies stayed in the notebook in the small fast hand, and the weddings stayed behind them, greyer, and the stack on the left had a sheet on it now with three lines on it that was not a worked sheet and was not an empty one.

The drip had stopped, or she had stopped hearing it; she could not have said which. The light was beginning, now, to come off the held angle and thin at the top of itself, the way it did when the long middle was over, and the room would go down into the blue after it the way the rooms went, and the cold would come up out of the corners. She did not get up to the lamp. There was the part-sheet on the stack and the notebook open to the babies with the most of them still in it and the weddings behind that, and the bread under the cloth, and the two cups, and the account squared to the edge of the table with the light coming off it now, and she sat with the pen across the sheet and the afternoon going, and the work was where it was, and she was not troubled by it, and she did not move to do the rest.
