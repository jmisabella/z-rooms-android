_Chapter 13_

The tray had been filling slower than the tray before it, because the row was getting toward the end of what it had to give, and Helen went down it taking the ripe ones and leaving the green, and Claire sat at the near end on the stool with the enameled cup gone cool between her hands and watched the tray fill. The ripe ones were not all the way red. The red came up the shoulders of them unevenly and stopped, so that most had a green still on them near the stem where the light through the leaves had not got to them the way it got to the rest, and Helen took them anyway, at the colour they had, because the colour they had was as far as it was going to come this far on in the year; and a few of them had split, the skin gone in a seam down the side where the wet had come up into them faster than the skin would stretch, and those went apart from the sound ones along the one end of the tray; and the small late ones that had set after the light turned and would not now come to anything stayed on the plant, because there was nothing to do with them and the plant could have them.

The light was up at the angle it held at. It came down the length of the house and lay along the row and on the fruit in the tray and made the red of them redder where it touched and left the green ends green, and it was the held light, the long bright middle of it, the part of the day that did not move and would not for hours yet before it began the going. The heaters had been off since before Claire came in. The warm they had made was in the house still, the way it held in the held part of the day, and it had gone into Claire the way it went, the face first and then the back of the neck and then the hands, and she had stopped marking it, and what she marked instead was the cold under it, the night's cold that had not gone out of the place, standing in the corners and along the floor where the light did not reach, against the side of her foot where it had come along the boards to her.

"There's not the weight in them there was," Helen said. She did not stop the work. "You pick a tray and it's the half of the tray it'd have been. It's the light not coming up when it should — they don't fill if it's not on them the hours it ought to be. They set all right. They just don't fill." She went on down the row. "I'll get what's on them and that's what there is."

"It looks like a lot," Claire said, of the tray, which it did not, and which she had said because the tray was the thing between them to say something about.

"It's not. But I'll take it."

She had been counting the sound ones as they went into the tray. She had started somewhere in the middle of it, the way she started now, without deciding to, and she had a number for the tray and it told her nothing, because she did not know how many more the row would give or how many of those would be sound and how many would have the split in them, and the number went up by ones as the fruit came off the plants and into the tray. She kept it the way she kept all of them now, without its being a thing she did, while the rest of her sat in the warm.

Helen went down the row the way she went down a row. She did not stop over any of it. She took the ripe and left the green and set the split apart and the tray filled behind her and the row emptied ahead of her, and there was no waste in it, there had not been since Claire was a child, and she did not give the picking her eyes — they went on down the row to where the work was going rather than down to where it was, the way they did.

"Bran's back," Helen said. "He got in the night before last. The thing's holding, the one at the lower place — holding for now, he says, which is the thing he said the last time, so I'll likely see it again, but it's holding, and he's home, and he's at the near work today." She said it the way she said the light. "First he's been home of a day in three weeks. I'd near forgotten the shape of him about the place."

"That's good."

"It is."

There was a second tray out on the bench, empty, squared next to the soil-heap, and Helen nodded at it without turning. "There was a woman said she'd come and help me get the picking in. Down from the houses past the fence. She said it last week and she meant it when she said it. I had that one out for her." She laid a sound fruit in beside the others. "She's not come. I'll not have looked for her by tomorrow. You stop looking after the once or twice, and then it's only you again and the work the same size it was."

Then she came to one she did not put in the tray. She took it off and set it up on the bench behind her, apart, whole, and a while later another, and a third, picking them out of the row as she came to them, the soundest and the reddest and the most filled, the best of a thin picking, and stood them in a short row on the bench by themselves.

"Those are for the seed," she said, before Claire asked, or because Claire was looking. "You keep your own now. There's none coming into the town to buy, and what there is you'd not trust to come up. So you let the best of them go by — you don't eat them — and you take the seed when they've gone soft and you dry it and keep it, and that's next year, on the bench there." She stood another by the others. "It's a thing I didn't used to have to think about."

It came up without her having gone looking for it — not a thing she decided, only the wanting beginning to be there, under the warm, while she watched her sister stand the best of the fruit up on the bench in its row and go back to the picking. The want for Helen to have the thing. The want for it to be given to her, for her sister not to be standing in a half-bare row in the held light putting the best of a poor picking up out of her own reach against a next year that got harder every one of them. It went out toward Helen across the warm the way her attention went out toward a thing, out of the watching, and it went to the doorway it went to.

And at the doorway it would have turned, except that it carried now the thing it had not carried before, which was that once, the once, at another table, in another warm, where it had always turned it had not turned — it had gone on — and it brought that to the doorway with it and stood there a moment with the going-on it had done the once. And then it turned. It turned back. Her sister was an arm's length off and then nearer than that, working down the row toward her, and the want came back without anything in it and she let it, and she did not bring it back, and she did not say any of it, and the picking went on.

"You've been a while coming down," Helen said. She said it the way she said the rest of it, not turning, the work not stopping for it, no point on it. "I'd started to think you'd gone off the walk."

"I meant to come."

"I know you meant to." She laid a ripe one in the tray. "The coffee's been out. It's out now, in the thermos, the same as ever, and it'll be out the next time and the one after, whenever the next time is." She went on down the row. "I'd rather you came. You know I'd rather you came. I'm only saying you've been a while."

Claire said she knew. She did not say she would come the next time, or the one after — which was the thing she had used to say in answer to that, and had stopped saying it without having decided to. She did not say the rest of it either, because there was no rest of it she had the shape of, only the knowing that the door her sister was holding open was not the door she had wanted opened, and that this one had been open the whole time and would go on being open, and that she had still been a while in the coming to it.

"You can lay them in, if you want," Helen said. "The second tray. A single layer, not piled — the split ones to the one end so I can see them. They bruise if you stack them, and a bruised one won't keep the week." She set a sound one on the near edge of the bench to start her off. "Or you can sit. It goes the same either way."

Claire stood off the stool and her legs had the stiff of the long sitting in them, and she took up the second tray, the one that had been out for the woman who had not come, and set it where she could lay into it, and began to lay the fruit in the way Helen had said, a single layer, the sound ones in their rows and the split to the one end. The fruit was warm where the light had been on it and cool where it had not, and the weight of a sound one was a small good weight in the hand, and she laid them in one and the next the way her sister went down the row, and then her hand did one and stopped, the fruit in it, held over the tray, not laid in, and she did not lay it in for a moment, and then she did, and she did not notice that the hand had stopped, and Helen, up the row, did not notice it either, or if she noticed did not say.

They worked the two rows, Helen taking the fruit off and Claire laying it in behind her, and the trays filled between them, the sound and the split, and the best of it stood apart on the bench in its row for the seed, and the held light lay along the house at the angle it held at and did not move and would not for hours, and under it in the corners the night's cold stood and waited the way it waited to come up when the light went off the glass at the far end of the day, and Claire laid the fruit in a single layer the way she had been told. She did not count it. The picking went on.
