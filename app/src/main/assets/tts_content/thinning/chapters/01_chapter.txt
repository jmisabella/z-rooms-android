_Chapter 1_

The shop on the corner of the second street up had been closed for about a year. Claire registered the boards over its window the way she registered any of it now — without slowing, without quite looking — and went on. She had been walking since the light came up. The light came down the street at the angle it had been holding for most of the morning, and the morning had been going on for a long time.

She was carrying her notebook in the inside pocket of her coat, against her ribs, which was where she kept it on the walk to Ruth's. The pen was clipped to the top of the notebook. She had two pens because she had once arrived at Ruth's with a pen that had given out and had not made that mistake again.

The street up to Ruth's neighborhood ran along what had been a row of small houses and was now mostly a row of small houses with some empty ones between them. The empty ones were not boarded. There had not been anyone to board them. The doors stayed closed, the windows held what was left of their curtains, and the gardens in front of them did what gardens do when no one is in them — which in this light was less than people who didn't live here might think. Things had a hard time getting started. The wild plants that came in were the same few that always came in.

A dog she didn't know crossed the street ahead of her, going somewhere it was going. It did not look at her. She did not break stride for it.

The interview today was going to be about the school. Ruth had taken school pictures for forty years, and Claire wanted to walk through them — the rooms she'd photographed in, the way the children had been arranged, what the teachers had asked her to do and what she had quietly done differently. Claire had her questions in the front of the notebook. She had been thinking about them on and off for two days. _Did the parents ask for anything specifically. Did you notice the rooms changing. Did anyone ever cry._ She wasn't going to use all of them. Ruth would talk and the questions would go where Ruth went.

She had not been to Ruth's the week before. She had meant to. Something — she could not quite get hold of what — had happened to the Tuesday, and then to the Wednesday, and then it had been Thursday and too late. She had written Ruth a short letter the next morning saying she was sorry and would come the following week. Ruth had written back the same day with two lines. _Thursday is fine. I'm not going anywhere._

The handwriting on the second line had been very slightly less steady than the first. Claire had noticed and had not looked at the letter again.

She turned onto Ruth's street.

This was the street where the emptying was the most visible to her, because she walked it every week, and because what was missing from it was specific. The bakery had been on the right, halfway down. It had closed about eight months ago. The baker had not died; he had stopped, was what Ruth had said, and Ruth had said it with a particular emphasis on _stopped_ that Claire had understood without asking her to explain. The bakery's front window was clean. Someone was keeping it clean. Claire had never asked who.

Ruth's house was the third from the end on the left. White, two stories, the upstairs windows shaped wider than the downstairs ones, which was unusual and which Ruth had chosen the house for. The front garden had a low stone wall around it that Ruth's second husband had built. There was no name on the gate. There never had been.

Claire knocked twice and let herself in.

"Coat on the chair," Ruth called from the back room.

Claire put her coat on the chair. She kept the notebook with her.

The back room was the worktable room. The windows ran nearly the height of the wall on the south side, and the light came in across the table and across the boxes Ruth kept stacked against the opposite wall and made the floor between them very bright. Ruth was at the table with two photographs in front of her and her hands folded over them. She did not look up as Claire came in. She finished what she was looking at and then she did.

"You found one," Claire said.

"I found several." Ruth tapped the topmost photograph. "School pictures. We can start with these or we can start with whatever you wanted to start with."

"These are fine. I saw a dog on the way over. I didn't know it."

"That'll be the one from two streets over. He's been around again. I'm glad he's still going."

"He didn't look at me."

"No, he wouldn't have. Sit down."

Claire sat in the chair on the long side of the table. It was the chair she always sat in. The cushion had a worn place where she had been sitting in it for over a year. She took out the notebook and the first pen. She set them on the table to the right of her hands.

Ruth was looking at her.

"What," Claire said.

"Nothing." Ruth looked back down at the photograph. "I was going to ask if you'd had breakfast, and then I remembered you don't eat breakfast."

"I had something."

"All right."

Ruth slid the first photograph across the table. It was a class photograph from a year Claire could not have guessed at — three rows of children, the smaller ones in front sitting cross-legged, the older ones standing in the back. The teacher was off to one side. The children were not smiling in the way Claire had seen children smile in photographs of her own childhood. They were just looking at the camera. One girl in the second row had her hands clasped in front of her in a way that suggested someone had told her to.

"That's Mrs. Halsey's class," Ruth said. "I'm not going to remember the year. It was after my first husband and before the bridge went out, so somewhere in there."

Claire wrote down _Mrs. Halsey, before the bridge._

"The girl with the hands," Ruth said.

"Yes."

"That's the one I always remember. I don't know why. I took thousands of these and most of them are gone from me. That one I see when I close my eyes."

Claire wrote that down.

"Did you know her after?" she said.

"A little. She married a man from a valley over and I took those pictures too. I have those somewhere. She died in her forties. I'd have to look up of what."

"You don't need to look it up."

"No."

Ruth turned the photograph back toward herself and looked at it again. Claire watched her looking. Ruth's hands on the table were not still in the way that hands were when someone was at rest; they were still in the way of someone who had decided to keep them still. The knuckles of the right hand were larger than they had been a year ago. Claire had noticed this several visits ago and had not written it down anywhere.

"What did you want to ask me," Ruth said.

"About the rooms. The classrooms. Whether you noticed them changing."

"Over the years, you mean."

"Yes."

Ruth thought about it. She was good at thinking about it; she took the time and did not fill the time with talking while she took it. Claire had learned, over the year, to let the time be. Early on she had tried to help — rephrasing, prompting — and Ruth had put up with it for two sessions and then said, _Claire, when I'm thinking, I'm thinking. You'll know when I'm done._

"The light," Ruth said. "The light in those rooms changed. Not because of the rooms. Because of the light. I had to do different things by the end than I did at the beginning. The windows didn't move. The pictures from the last ten years have a different quality and you can tell. The children look further away."

Claire wrote down _children look further away._

"What else."

"The children got quieter. I don't know if that's about the rooms or about the children. They sat more still. I used to have to tell them to stop moving. By the end I didn't. They were already not moving when I came in." Ruth paused. "That's probably not about the rooms."

"It's about something."

"It's about something."

They worked through three more photographs. Ruth told Claire about a teacher who had refused to be in any of the pictures and had stood off to the side every year for thirty years and only relented once, in her last year of teaching, and had stood at the end of the back row with her hands folded and her face composed and had cried, very quietly, the whole time the picture was being taken. Ruth had developed the film and had seen it. She had not said anything to the teacher. She had given her the picture along with the others and the teacher had not said anything either.

"Do you still have that one," Claire said.

"Somewhere. I don't think I want to find it today."

"All right."

Claire wrote down what Ruth had said about the teacher, more or less, in her own shorthand. She underlined _had not said anything either._ She was aware, distantly, that this was the kind of thing she would have, in earlier sessions, asked a follow-up about — what the teacher had been crying about, what Ruth had thought it was — and that she was not asking. 

Ruth was watching her again.

"I'm going to make tea," Ruth said.

"You don't have to."

"I know I don't have to. I want some."

Ruth got up. The getting up was slower than it had been a year ago. Claire watched her hands on the edge of the table as she pushed herself to standing and then watched her cross the room and go into the kitchen.

Claire looked down at the notebook. The page in front of her had her handwriting on it from the last half hour and the handwriting was even and legible and the notes were what she would want to find when she came back to them. The work was still there. She could still do the work. She turned the page and wrote the date at the top of the next one in the small clean way she wrote dates and underlined it.

The light from the south window had moved a little along the floor while they had been working. Not much. Enough that the bright square it made had a different edge than it had when she came in.

Ruth came back with two cups and set one in front of Claire and sat down with the other. She did not start the next photograph. They drank the tea. Ruth asked her about the walk over, and Claire said it had been fine, and Ruth said good.

They did one more photograph. Then Ruth said, "I think that's me for today," and Claire said, "All right," and closed the notebook and clipped the pen to it and put it back in the inside pocket of her coat.

At the door Ruth said, "Next week."

"Next week."

"Thursday, or whichever day."

"Thursday."

"All right."

Ruth did not say _try to come._ She had never said _try to come._ Claire was aware of the not-saying and was aware that Ruth was aware of it, and they both did not say it, and Claire stepped through the door and Ruth closed it behind her.

The light on the street was still where it had been. Claire stood for a moment on Ruth's step with her hand on the gate and then she opened the gate and went through it and started back the way she had come.

She went down past the houses she had come up past, the empty ones and the lived-in ones between them, and on the lived-in ones the curtains were drawn across the lower windows against the light. They had been drawn coming up too, or she had not looked. They were drawn in the middle of the day, the day with hours of itself still in it, and behind them the people who had drawn them would be asleep in the bright.

At one of them the curtain had not been pulled the whole way. A hand's width of the room was left open to the street, and in it there was a bed, and someone on the bed asleep, turned half from the window so that the face was not in it. While she was even with the gap the sleeper's arm went out across the bed — out across the empty side of it, the side with no one lying in it — and it went out all at once and straight, the way a thing goes that is going somewhere and not feeling its way there, and the hand opened on the bare sheet where no one was and stayed open, and nothing came into it. The face stayed down in its sleep. Nothing in the room moved but the one arm gone out across the empty side and the one hand open on the sheet with nothing in it.

She was even with it and then she was past it. She had not slowed. She went on down the street the way she went on past any of it, and a little below it the curtains were drawn the way they were drawn, the lower windows of the lived-in houses dark in the bright of the day, and she went down between them toward the bottom of the street and did not look back at the one that was open.
