_Chapter 9_

Helen had the flat of seedlings up on the bench and was potting them on, and Claire sat at the near end of the bench on the stool with the enameled cup gone cool between her hands and watched her do it. They were the ones that had come through the thinning, the half Helen had let stand back in the cold weeks, and they were big enough now to go each into a pot of its own, and Helen took them one and then the next — knocked the flat against the heel of her hand to start the roots, lifted a plant clear, set it down into the pot she had standing ready and pressed it in and set the pot off into the row of the done ones, and went on. She did not look at her hands. There was no waste in it. There had not been since Claire was a child.

The pots were the old ones, set up in their rows along the bench waiting to be filled. There were the clay ones, gone pale and chalky at the rims and darker where the wet had been in them, and there were the others, the ones that were not clay and had been mended, one with a wrap of wire round a split in its side and one with the bottom of another pot pushed up inside it to take the place of a bottom it had lost, and they had been washed and stacked and brought out and stood up in their rows, because you did not throw a pot away now, you kept the pot, a pot was a thing that did not come into the town anymore and so the ones there were got kept and mended and kept again. The soil for them was in a heap at the far end of the bench, dark and broken fine, damp through from the wetting Helen had given it, and the smell of it was the smell that came up off turned ground, low and cold and mineral, under the green smell of the plants and the warm.

The light was coming up the glass. It was the morning light, still climbing, not yet up to the angle it would hold at through the long middle of the day, and it came in low along the length of the house and lay across the bench and across the rows of pots and threw the small shadow of each plant a long way down the bench from where it stood. The heaters had gone off a while ago — Claire had heard the last of them stop while she was still at the door with the coffee — and the warm they had made was holding in the house yet, the way it held in the held part of the day, and under it, in the corners and along the floor where the light had not got, the night's cold was still standing, not gone out of the place yet, waiting the way it waited to come back up when the light went off the glass at the end of it.

"It came up right this morning," Helen said. She did not stop the work. "The light. Came up when it should, more or less. First morning in a week it has." She knocked the flat, lifted a plant clear. "I'll not make anything of it. It's done that before and gone wrong again the day after. But it came up right this once."

"That's good."

"It's nothing. It's one morning." She set the pot off into the row. "I'll take it, though."

Claire drank the last of the cool coffee so as to have done something with the cup, and set it down on the floor by the leg of the stool where she had set it the last time and the time before. The warm had gone all the way into her a while back, the way it did, face and the back of the neck and then the hands, and she had stopped noticing it, and what she noticed now was the cold under the warm where the morning had not reached, against the side of her foot where it had come along the floor to her. She had been counting the pots in the done row. She had started without deciding to, somewhere in the first few, and she had a number for the done row and it told her nothing, because she did not know how many were in the flat to start with or how many of those would take and how many would not, and the number went up by ones as the pots came off Helen's hands. She kept it. It cost nothing to keep, and she kept it while the rest of her sat in the warm with the done row growing along the bench toward her.

"Is Bran back," Claire said.

"Not yet. Tonight, he said, or tomorrow. It's the same thing he's been saying." Helen pressed a plant in and went on. "It's the same thing it always is. They get it running and it runs a week and then it's the same again, and they send for him, because there's no one nearer who'll come. He'll be back when he's back." She said it the way she said the light. "I don't plan the week round it."

"No."

"There's bread, if you want it. From the day before yesterday. It's still all right under the cloth."

"In a minute."

"All right."

She worked. The water moved somewhere off behind them in the slow system Helen had built and that Claire did not understand the working of — the run of it, the where-from and the where-to of it, the way it took the wet round the house without anyone carrying it — and it had run all through the long night, Helen would have set it to, and it ran now under the small sounds of the bench, the knock of the flat, the press of the soil, the chalky scrape of a pot set down against the next. The done row grew along the bench toward Claire. Helen came down it toward her as she filled it, the way she came down a row, a few inches nearer with each plant she potted, and Claire watched her come and watched her hands do the thing they did, the lift and the set and the press, and her sister was an arm's length off and then nearer than that, working, the morning light along the side of her face and on the backs of her hands, and the count of the pots was up past where Claire had it before and she let it go and started it again.

It came up the way the soft thing came up now, without her finding where it started — not a decision to say it, only the saying beginning to be there. She had watched Bran not come back, and watched the far house go undone, and watched her sister fill a row of mended pots in the early light on her own with no one to give it to but the pots, and the thing came up out of the watching, unasked.

"You don't have to keep the far house going at all," Claire said. "You could let it go. For good, I mean. Not just this week." The cup was at her foot and her hands were empty in her lap and she heard her own voice be level, which was a thing she had not decided either. "There's where none of it would be this hard."

Helen did not stop the work. She set the pot she had in her hands off into the row, and she took up the next plant, and she said, in the plain voice, not turning, "I heard you the once, Claire."

That was all of it. She did not set the work down for it the way she had set the twine down. She did not turn and look down the row. The hands kept on, the lift and the set and the press, even, the rhythm not broken anywhere Claire could find, and the saying of it had cost her, from the outside, no more than the saying of the light had cost her, and it went out into the warm and did not sit there, because the work took it up, the next plant out of the flat and into the next pot, and that was the whole of it.

"I know," Claire said.

"I'd still rather you came." She knocked the flat. "That hasn't changed. The coffee's out, isn't it." It was not quite a question. "It'll be out the next time and the one after. I told you that and it's so."

"I know it's so." It was little, against what Helen had put out — the rather-you-came, the coffee out, the next time and the one after — and Claire kept the rest of what she had, the saying she would come that she had given the day before and had again to give, and held it back.

"All right, then."

The thing came up out of the work the way it did, the open hand turned up and offered, the breath that had aimed itself across the dark and known where she was, the arm that had not searched — it came up unasked and it went out toward what her sister had said the day before, toward _I'm not coming_ said in the plain voice over the tomatoes with the green stain on her thumb, and it reached the doorway of it and turned. Her hands were empty in her lap and the cold the morning had not got to was against the side of her foot, and the saying her sister had done the day before was a thing in the house now the way the bench was, the way the rows of pots were, a thing that was simply there and that neither of them was going to pick up, and the hand that had gone toward it came back without anything in it and she let it.

"You can firm these in, if you want," Helen said. "The ones I've done. Go down the row and press the soil down round each where it's settled, an inch off the stem, not against it. They sink as the wet goes through and you have to bring them back up firm or the root sits loose." She set another pot off into the row. "Or you can sit. I don't need it done. It's better done."

Claire stood off the stool, and her legs had the stiff of the long sitting in them, and she went to the head of the done row where the first pots were, the clay ones gone pale at the rims and the one mended with the wire, and she put two fingers into the soil of the first an inch off the stem the way Helen had said, and pressed, and felt the soil go down a little under her fingers and the wet come up cool round them, and she brought her fingers round the stem and firmed it the rest of the way, and went to the next. The plant stood up out of the pot a little straighter for it, or seemed to. She did not know if it seemed to or did. She did the next, and the next, down the row, an inch off the stem and round, and her sister went on filling the row ahead of her hands faster than Claire firmed behind, so that the done row got longer between them and not shorter, and the green smell was on Claire's hands now where she had been in the soil, sharp, not like anything a person would eat, and somewhere behind them the slow system carried the wet round the house, and the light came up the glass toward the angle it would hold at, and the two of them worked down the bench toward each other in the warm Helen made against the cold that was standing yet in the corners and would come up again when the light went off the glass at the far end of the day.
