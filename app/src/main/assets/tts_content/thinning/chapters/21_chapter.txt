_Chapter 21_

Claire carried the young up to the head of the bed a flat at a time and set them down by Helen's hand, and Helen took them out of the cells and put them into the cleared ground, one and the next, the long bare run of the bed that had stood open and broken fine since the year's end taking them in down its length. The light was the day's. It came in along the roof the way it had not come the whole cold length of the year, the real light back at last and lengthening, lying down the house and out past the glass on the white fronts of the houses below and along the valley where the year had turned up at last out of the bottom of itself and come back; and the low pale made light that Helen had run close over the bench through the dead of it was off and gone back along the house to the nights where it lived, because the day was worth the work now and gave the young what they wanted, and there was no more making the light by day. The heaters were off. The warm the day was lending the house had got into Claire when she came in, the face and the neck and then the hands, and she had let it go unmarked as she let it go, so that what was left her to hold was the enameled cup gone cool between her hands and the cold that stood off yet at the dark end of the house where the day had not got.

"They took better than I'd have said, off the seed there was," Helen said. She set one in and drew the soil to it. "More than half the flats in the end, and the most of those came on once the day started giving them something. I had them onto the glass a fortnight back to harden, once it wouldn't kill them to be off the made light, and they stood it. So they go in now. The bed's been ready for them since the autumn. It was only ever wanting the year to come up far enough to put them in it." She went on down the bed. "You sow in the dead of it on the say-so the light comes back, and you clear the bed in the cold for a thing you've not got yet, and then the light comes back, the little it comes, and there's the thing grown to put in it. And there it goes in."

The seed was the seed she had saved off the poor year — Claire had watched her stand the soundest of it up on the bench in the autumn, and watched her sow it down the cells in the dead bottom of the year, and seen the green come up in the flats at the turn — and now it was the young going into the ground, grown on out of the seed-leaf into the thing itself, and Helen set them down the bed and drew the ground to them and went on. Claire had the number of them going, the young into the bed one and the next as Helen's hand put them down, up by the ones along the cleared run, and it arrived nowhere, because she did not know how many the flats would come to or how many would take in the ground or how many the year would let her keep, and she kept it the way she kept them. And the hands that set them did not come. What came down the bed to Claire was the young going into the ground, and the soil drawn to them, and the bare length filling behind Helen's hand; and the bent shape of her at it; and the voice, when she said a thing about the taking, plain and sure of it.

It came up unasked, under the count, and took the count's place. She did not feel it decide to. She was sitting in the day's warm with the cold cup in her hands and the young going into the ground and her sister bent to the work down the bed, and the want came up under all of it, the old want, for Helen to have the thing, for it to be given to her, into all of this going right — the light come back and the year started and the young in the ground — the having of it, the way the bed was being given the thing it had waited the cold for. And the count went out of her and she did not reach for it. The want came up and it carried the once it carried, the time it had gone the whole way and not turned, and it went out toward Helen down the bed the way her attention went out toward a thing, and this time, where it would have turned, at the place it always turned, it did not turn. It went on toward her.

She watched her own hand come up off the cup, from somewhere back of it, the way she had watched the others. It came up and crossed the warm day air toward where her sister knelt at the head of the bed with her hands in the young, and it did not search; it went level, knowing the distance and certain of it, the way the breath had gone across her sister's dark room knowing where she was; and it opened. The fingers loosened off it and the palm turned up and it lay open in the air over the bed, offered, and it asked nothing a waking hand could ask and it asked anyway, the only way it had, by being open and waiting, for the one thing that would answer it, which was for Helen to turn one of her own hands over out of the soil and lay it up into Claire's.

And on the far side of the open hand, where the reach had never been let come before, the thing it was holding open toward her came near. Not the picture on the windowsill above the sink, the four of them in front of the house Claire did not remember, the two who were the age they had been and no older — but the older the picture had never grown into, the old man and the old woman there had been no later picture to become, near now, grown the whole way into the years the picture had not had, the way they would be by now; near on the far side of the reach the way a room is near on the far side of a door you have your hand on and have not yet opened, the warm of it already come under the door; near, and not yet, and the not-yet of them the thinnest thing in the world, a turn of a hand wide. They were that near. The reach was open the whole way to them and the whole way to her sister both, the one through the other, and there was only the small turning left, her sister's hand over out of the soil, and they would be here.

Helen had stopped. Her hands had stopped in the young at the head of the bed and she was looking at the open hand. She did not startle and she did not get up. She did not do the thing she did, the thing her hands had always done — take the offered hand in both of hers without waste and without hurry and carry it the short way back to where it had come from and lay it down and let it go. She did not head it off. She looked at the open hand a beat past the looking, the look she gave a thing she had decided long ago what she would do about, and then her own hand came up out of the soil.

It came up and it came toward Claire's. Claire watched it come. It came the short way across the bed, the hand that had been in the ground, and the warm of it reached Claire's open one before the hand did — the warm Helen made and the warm the day had laid along the back of it, coming whole into the cold of Claire's open palm ahead of the hand the way it had come into the back of her hand at the watering — and the hand came up under the warm, near, nearer than it had ever come to the answering, and it began to turn. Claire felt it begin to turn over. The palm of it started up toward Claire's own, the one turn that would lay her sister's hand into hers, the one thing that would answer the open hand; and her palm and her sister's were a turn of a hand off; and the hand came that near to the turning, and Claire could not get it. She could not get the hand. She had it there, the warm of it whole in her cold palm and the turn of it begun, near enough to feel the begin of the turn, and what it was — the soil up the back of it, the lines of the work in it, the hand that had set her own to the angle and pulled the weaker half out of the trays and lifted the sleeping arm back so many nights it no longer woke her — did not come; and on the far side of the begun turn the two were nearer than the hand was, near, the warm of them under the door, more here than the hand that was a turn of itself from her own.

Then it stopped. The turn stopped begun, the palm part of the way up and not the rest of it, and it held there, stopped, the longest Claire had ever known her sister hold at anything; and the warm stood in Claire's palm and the turn stood half made and the two stood a turn of a hand off and did not come the rest of the way in, because the hand did not come the rest of the way over. It held at the edge of it. And then the fingers drew in. The palm that had started up turned down, and the hand closed, the begun turn taken back into the closing of it, and it went down out of the air and back to the soil at the head of the bed, to the young, where the work was; and it did not lay itself into Claire's, and it did not take Claire's and carry it away, and it did neither of the things it knew, and it was the doing of neither that had cost. Claire saw it cost. She saw the cost of it in the hand going down and in the breath that went down with it, though what the cost was in her sister she could not get either.

"No," Helen said.

She had her hands in the young again, both of them, and they were not at the rhythm yet. They lay flat on the soil to either side of the plant she had been setting, holding there a moment the way the other had held in the air, before they took the plant up and went on with it.

"No," she said again. "Not that." The voice was level and there was nothing breaking in it; it was the voice she said the temperatures in, the voice that had said _I'm not coming_ down the row of the tomatoes, only there was more of the work in it now and more of the holding. "You brought it nearer than you've brought it. And the answer's the answer it was. I'm not going where you'd take me. Not for that, not for what's in it, not for any of it." Her hands took the plant up and set it into the ground and drew the soil to it and went on to the next. "I'd rather you came. You know what I've always said to you. I'll have the coffee out the same as ever, and there's no part of this where I want you not to come down here." She set another in. "But you're not to bring it that near to me again. I can say no to it down the row. I can say no to it the once a visit you say it, the years you've said it. I felt it just now and I'll tell you the truth of it, which I've not told you before — I don't know that I can say no to it that near a second time."

And that was the whole of it, the only part she let into the air, and she did not look up from the bed to see what it did, and her hands went on down it, the young into the ground one and the next, the soil drawn to each.

Claire's hand was still open over the bed. It had stayed open through the whole of it, offered, the warm gone out of the palm of it now and the cold come back into it, and there was nothing in front of it any more — no hand coming, no turn beginning — and the two had gone back the turn of the hand to wherever the turn of the hand kept them, near and not here. It had gone the whole way and there was nowhere left in it to turn, because it had not turned at the place it turned, and so it did not turn. It came back. Claire brought it back across the day air to the cup and closed it round the cold of the cup and held it there, and the want went down where it had come up, not gone but down, under everything, and the open of the hand was over.

Helen set the young into the bed down its bare length, the light of the lengthening day full on the whole of it, the year going into the ground in the light come back; and her sister was a bent shape down the bed and a voice over it, plain, when she said the next thing about the taking; and Claire kept the cold of the cup in her two hands and did not take up the count, and the young went into the ground behind Helen's hand toward the dark end where the cold still stood off, and that was the work, and it went on.
