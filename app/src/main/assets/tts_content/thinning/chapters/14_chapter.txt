_Chapter 14_

The cold had come up a long while ago and was all the way into the rooms now, standing in them the way it stood along the floor and in the corners when the light went off a thing, except that there was no light to have gone off anything; the light had gone hours back, the way it went, off the wall at the far end of the day and out of the room and down into the blue and then past the blue, and the rooms had come down after it into the dark they came down into, and she had not lit the lamp, and she was up in the dark with the cold, easy in it the way she had got easy this last while in the dark that came after the light, easier in it than she was in the light, so that being up at this hour was not a thing she came to anymore. It was where she was.

She had been up since she could not have said when. The since-when was not a thing the night kept. The day kept its hours — the light at the angle it held and then the going of it, you could set a thing by it, you could read the hour off the wall — and the night did not; the night was the one long piece of itself from the time the cold first came up to the time it began to go again, and she was somewhere in the middle of that piece, in her socks, with her hand along the edge of the table where she knew it was in the dark.

The wood gave as it took the cold. It had been giving since the light went, a small report somewhere in the place each time the cold came another degree into it — the frame of the window, or the boards under the floor, or the one loose stair — not at a rate she could have kept the way she had kept the drop at the basin a while back, because the cold did not come at a rate; it came as it came, and the wood gave when it gave, and the count of the givings was going in her before she had marked the start of it, a number that went up by the ones in the dark and stood for nothing, because she did not know how cold it would get before the night turned or how many the wood had left in it to give. She kept it the way she kept the others, while the rest of her stood in the cold rooms in the dark and was awake.

There was bread in the other room under the cloth, two days made, the same loaf or the loaf after it, what had come in, and she went to it in the dark because she knew where it was, and cut a piece off it, and ate it standing by the basin the way she ate now, when she ate, which was at the hours she was up for; and the hours she was up for were these. She did not taste much of it. The tap had stopped letting its drop go, or she had stopped hearing it, she could not have said which, and the basin held the water it held with the cold coming into that too, and she ate the bread over it and did not turn on the light to do it.

She moved through the rooms in the dark and they were the rooms she knew — the table, the chair with the worn place, the other chair with the coat over the back of it that was not the coat she wore out — and she went among them without putting a hand out, the way you go through a room you have gone through enough times that the dark is no different to the feet than the light. The cold was up around all of it. The night held the rooms the way the day held them, only it held them at this hour, when there was nothing being asked of them and no one in them but her; and she was in them, awake, the most of her there was at any hour now in the dark of them.

The window over the table was black. It was the window the table had been put under for the light, years back, and the light came across it in the day at the angle it came, the held bright middle of it, hours of it, good light to work by; and in the night it was black, it gave the room back to itself and little of the street. Only, out past it, across the way, beyond the empty house and the one beside it, there were windows in the dark that still had a light in them, three of them, or four, low and far apart on the face of the night, where there were people up still, or a lamp left burning, in rooms that had not gone dark yet. She watched one of them. While she watched it, it went out — not dimmed and then gone, just gone, between the one looking and the next — and there were the others, fewer by the one; and she had the going-out of them for a while, one and then a long while and then another somewhere off to the side of where she was looking, and then she let it be, and did not keep it, the way the wood-count went and was not started again. The day would come up out beyond the black of it after a while. It would come up the way it came, over the valley and along the fronts of the houses, and hold at the angle it held; and the streets would have in them at that hour what they had, the few that were out, going where they went; and the walk to Ruth's was a thing that went in that light — up the street and along to the white house with the wide upstairs windows where the light was best in the town — it was a daytime thing, the walk, it had always been a daytime thing, you went in the morning and came back in the going light and the steps of it were daytime steps. Ruth had said she would be there. She had said it the dry flat way she said it, no point on it — _I'm not going anywhere between now and whenever you're next by_ — and it was a true thing she had said; she was there, in the white house, with the boxes still better than half full and the look she had, and the whenever-you're-next-by standing open in the daylight the way the coffee stood out in the thermos at Helen's, out in the part of the day the walks went in.

The things that might have pressed did not press. They were in the rooms with her the way the cold was and the cups were, the two of them on the table with the rings dried in them that she had not moved; and the account squared on the table beside them, with the sessions owed to it and not carried over — the weddings the longest waiting of them, the bride on the steps of the building that had come down, got down in the fast hand a long while back and sitting; and the babies behind the weddings, the one fair sheet with the three lines started on it and nothing under the three, that she had not gone on with — got down, all of it, in the notebook, and not carried the hand's-breadth across into the long hand it was owed to. She went around them the way she went around the table in the dark, knowing where they were and not putting her hand on them. There was the walk that went up and along in the daylight, and the white house at the end of it, and the whenever-you're-next-by standing open in the light. There was the other walk, the down-and-around one, to the houses at the edge where the land dropped, and the coffee out the same as ever — _it'll be out the next time and the one after_ — said over the picking with the work not stopping for it; and the held light along the row at the end of that walk, and her sister coming down it doing all of them; and the thing her sister had said the once, in the plain voice, that was a thing in the house now the way the bench was. She went around all of it. She did not put her hand on any of it. The going-around was not a thing she did on purpose. It was the shape the rooms had for her in the dark, the way she moved in them, and she moved in them, and the cold came another degree into the wood somewhere, and the wood gave, and she had lost the count of it and did not start it again.

Once, in the going-around, the want came up. It came on its own, not toward a thing she had turned to but up from under her before she had the start of it — the want for her sister to have the thing, for it to be given to her, the old want, with nothing in it she could have put a shape to in the dark — and it went out toward Helen the way her attention went out toward a thing, toward the row in the held light and her sister coming down it, and it went to the doorway it went to. There it would have turned, except that it carried now the once — the time it had not turned, the time it had gone on — and it stood a moment with that. Then it turned. There was no one in the rooms for it to go to, and the rooms were dark, and the want came back without anything in it, and she let it, and she did not bring it back, and the cold came up.

The cold would come up a while yet and then begin to go, the way it went, when the night turned toward the far end of itself; and the dark would thin at the window the way the light thinned off the wall at the other end of the day; and the day would come up over the valley and along the fronts of the houses and hold at the angle it held, hours of it, the held bright middle, good light to walk in and to work in and to go up the street to the white house in. And she would be in the rooms. She was in them now, in the dark, with the bread eaten and the lamp unlit and the account squared on the table where it had been since before the night, and the out-coat with the notebook in its pocket hung by the door against nothing, and the window black over the table; and the wood gave somewhere as the cold took it, and she did not count it, and she was awake in it, and she did not move to the lamp, and the day was a long way off yet, coming, the part of it she would not be in.
