_Chapter 5_

The photograph in Claire's hand was of a wedding party on the steps of a building that was not there anymore. Ruth had said so when she handed it over — not the bride first, the building. It had stood at the end of the market street and it had come down over three or four winters the way things came down now, not pulled down, just let go of, a wall at a time, until what was left was cleared because it had become a danger to the street. Claire had written the name of the building in the notebook. Then Ruth had gone on to the people on the steps, which was the part she wanted Claire to have.

"The one in the middle's the bride," Ruth said. "You wouldn't know it from how she's standing. The tall one beside her is the mother, and the mother ran the whole day, and you can see the bride letting her. I got more of the mothers than the brides, over the years. The brides held still. The mothers were always doing something, and a thing that moves takes the eye."

"Did they like that. When you brought them the pictures."

"The mothers liked it. The brides didn't always know what they were looking at." Ruth turned the photograph a little where it lay in Claire's hand, to see it the right way up herself, and looked at it the way she looked at the ones she was giving away, which was not the way she looked at the ones she was only sorting. "Both of those are gone. The bride went first, which you don't expect. The mother lasted into her nineties, same as me. I did the mother's funeral picture too, in the end. I won't find it today but I have it."

Claire wrote _bride d. before mother_ in the shorthand and did not write anything after it.

There was a stack of the wedding ones on the table between them and a second stack Ruth had already been through and set face down to the side, and the boxes the stacks came out of were on the floor by Ruth's chair where she could reach them without getting up. Ruth had got them out before Claire arrived. That was new. Ruth had always before done the getting-out as part of the session, slowly, so that the choosing of what to look at was itself a thing Claire watched her do; today they had been out and waiting, squared on the table in the light, and Ruth had started almost before Claire had the notebook open, as if there were an order she had decided on in advance and meant to keep to. Claire took this in.

Ruth handed her the next one. She had a way of getting a photograph up off the stack now, with the side of her thumb, that she had not had to have a year ago. It was a smaller picture, a couple alone, the two of them standing in front of a painted backdrop of a garden that was meant to look like somewhere they were not. "That's a studio one. I did those in the back of the first place I worked, before I had my own. The backdrop was the man's I worked for. I hated it. Everybody wanted it." She held the photograph close to her face to find the thing she was looking for in it, and then held it further away and tilted it to the window, because her eyes did the near and the far differently now and neither of them did detail. "Her name's gone from me. His was Aldous, which is the only reason I can give you his, because you don't forget an Aldous. His people had the farm out past where Helen is now. They'd both be gone."

Claire wrote _Aldous, farm past Helen's._ She had started, without deciding to, to count the gone ones. It was not a count she needed. Ruth said _gone_ or _both gone_ or _she went young_ about nearly every picture, because the pictures were old and the people in them had been grown when they were taken, so the count only went the one way and arrived nowhere, and Claire kept it anyway and lost it and started it again, the way she kept the steps on the walk home and the sheets in the stack on her own table. Keeping it had never been the point. Keeping it was the thing her attention did while the rest of her sat in the bright room and took what Ruth gave her.

"How long did you do the weddings," Claire said.

"Thirty years, near enough. Longer than I should have." Ruth set the studio couple down and took up the next without looking at Claire, working her own pace. "I'd have given them up sooner, but people kept asking, and I had the paper and the chemicals to do them a while after other people ran out, so I was the one left who could. That's most of why anybody was the last of anything, by the end. Not that you were better at it. You just still had the materials."

"What made you stop."

"The light, partly." She said it the way she said the names of the dead, as a fact that had finished happening. "You did a wedding at a certain hour of the day for the light you wanted on the faces, and there came a stretch of years where that hour stopped giving it. The sun would sit up there and not come down to the angle you needed when you needed it. The day got longer on me, too, across the years of doing it. When I started you got your hour and it would move on and be gone, and you'd lost it if you weren't ready. By the end the same light would sit up there half a day in the wrong place and you couldn't make use of the most of it. More day, and less of it any good to me. You can fix some of that with where you stand people. You can't fix all of it. The pictures from the last while have it wrong on the faces and I knew it and did them anyway, because they wanted them." She turned the picture to the window. "And the orders fell off. The wanting went out of them. That was the bigger part. The light I could have worked around longer than the wanting."

Claire wrote _light wrong on faces, last years_ and _orders fell off._ She did not write the thing about the wanting. It was not the kind of thing the account was made of, and writing it would have meant deciding what it was.

"You could put a year on it, if you needed one," Ruth said. "The last proper wedding I did. My daughter was older by then than that bride on the steps, so it's at least that far back. You can count from her if you want it exact. I'd have to sit and think, and I don't want to think that hard today."

"It's all right. I don't need it exact."

"No."

Claire's pen was on the page where she had set it down. The page had the building on it, and the year of the light going wrong, and the orders falling off. The daughter was not on the page. Claire turned to a clean part of it and waited for the next picture.

They went on through the box. Ruth gave her a double wedding — two brothers married to two sisters the same morning, to save on the doing of it — and told her which couple had lasted and which had not, and how the not-lasting had gone, mildly, without unkindness, the way you can talk about people once everyone concerned is past minding. She gave her a picture of a wedding the groom did not come to, where the bride had wanted the picture taken anyway, alone, in the dress, and had paid for it the same as if he'd come; Ruth had taken it and never been told the rest, and had her own idea of the rest, and did not say what it was. Claire wrote them down. Her shorthand on the page was getting shorter as the afternoon went, fewer words holding more, which she would have called efficiency if she had said anything to herself about it at all.

At one point Ruth paused, a picture half out of the box, and looked at Claire across the table, the way she did, with the whole of her attention, which was a thing Claire had got used to and still felt when it came. Ruth had spent her life looking at people and the looking had not worn out with the eyes. It had moved back behind them somewhere and got stronger. She looked at Claire, and Claire looked back, and waited for whatever Ruth had seen to become a thing Ruth said.

Ruth did not say it. She got the picture the rest of the way out and turned it over and it was a wedding.

"None of this matters, you understand," Ruth said, not unkindly, handing it across. "Who married who. Whether they lasted. There's no one left it matters to but me. The ones it would have mattered to are gone, or they've stopped, the one or the other, and a picture's no use to either kind. I'm nearly done myself. I'm giving it to you because you came and asked, and because it's better given to somebody than left to be burned, but don't let me tell you it matters. It's a hundred years of people standing still a minute in their good clothes. That's the whole of what it ever was."

"It matters to me."

"I know it does. That's why you get it."

The light from the south window had gone along the table while they worked, off the boxes now and onto the face-down stack, which had grown all afternoon and was thick. The box by Ruth's chair was still better than half full. Ruth looked down into it and Claire watched her measure what was left in it against what she had left in herself for the day, and find the day the shorter of the two.

"That's me," Ruth said. "I had more out than we'd get through. I always do." She did not put the rest back. She squared the ones they had not reached, the corners to the corner of the table, and set the gone-through stack beside them, and laid her hands flat over both, the way she laid her hands on a thing she had decided to leave where it was. "They'll keep. They've kept this long."

"I'll come back through them."

"No hurry. They're not going anywhere and neither am I." The same two notes she had put in the letter, the joke and the thing the joke was holding up, and Claire heard both of them and took down neither. "Get your coat before the cold comes up. It comes up in here as soon as the light's off the table."

Claire ruled the date at the top of a clean page, for the next time, and underlined it in the small clean way she ruled dates, and closed the notebook on it. Ruth had not got up. She sat in the last of the south light with the box still half beside her and her hands still on the squared photographs, the gone-through and the not-yet both under them, not sorting and not putting away, only keeping her hands where they were, in the room she had spent her life making bright. The better part of it was still in the box.
