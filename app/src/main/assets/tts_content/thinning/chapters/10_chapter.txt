_Chapter 10_

The photograph in Claire's hand was of a baby, and the baby had been held still by someone who was not meant to be in the picture and was in it anyway. Claire saw the hand before she saw the child — it came in from the left edge of the frame, a grown hand, a woman's, splayed under the baby's back to keep it upright through whatever length of holding-still the picture had needed, and the rest of the woman was off the edge or behind the cloth they draped for it, so that there was the baby in its long gown looking out at nothing the way the babies did and there was the hand that belonged to no one in the frame holding the baby up into the looking.

"You'd crop that out, if you were doing it right," Ruth said. She did not lean over to see which one Claire had; she knew the box and she knew the order she had them in. "Or you'd put the mother behind a cloth and have her hold the child through it, and you'd hope the cloth read as a chair. I never minded it the way some did. A baby won't hold still for the time you needed, back when the plates were slow, so somebody held it, and the somebody was the mother, near enough always, and I'd as soon you saw the hand as not. It's the truest thing in half of them." She had the next one already up off the stack, between the side of her thumb and the side of her first finger, the way she got them up now. "People wanted the child alone. As if the child got there alone."

Claire wrote down _the hand in the frame_ and did not write the rest of it. The notebook had come out of her coat late, this visit. She had sat down in the chair on the long side of the table, the one with the worn place in the cushion where she had been sitting for over a year, and Ruth had started before the notebook was out, the way Ruth started now, with the box already on the floor by her chair where she could reach it without rising, and Claire had let two or three of the first ones go by held only in the looking before she had thought to get the notebook out and the pen unclipped, and when she did get it out the page she opened to was not a clean one. It was the wedding session. It was the last thing she had written, the bride on the steps and the building that was not there and the studio couple whose name was gone from Ruth, and it was further back than it should have been, the ink of it gone the flat grey ink went when it had sat, and she had turned past it to a clean page and ruled the date at the top and underlined it in the small clean way she ruled dates, and she had not let herself count how many clean pages there had been to turn past.

She had not written, either. The letter. There had been a stretch of days she could not have given the count of where she had meant to and had not, the way she had meant to the year before and had written it the next morning, _I'm sorry, I'll come the following week,_ and Ruth had written back the same day, two lines, _Thursday is fine, I'm not going anywhere._ This time there had been no Thursday she could point to that she had missed, only the gap getting to be the size it was without a single day in it she had decided against, and she had not written the letter, because the letter was for a missed Thursday and this was not that, this was no shape she had a letter for.

Ruth had not said anything about the gap. She had said _coat on the chair_ from the back room the way she always said it, and Claire had put her coat on the chair and kept the notebook, and Ruth had been at the table with the box already down by her, and that had been the whole of the arriving.

"How long did you do the babies," Claire said.

"Longer than anything. You did the babies first and you did them last." Ruth turned the photograph she was holding to the window, because the near and the far had gone different in her eyes and neither of them did the small things anymore, and she found in it the thing she was finding. "A wedding's the one day. A school picture's the one year. A baby they bring you because they've just got it and they don't yet know whether they'll get to keep it, and they want the having of it put down somewhere it can't be taken back out. I did three of one family before one of them lived past the picture. The fourth's still going, last I knew. You'd not know which of the four it was to look. They all look the same at that age, which is the thing nobody wants told them and the thing I knew better than anyone, having done so many. A baby looks like a baby. It's the hand holding it that's anybody."

She said it without weight on it, the way she said the names of the dead and the year the light went wrong, and Claire wrote down _three before one lived_ and did not underline it.

The light from the south window had come around onto the table and was on the boxes Ruth had not got to and on the back of Ruth's right hand where it lay on the squared stack, and Claire saw, in the light, that the knuckles were larger than they had been, larger than the last time she had been in the chair, that the hand had gone on doing the thing hands did when she was not there to mark it doing it. Ruth got the next one up. She did it with the side of the thumb, not the pad, and Claire understood without being told that the pad did not do it well anymore and that the side of the thumb was the thing she had found that still did.

"That one I can date for you, near enough." Ruth held it to the window and tilted it. "The faster plates had just come in — I'd waited two years to get them up the valley and they came the spring before this — so it's the year after Joan was born, give or take. I did it standing, see, no holding, because I had the speed by then and the child was old enough to sit. You can put a year on it from that if you want the year." She set it down on the going-through stack, squared. "I don't need it. But you can have it."

Claire wrote down _faster plates, the year after,_ and the name went past her into the room and did not come back, because Ruth did not bring it back. Ruth was already getting the next one up off the box with the side of her thumb, and the name had been a peg she had hung a year on, the way she hung years on the things she could still be sure of, and she had hung it and let it go and gone on, and the only mark of it Claire could find was that Ruth had not looked up while she said it, which was nothing, because Ruth did not look up for most of what she said. Claire's attention went toward it — toward the name, and the year hung on the name, and the wedding session sitting back in the notebook un-carried-over, and the clean pages she had turned past to get to this one — it went toward the whole of it the way it went, and at the edge of it it turned. She wrote _the year after_ and let the rest be the rest.

"You're writing less," Ruth said.

She had not looked up to say it either. She was turning a photograph to the window. There was nothing in it Claire could have called anything; it was the flat exact voice Ruth used about her own work, the voice that corrected a date and in the next breath said the date did not matter.

"There's less to write," Claire said. "You're giving me the ones you've told me before."

"I'm not, though." Ruth set the photograph down. "I've not told you a one of these. I'd know if I had." She said it mildly, with no point on it, a woman keeping her own record straight because the keeping of it was the work and she did the work right or not at all, and then she let it go the way she let the name go, and got the next one up. "But it's all right. You'll write what you'll write. I had more out than we'd get through anyway. I always do."

Claire looked at the page in front of her. There was the hand in the frame, and three before one lived, and faster plates the year after, and that was the session, that was the whole of what an afternoon of it had got onto the page, and the shorthand of it was smaller than it had used to be and there was a great deal of white under it. She had used to fill a page. She had used to ask the thing after the thing — what the family had been like, whether the fourth one knew about the three, what Ruth had thought, holding the camera, knowing — and she was not asking it, and she was aware of the not-asking the way she had been aware of it before, distantly, the way you are aware of a sound that has stopped.

"Did the family know," Claire said. "The fourth one. That there'd been the three."

"I'd think so. People knew their own." Ruth turned the one she had to the window. "Why."

"No reason."

"All right." Ruth set it down. She did not make Claire have a reason. She had never made Claire have a reason for any of it; she gave Claire the thing and let Claire do with it what Claire did, which had been, for over a year, to write it down in the small fast hand and carry it home and make it into the longer account, the fair sheets, the sentences someone could read who had not been in the room — and Ruth did not ask after the account, had never asked whether the sessions became the thing they were for, because that was Claire's end of it and Ruth kept to her own end, which was the box, and the boxes, and the better part of them still in the box.

The light had gone along the table while they worked, off the boxes and onto the going-through stack, which had not grown the way it grew on the long afternoons. Ruth looked down into the box by her chair and Claire watched her measure what was left in it against what she had left in herself for the day, the way she measured it, and find the day the shorter of the two, sooner than she used to find it.

"That's me," Ruth said. "I'd more out than we'd reach." She squared the gone-through ones and set them by the not-reached ones and laid her hands flat over both, the worsened right one and the left, in the way she laid them on a thing she had decided to leave where it was. "They'll keep. They've kept this long." She looked at Claire then, the whole of the attention coming up off the photographs and arriving and staying, the looking she did that Claire had got used to and still felt when it came, the looking of a woman who had spent her life looking at people and had not worn it out with the eyes, had only moved it back behind them. She looked at Claire a beat past the looking. She did not say what she had seen. She got it the rest of the way into herself and kept it, the way she kept the dates she did not need, and what she said was the dry thing, the thing she said instead.

"Get your coat before the cold comes up. It comes up in here the minute the light's off the table, and I'm too old to be the one tells you you've gone blue." A small motion of the worsened hand, toward the chair where the coat was. "I'm not going anywhere between now and whenever you're next by. I want that understood. Whatever else I do, I'll not do that."

"I know," Claire said.

"I know you know it. I'm saying it." She did not say _try to come._ She had never said it and she did not say it now; she said the other thing, the thing that was not a reproach and sat in the room not being one, and Claire heard it and took down neither it nor the looking that had come before it, because there was no shorthand for either and never had been.

Claire ruled the date at the top of a clean page for the next time and underlined it in the small clean way and closed the notebook on it and clipped the pen and put it back in the inside pocket of her coat against her ribs. Ruth had not got up. She sat in the last of the south light with the box still better than half full beside her and her hands flat on the squared photographs, the gone-through and the not-reached both under them, the truest things in half of them being the hands that had held the children up out of the frame, and the better part of it still in the box, and the cold beginning to come up off the floor in the corners where the light had already gone.
