_Chapter 6_

Helen worked the brush down the row of tomatoes, blossom by blossom, and Claire watched her do it from the stool at the end of the row, where she had been sitting long enough that the warm had gone all the way into her and she had stopped noticing it. The brush was a small one, the kind that had been a paintbrush once, worn down at the ferrule and worn at the bristles to the shape Helen's holding of it had made over years, and Helen took each yellow flower in the fingers of her left hand and touched the brush to the inside of it and let it go and went to the next. She did not look at her hands while she did it. She had stopped having to. She looked along the plant ahead of where her hands were, the way a person reading aloud looks ahead of the word they are saying.

The light in the greenhouse this part of the day was the long light, the dry bright hold of it through the roof, and it came down across the tops of the plants and put the row in a green that was nearly the green of a real spring and was not it. Outside, beyond the glass, the same light lay on the yard at the same angle it had been lying at for hours and would lie at for hours more. In here it had work to do. Out there it only held.

"There's no one to do this but me now," Helen said. She said it without complaint, the way she said the temperatures and the names of what was coming up slow. "There used to be the bees in the third house, the box of them, and you'd open the vents between the houses on the warm days and let them through. The box failed over the winter before last and I didn't get another. You can't get another the way you used to. So it's the brush." She moved to the next plant. "It's slower than the bees. But it's surer, if you do it. The bees would do the near ones and miss the high ones and you'd get fruit at the bottom and nothing up top. The brush gets all of them. If you do all of them."

"How long does it take you. The whole house."

"Longer than the day used to be, and the day is longer now, so." She did not finish it. It was not a thing that needed finishing and she knew Claire would not need it finished. "I do this house, and I sleep, and I do the far house after, if I get to the far house — the work and the sleep in the middle of it and the work again, and that's the one day. Lately I don't always get to the far house. The far house can hold a day. It can't hold two."

Claire had been counting the blossoms Helen touched. She had started without deciding to, somewhere in the second plant, and she had a number for the plant and a number for the one before it and she was carrying them together the way she carried the steps on the walk home and the pulled seedlings the last time she had been here, in the part of her that did that now, and it told her nothing because she did not know how many blossoms a plant had to begin with or how many it would keep. The count went up and arrived nowhere. She kept it anyway. Keeping it was the thing her attention did while the rest of her sat in the warm and watched her sister's hands.

The brush went on, blossom by blossom. "There was a woman did the near rows for me, two winters back," Helen said. "Good hands. Then she had her boy back — drowned, years before, when he was small — and after that she didn't come. You can't keep a hand once they've had someone back. The work's nothing to them after." She took the flower in front of her and touched the brush to the inside of it and went on to the next. "I don't ask anymore."

The hand came in late. The door at the bright end of the house opened and the outside came in with him, the dry cold of it for a second before the door took itself shut again on its weight, and he stood inside the door a moment the way a person stands who is letting his eyes do something. He was young. He had the look they had, the ones who had started — not far into it, not the look of the ones who were most of the way gone, but the front of that look, the thinness coming into the face before the rest of it, the way of holding still inside a bright room as if the brightness were a thing he was waiting out. He kept to the shaded side, where the staging threw the plants' own shadow across the walk. Claire's eye went to him and went to the held way he stood and to the front of the look in his face, and went toward the thing the look was the front of, and at that it turned, and was on the row again, on Helen's hands, on the count.

"Tam," Helen said. She did not stop the brush. "There's a second one on the bench. Start at the door end and work toward me."

He got the second brush off the bench. He did it slowly, not unwillingly, only slowly, the way a person does a thing his hands know while the rest of him is elsewhere, and he came down the shaded side to the door end of his own row and started on the plants there. Claire watched him a moment the way she had watched Helen, to see the work, and saw that he did the near flowers and not the others, that his hand went to the ones at the height his hand fell to and left the high ones and the low ones, that he was doing what Helen had said the bees did, the near ones, and missing what she had said you had to get. He was not slow at each flower. He was slow between them. His hand would do one and then his hand would stop, holding the brush, not at rest exactly, just stopped, and then it would go on.

"You'll want the high ones," Helen said, not looking up from her own row. "On the trusses you can't reach standing. There's the step by the heater."

"I know it." He got the step and stood on it and did some of the high ones and came down and did not move the step along as he went, so that he got the high ones on the one plant and not on the next, and Helen registered this without looking, Claire could see her register it in the small way her own hand kept its rhythm and did not, the way the body keeps doing the thing it is doing while the mind makes a note for later.

They worked. The warm held in the house and the light held outside it and Helen's brush went blossom by blossom down her row with the small certainty it had and Tam's went down his with the gaps in it. For a while no one said anything. The water moved somewhere in the slow system Helen had built that Claire did not understand the working of, and the heaters were off in the long light and would come on again when it went, and the green smell of the tomato leaves was on Claire's hands where she had brushed a plant getting to the stool, the smell that stayed on you, sharp and not like anything you would eat.

Then Tam said, to no one, with the brush in his hand and his hand stopped, "We'd have the whole top end of the orchard picked before it got hot. The lot of us. You'd start in the dark and you'd be done the top end by the time the heat came up and you'd go down into the trees for the day part where it was cool." His hand was not moving. "You couldn't see the end of the rows for the leaves. You'd lose the others in it. You'd call out and they'd call back and you couldn't see them." He said it the way a person says a thing he is looking at. He was not looking at anything in the house. "I don't know is it real," he said, to no one, after a moment. "I don't ask it." Helen did not answer him. She took the flower in front of her in her fingers and touched the brush to the inside of it and went on to the next one. Then his hand went on, to the next near flower, and did it, and stopped again.

Helen did not say anything to it. She came to the end of her row and turned and started back up the next one, which brought her a few feet nearer to him, and she said, in the same voice she had said the bees in, "Get the far side of yours before you go, Tam. The far side doesn't do itself."

"I'll get it."

"All right."

Claire had stopped counting somewhere in the orchard. She found the count gone when she reached for it and did not start it again. She sat on the stool with the green smell on her hands and the boy at the door end doing the near flowers and her sister coming up the row toward her doing all of them, and her attention had gone out toward the boy again, toward the orchard he had said and the held way he stood and the front of the look in his face, and she did not follow it. It went to the doorway of the thing and stopped there, and she left it, in the bright house, with the smell of the leaves and the small sound of the two brushes, the one that was steady and the one that was not.

"You could help him with the far side," Helen said. It was not quite to Claire and it was not quite not.

"I'll help him," Claire said, and got up off the stool, and her legs had the stiff of the long sitting in them, and she took the brush from where Helen had set the spare one back on the bench — there had been a third, she saw, an older one with less bristle, that Helen kept for when there were hands to use it, which there were not, mostly, now — and she went down the shaded side to where the boy was. She did not say anything to him. He did not say anything to her. She started on the far side of the plants he had done the near side of, the side he had left, and she found the high flowers and the low ones and touched the brush to the inside of each the way Helen did, and she did them, and then her hand did one and stopped, holding the brush, and after a moment it went on, and she did not notice that it had stopped, and the boy beside her did not notice it either, each of them at the work and elsewhere in the way the work allowed, and Helen at the head of the row did all of hers and did not look at either of them.

Tam went before the row was done. He set the brush back on the bench and said something to Helen that Claire did not catch and Helen said all right and he went out the bright end the slow way he had come in, letting the cold in for a second and then the door taking itself shut, and through the glass Claire watched him cross the yard in the held light, not hurrying, keeping to the side of the yard where the house threw its shadow, and go down the path, and be gone behind the shoulder of the ground the way everything went behind it.

"He'll be no use to me by the high summer," Helen said. She said it the way she said which half of the seedlings would live. She had come down to where Claire was and she took up the row from where Tam had left it and went on with it, the near flowers and the high ones and the low ones, all of them, the brush going blossom by blossom with the small certainty it had. "He's a good boy. His people had the orchard, the middle one, before it stopped bearing the way it did. He was out there for it, the last few years it bore. I think that's where he's gone." She did one of the high ones standing flat-footed, reaching, not bothering with the step for the one. "I'll have to do the far house myself or not at all. I've been not at all, this week."

Claire stood with the third brush in her hand and did not say anything. The boy was a thing that had happened in the bright house and the orchard was a thing he had said and her sister was doing the row he had left, and the light outside held at its angle on the empty yard and did not move, and would not for hours, and Helen's hands went on down the row, blossom by blossom, getting all of them, in the warm she made against the cold that would come up out of the long night when the light finally went. Helen worked. The light held.
