_Chapter 8_

Helen was a few plants up the row, taking the side-shoots off the tomatoes with the thumbnail of her right hand — the small green shoots that came in where the leaf met the stem, that would make leaf and not fruit if she left them, and she did not leave them. She found each one and pinched and gave it the small turn that took it off clean and dropped it into the bucket without looking at the bucket, her hand going to the bucket because the bucket was where it had been the visit before and the visit before that, and then her hand went back up the plant to the leader and ran a length of the soft twine round it and the cane and made the loose figure she made, not tight, with room in it for the stem to thicken, and went on to the next plant. She did not look at her hands. She looked along the plant ahead of where her hands were. There was no waste in any of it. There had not been since Claire was a child.

Claire sat on the stool at the near end of the row with the bucket between her feet, where Helen had set it when she moved up, and she was counting the shoots in it. She had started in the second plant, the way she started now, without deciding to, and she had a number for the bucket and it told her nothing, because she did not know how many shoots a plant put out in a week or how many Helen had taken from the plants before Claire had come in, and the number went up by ones as the shoots came down off Helen's thumbnail. She kept it. It ran on by itself off to the one side of her while she sat in the warm with her eyes on the work.

The long light was on the yard outside the glass, holding at the angle it held, and inside the house it was warm in the way the house was warm in the held light, the heaters off, the warmth a thing the day was lending the house and would take back when the light went. The water moved somewhere in the slow system Helen had built. The green smell of the broken shoots was coming up off the bucket between Claire's feet, sharp, not like anything a person would eat.

"Bran's at the lower place again," Helen said. She did not stop the work. "The same thing. It seizes and they get it going and it seizes again. He says it's the last time but he said that the last time." She took a shoot, turned it, dropped it. "He'll be back tomorrow or the day after."

"You said that last time too."

"I did." Helen tied off a leader. "We're both of us saying it."

Claire watched the twine go round and the loose figure get made. "Is the far house done."

"No. The far house hasn't been done in nine days." Helen said it the way she said the temperatures. "I do this one in the morning and I mean to get to the far one and the day's gone before I do. I had the boy for a while, on the near work, and now I don't have the boy, so it's the one house properly and the far house when I can, and I can't, lately." She moved to the next plant. "He didn't come back. Tam. I told you he wouldn't last to the summer and he hasn't lasted to it. His people had the middle orchard. I think that's where he is."

"You're doing two houses' worth on your own."

"I'm doing one house's worth and letting the other go. That's not the same thing. If I tried to do the two I'd do neither right." Pinch, turn, drop. "There's no one to ask. There's no one in the town to ask now who'd come and do it and still be coming the week after. You ask and they say yes and they mean the yes, and then they're not there, and you've planned the week round a hand that isn't there, which is worse than planning it round no hand at all." She found a shoot low on the plant where the staging threw its shade and got it. "So I plan it round no hand."

Claire counted the shoots in the bucket. The number had got up past where she had it before and she let it go and started it again.

It came to her the way the soft thing came to her now, without her finding where it started — not a decision to say it, only the saying beginning to be there, the way her feet were on the path to Helen's before she had decided to come. She had said versions of it before. She had said _you don't have to_ and she had said _it doesn't have to be like this_ and Helen had let each of them go by the way she let the cold by, with a small motion of getting on, a hand to the next plant, the work taking the thing up before it could sit in the air, so that it had never once landed and Claire had never once watched it land.

"You could put it down," Claire said. "All of it. The far house and this one. You know you could. I keep telling you." The warmth was on her face and her hands. "It doesn't have to be this hard. None of it. You could come where it's easy."

Helen's hands did not stop.

They went on down the plant, the shoot and the turn and the drop, the leader and the twine and the loose figure, and Claire watched them go on and waited for the small motion, the getting-on that took the thing up, and it did not come, and the hands kept their rhythm and came to the end of the plant and tied it off, and then Helen set the loose end of the twine down against the cane where it would hold, deliberately, the way she set a thing down that she meant to come back to, and she straightened the small distance she straightened, and she turned and looked at Claire down the row.

She looked at her a beat past the looking. Claire felt it the way she felt it when Ruth did it, the whole of the attention arriving and staying, except that this was Helen, who had been looking at her since before Claire could remember being looked at, who saw the adult and under the adult the rest of it, all the way down.

"I know what you're asking me, Claire," Helen said.

The work was out of her hands now. She stood with her hands empty at the end of the row and the light from the yard was on the side of her face and she was not doing anything with her hands, which was a thing Claire had almost never seen.

"I've known a long while what it is. You don't have to keep it from me. You've not been keeping it from me, I don't think — you've been keeping it from the room, the both of us have, and I'm tired of the room." She said it level. There was nothing breaking in it. "I'm not coming."

Claire said nothing. The count was gone out of her and she did not reach for it.

"I'm not going to come where you go," Helen said. "Not for that. Not for what's in it. I've thought about it more than you'd want to know I have, and I'm not going to, and that's the whole of it. There's no more to it than that and I'm not going to make more of it." Her hands came together in front of her, the right thumb working the green stain at the side of the left thumbnail without her watching it do that, the only part of her that moved. "I'd have said it years ago if saying it did anything. It doesn't do anything. But I'm not standing in the room not saying it anymore. That's all that's changed."

The thing came up out of the work, then, the way the count came up, unasked — the open hand, turned up, offered, the breath that had aimed itself across the dark and known where she was, the hand that had come down and opened and waited for the one thing that would answer it — it came up and went out toward what Helen was refusing, toward the shape of her coming, toward what was in it — the two over the sink, not as the picture had them but as they would be now — and it reached the doorway of that and turned. Claire did not go in. She did not bring it back. Her own hands were on the rim of the bucket between her feet and she felt the cold the long light had not got into the bucket yet against her fingers.

"All right," Claire said.

It was not the right thing. She heard that it was not, the way you hear a wrong note without being able to name the note, and she did not have another thing to put where it should have gone, and so the _all right_ sat where it was and was wrong and stayed.

"I'm not telling you not to come," Helen said. "I want you to hear that part. I'm not saying don't come here." She picked the twine back up off the cane where she had set it, between two fingers, and held it without using it yet. "You'll come or you won't, the way you do. I'd rather you came. I've always rather you came." She turned the twine in her fingers. "I'll have the coffee out for you the same as ever. There's no part of this where I want you to stop walking over."

"I'll come," Claire said, down to the bucket and not up to Helen, and the warm thing the coffee being out had asked back of her she did not give.

"I know you mean it."

She had not said _and you won't._ She had said _I know you mean it_ and let the rest of it be the rest of it, the way she had let _Thursday, or whichever day_ carry what it carried at Ruth's door, except this was Helen, and Helen had never had the joke in her the way Ruth had, and there was no joke in this, only the plain front of it and the thing the plain front was holding up, and Claire heard the both of them and took down neither, because there was no notebook for Helen's and never had been.

Helen turned back to the row. She fitted the twine round the next leader and the cane and made the loose figure with the room in it, and went to the shoots, the thumbnail finding the small green where the leaf met the stem, the pinch and the turn, the shoot down into the bucket without the looking. Claire watched the bucket take it. The number was still gone and she did not start it again.

"You could move up," Helen said, after a while, not turning. "Bring the bucket. I'm losing the light off the low ones down this end and I'd as soon have it nearer."

Claire stood off the stool and her legs had the stiff of the sitting in them. She picked the bucket up by its handle, the broken shoots shifting and giving off the sharp green of them, and she carried it up the row to where her sister was working and set it down where Helen's hand would fall to it, in the place the hand already knew, and she stood there with the warm of the held light on her and the green on her hands where the bucket had brushed them, and Helen went on down the plant, shoot and turn and drop, leader and twine and the loose figure, getting all of them, the high ones and the low, in the warm she made against the cold that would come up out of the floor when the light finally went off the glass, and neither of them said the thing again, because it had been said.
