_Chapter 24_

Helen came up the street to the white house the way Claire used to come to it, up and along in the long early light, past the corner shop that had been boarded a year and more, past the houses that stood empty and unboarded between the lived-in ones with nothing in them and nothing said over them, past the bakery on Ruth's own street that had gone quiet in the winter and whose window somebody still kept clean for no one. She did not have the house by heart. She had it from the telling, years back and in another room — the upper windows wider than the lower, the low stone wall, no name on the gate — and she came up watching for it and found it where the telling had put it. She had the soil still on her, off the morning's work she had left standing to come; the gloves were in her coat and the cold of the walk had not yet got the made warm of the greenhouses out of her hands. She stopped at the gate. Then she opened it and went up the path and knocked.

Ruth had the box out on the table in the south light, square to the edge with the lid off and the ones she had not reached squared down inside, set for the end of a day against the next one; and it had stood so through more mornings than she had the count of, the light coming along the table and going off it and coming back, out for a next time that had not come up the street. So the knock was a moment placing itself, because a knock in the late of the morning had got to be the one knock over the long while, the way a sound gets to be the one thing that makes it, and this was not the day for that knock and was not its weight besides. She got up out of the chair the slow way she got up now and went through the bright room and the front room to the door, and opened it, and it was not Claire.

It was a woman she had seen twice and would have known for whose she was in any street in the town, because the younger one was in the face under the years the younger one did not have on her yet — the same set to the eyes, the same way of standing square to a thing and waiting on it. There was soil dried along the side of the jaw where a hand had pushed the hair back and not known it left the mark, and the hands at the ends of the coat-sleeves were a worker's hands, scrubbed at and not clean, the knuckles standing, the nails kept short for the work. Ruth read the whole of her in the doorway as she had read people in a doorway for sixty years, and what she read first, before the sister, was the other thing — the thing she knew by signs too small to say, that this was a woman the dream had asked and been told no, and gone on asking, and been told no, and that she had the look of the ones who said it and stayed.

"You'll be Helen," Ruth said.

"I am."

"I'd ask did Claire send you, but Claire's not sending anybody anywhere this while. Come in out of it. The cold's coming up the street behind you and it'll be in the room before you are if I stand here holding the door for it."

Helen came in and Ruth shut the door on the cold and took her through to the bright room, where the box stood out on the table in the light it stood out in. Helen looked at it the way a person looks at a thing set out and waited on, and did not ask who it was waited on for, because she would have known, and Ruth did not say.

"She's not been," Helen said. It was not a question. She said it flat and let it be the thing it was.

"She's not been. Not in — I'd have to think, and I don't keep the dates, I keep the box. A good while. It was every week the first long while, and then it was the longer stretches, and then it was the box out and nobody come for it." She lowered herself back into the chair. "Sit, if you'll sit. You've come a way and you've a way back and there's a chair doing nothing."

Helen sat. She kept her coat on, the way you keep it on in a house you are not stopping long in, and she put her hands on her knees and they were still on them.

"She's stopped coming down to me as well," Helen said. "I'd her down for the planting in the spring and not after. I let it be a while, the way you let a thing be, and then it was a while too long to be letting it. So I came up to see were you getting more of her than I was." She looked at the box. "And you're not."

"I'm not."

The light moved on the table the small distance it moved, and neither of them did anything in it, and then Ruth reached into the box and got one up off the not-reached, the side of the thumb and the side of the first finger, the lift she had now, and held it off at the arm's length where the near and the far of her eyes still met on a thing, and looked at it.

"That's nobody you'd know. A christening, down the valley, before your time and near before mine. I get them up to look at them, is the most I do with them now." She tilted it a degree into the light. "I can put a year on the most of them inside a year or two, which is more than the families could, the ones still going. That one'd be the year after my daughter was born — Joan — give or take the once way or the other. I count off her when I want it close. She's the fixed thing I've got." She set the picture on the gone-through stack, squared. "She'd be an old woman herself now, older than the one I made that of. That's the kind of sum you're left doing, at my end of it." She did not look up while she said it and she did not bring the name back, and the name went out into the room and stayed where it fell.

"You'd have looked at her," Helen said. "The while she came. It's what she came to you for, the looking."

"She came for the pictures. But I looked at her. You don't stop, with a face in the room — sixty years of it doesn't switch off because the body's done with the trade." She had her hands flat on the stack now, the worsened right one and the left, laid the way she laid them on a thing she had decided to leave. "You want to know how she was."

"I know how she was when she came down to me. I want to know was it the same up here, or did she keep a better face for you."

"It was the same face." Ruth looked at her, the whole of the looking come up and arriving and staying. "I'll tell it the only way I have it, which is mine. I watched it come over her the way I'd watched it come over a good few, the years I had the eyes for it. There's how a tired face takes the light, and a grieving one, and an old one — they take it and give you something back, and you've a picture. And there's a face that takes the light and gives you nothing, and I had a few of those across the years and learned there was no lighting them; you waited, and they didn't come back. Hers had gone to that, the last while she sat there. I'd not have got a picture of her worth the plate, and I made my living off people who'd been at a graveside the hour before. The light was off her and not coming back the way it comes back of a morning. Two autumns gone it started, near as I'd date it, and it went the one way from there."

Helen took it the way she took it. She did not say anything for a moment, and her hands stayed where they were, and then the one of them closed on the cloth over her knee and opened again, the once, and that was the whole of what moved in her. Ruth watched it go through her and watched her hold — the worker's stillness of someone who learned young that you do not come apart in front of whoever is in the room, and had not unlearned it for being in a stranger's house over the one thing that might have undone it.

"I'd thought it was the worst of it down with me," Helen said. "That she kept the rest of herself for the work and for up here, and it was only with me she let it go to what it'd gone to. I had a kind of comfort in that I didn't look at too close." She looked at the box. "There wasn't a better face kept anywhere, then."

"There wasn't a better face kept anywhere," Ruth said. "There was the one face and it was going, and you got the under side of it and I got the over and neither of us got it back. You'll not have her back for having walked up here, if that was any of it."

"It wasn't. Or it was, and I knew better than to bring it." Helen's voice was level; there was nothing breaking in it. "I came up to see were you all right, with her not coming. She was the one came to you. There's nobody comes to you now, with her not, and somebody had better see were you all right, and there was nobody but me to think it."

Ruth looked at her a while. The light had gone the small distance along the table and the cold was beginning to come up off the floor in the corners where the light had left it, and she did not get up to anything about it.

"She asked you, then," Ruth said. "Down at your end. The thing she asks."

"She asked me."

"And you told her no."

"I told her no."

"So did I." She squared the box without taking anything from it, the gone-through and the not-reached both, and laid her hands back over it. "There's the two of us, then, and her gone the other way off the both of us, and we never had the sense to know each other while there was the one of her between us to do it through." She let the rest of it be the rest, the staying with the no and what the staying had cost the two of them, which she had never been one to say out where the saying did not help; and she could see it was a thing the woman across from her did not say either, and there was an ease in the not-saying, the two of them at it together, that Ruth had not gone looking for and did not name.

Helen stayed a while longer than she had set out to, by the look of her, and then she stood, because the day she had was the day she had and the work left standing did not stand itself. Ruth did not get up. Helen put her hand out, and Ruth took it the way she took things now, the side of the thumb, and let it go.

"Mind the walk back. The streets are nothing to look at and they're worse for not minding them."

Helen went out through the bright room and the front room, and the latch took the door behind her. Ruth got up and went to the wide window she had put where she put it for the light, a long time before there was this little to look at by it, and watched the sister go back down the street. She went down it square to it, not hurrying, the soil on her and the work waiting, a woman going back to the thing she did because it was the thing there was to do, and she did not look back at the house, and Ruth did not want her to. Ruth watched her to the turn and a little past it, the look that had been her whole life going out after her, holding the whole of her and saying none of it, the way it had held a great many of them, down the years and out of the town, and let them go. Then the street had her no more. Ruth turned back into the room where the box stood out in the going light, set for a next time the way she set it, and the next time she had set it for was not coming, and one she had not set it for had come and gone; and there was the rest of the day, and she went back to the chair and was in it.
