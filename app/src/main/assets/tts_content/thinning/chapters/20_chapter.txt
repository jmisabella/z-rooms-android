_Chapter 20_

"That one's come," Helen said, and turned the flat round to the made light and went down it with her eye. "And the one beside it. This run here's gone blind on me, the most of it — nothing up in them and no saying why, the same seed off the same dish and the same wet and the same warm down the lot of them." She set the flat by and drew the next along the bench. Claire sat at the near end of the staging with the enameled cup gone cool between her hands and watched the flats come along under the light, the ones with the green up in them and the ones with the cells still dark; and the green was the come-up of the seed Helen had saved off the poor year and sowed down the cells in the dead bottom of it — Claire had sat here while she did it, the dish at her left hand and the cells going dark and sown behind her — and it had lain through the cold weeks with nothing showing, and now in the most of the flats it was up, the first of it standing out of the dark of the medium, no more than the seed-leaf yet, small and even and pale in the made light.

The day past the glass was not the poor grey it had been. It had come up better this last while — later still than it ought and going sooner than it should, but with something in it now, a faint wash of the real light along the white fronts of the houses below where there had been none, the year turning up out of the bottom of itself the slow way it turned. It was not yet a light to bring the seed on by, and would not be for weeks; so Helen had the made light run close over the bench still, the same low pale light she kept through the long nights, the line of it Bran had set out to the bench for her, on by day over the young because the day did not yet give them what they wanted. The heaters were on under it. The warm of the house had got into Claire a while back, the face and the neck and then the hands, and she had let it go unmarked as she let it go, so that what was left her to hold was the cold of the cup in her hands and the cold that stood off past the light at the dark end of the house, over the bed Helen had cleared at the year's end, the bare run of it still open and broken fine and waiting on the year.

She went down the standing flats and gave the come-up its water out of a small can with a fine rose on it that broke the wet to almost nothing, so it came down on the young like a breath of it and did not lay them over or wash the seed-leaf flat; and where a cell had come up she gave it its little drink, and where a cell was dark she gave it the same, because the seed might be only slow in the cell and not dead, and she watered the blind ones the same till she was sure of them. "Some'll come up a week behind the rest," she said. "You'd have thrown the cell out if you'd been quick about it, and there'd have been a plant in it the next time you looked. So you wait on them. But the ones that don't come don't come, and there's no second sowing them — I'd not the seed for it, and what there was is down already, the whole of it."

Claire had the number of the green going. She had it without setting out to have it, the cells that had come up, up by the ones down the flats as Helen turned them round to the light, and it arrived nowhere, because the green that was up so far was not the count of the ones that would make it nor of the ones that were gone — some of these would go off yet, and some of the dark cells would still come — it was only the count of what was up this far on, which was a different thing and would stay a different thing for weeks. It stood for even less than the counts had stood for. She kept it anyway.

"They'll want easing off the made light before long," Helen said. "Once the day's worth the bringing them out into, I'll have them onto the glass and the made light back to the nights where it lives. I'll know by the turn which of them took and which I'm to grow on, and how many flats it comes to against the year — not many, off what's up. But it's up, the most of it, and that's the year started." She turned a flat. "You sow in the dead of it on nothing but the say-so that the light comes back, and then it comes back, a little, and there it is."

"Bran's been up at the pump-house this week," she said, after a while. "The one on the near side that went and that no one had touched since the man who had it died. Somebody asked him, and he had the time of it now, being home, so he went. He'll be at it a few days yet." She set a flat by. "It's the kind of near thing he'd not have got to before, always away down at the lower place. There's the time for them now."

"You can give them their water, if you want," Helen said. "The young. Mind you keep the rose on it and hold it high — bring it down close and hard and you'll have them over, and a laid-over one at this size doesn't always come back up. A pass to each flat and no more. They've not the root yet to take much." She set the small can by Claire's hand on the staging and went back down the bench to the turning of the flats.

Claire set the cold cup down on the staging and took up the can — the metal of it cold, and the water in it cold, in the cold of her hand — and went down the first flat as Helen had said, the rose held high and the wet coming down soft and broken on the green, a pass and on to the next; and her sister was down the bench at the turning of the flats, the come-up brought round to the light and the dark cells set by; and the hands that did it did not come. What came up to Claire was the work coming right — the flats turned and read and stood back in their row — and the voice over it, plain and sure, when she said a thing about the watering. Then Claire's own hand brought the can down too close over a cell, the way she had been told not to, the wet coming hard on the green of it, and before she had it back up Helen's hand was on the back of hers and took it up and round to the angle it should keep, high, and held it there while the wet went soft again on the cells the way it ought, and let it go. And the warm of Helen's hand came into the cold of Claire's whole, all of it — the heat of it and the dry of it and the work in it, and the press of the fingers that knew the angle and set her hand to it and went — the warm she made and the warm the made light had laid along the back of her working hand, crossing into the cold of Claire's and filling it the whole breadth of it; and the hand did not come. What came was the warm, all the way and whole, and the sure of the fingers setting the angle and gone; and the hand that did it, warm the whole breadth of it on the back of hers, did not come. The touch of it passed, and the want did not come up at it, and the watering went on.

Claire went on down the flats with the can, a pass to each and no more, the cold of the metal in the cold of her hand and the warm of the house gone into her unmarked as it went; and Helen turned the flats round to the made light behind her, the come-up to the light and the dark cells the same, watering the blind ones against the chance the seed in them was only slow; and the count of the green Claire kept went up by the ones and stood for nothing, because the green that was up was not the took and not the lost but only the up-so-far, and which of it had taken and which was behind for now and which was gone for good was a thing the weeks would say and the count could not. The made light lay close and even over the whole row of them, the green of the flats and the dark of them; the cold stood off at the dark end of the house over the bare bed; and the year was started in the cells under the light and a long way yet from anything you could call it by.
