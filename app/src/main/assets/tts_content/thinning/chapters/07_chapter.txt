_Chapter 7_

The light had started its going by the time Claire was well out into the streets — holding its angle along the fronts of the houses through the whole long afternoon, the way it held, and then thinning at the top of itself, so that once it had begun to leave it left faster than a person could walk in it. She had the sack folded into the pocket of her coat for the meal. The woman who still parceled the dry meal out kept her door open into the dusk on the days it came in, and this had been one of the days it came in when Claire set out, and the way to her went down through the close streets where the houses ran near to each other, which were the streets Claire took. The dusk came up sooner between the close houses than it did in the open part. That was nothing to her. She had got easier, this last while, in the light that was leaving than in the light that held.

She counted the windows that had a light in them as she went. She did it the way she counted things now, without having decided to begin and without being able to have said what the count was for. There were the windows with a light in them and the windows without, and she had a number for the lit ones going by the time she was a street down, and it told her nothing, because she did not know how many of these houses had anyone in them to start with, and the dark ones were not all empty, and the lit ones were not all full. The number went up as she walked. She kept it the way she kept the steps on the walk home, in the part of her that did that now, while the rest of her walked the route her feet knew without her.

There was almost no one out. There was almost no one out at any hour now on these streets, but there were fewer at this one, the hour the light went, and the few who were out in it were out the way she was out — at the edge of the dark, easy in it, keeping to the sides where the houses put their shade down early. She passed one of them going the other way and did not look, and was not looked at. The cold was beginning to come up off the stone underfoot the way it came, from the feet first, the warmth going out of the ground as the light went off it.

The street bent and ran down past the building where the back room was that she had sat in, two winters gone now, three or four times, with the man who had walked the round. The room was at the back and she had gone in at the side door, and they had sat at a table by a window that had not had much light in it even then, and he had given her the round across it while she wrote — how the round had gone shorter on him over the years he carried it, not because they had cut it but because the doors went dark one at a time and a dark door was no stop, so that he came in the end to walking the old distance for fewer and fewer of them, the same ground and half the doors, and then fewer than half, until they had not needed a man to walk it and he had been the last who did. She had that, more or less, in the notebook, in the words he had put it in. The side window of the back room was dark as she came down past it. The light that was leaving the street did not reach in that far.

He was out, though. He had got himself onto the low step at the front, his back to the brick where the wall threw down what little dark there was to throw at this hour, and he had the look of the ones who were most of the way gone — not the front of that look, the way the boy in the greenhouse had had the front of it, but the most of it, the face gone down to the bone of itself, the color gone out from under the skin, the stillness that was not a waiting and was not a rest, the stillness of a body with nothing left in the day that it had to do. His hands were in his lap. The last of the light was across the street from him, on the far fronts, and he had set himself where it was not. His face was turned a little up and out toward the street as she came down it, toward where she was, and there was nothing in it that came forward to meet her. The eyes were open. They were the eyes of the most-gone, that took the dark in and gave nothing back out of it, and whether they were on her or only on the place she happened to be crossing she could not have said, and it did not look to be a thing that he could have said either.

It came up out of the work then, the way the count came up, on its own and unasked, from the part of her that kept the sessions — the side door, the table, the window with no light in it, the voice that had given her the round across it while she wrote, the round gone short, the dark doors, the last man to walk it. It came up and it went out toward the man on the step, the voice toward the open eyes that gave nothing back, and it had nearly reached him, had nearly laid the one over the other and made them the same man, when his hand came open in his lap.

She did not see it move. It was closed and then it was open, the palm of it turned up the way a hand turns when the thing it is doing is being done somewhere the hand itself is not, and it lay open in his lap and did not reach for anything. And at the open hand the other came up under it, the way a thing comes up that she had not called — the dark of her sister's back room, and the breath that had stopped being a sleeping breath and aimed itself, and the arm that had crossed the cover not searching, that had known where her sister was and gone to her certain, and the hand at the end of it that had come open and turned its palm up and lain against her and waited, offered, for the one thing that would answer it — it came up under the man on the step, and the two of them were there at once, the open hand in her sister's room and the open hand in the man's lap, the same shape turned up in the two dark places, and her attention went out toward the both of them, toward the one hand and the other, and found nothing on either it could take hold of, and turned.

She did not bring it back. She did not give either of them a name. The thing that had come up out of the work came apart again into the parts it was made of — the side door, the table, the voice, the round — and let go of the man on the step, who was not the voice now, who was a body in the dark the light had set itself across the street from, and she walked on past him the way she walked on past the dark houses, not breaking her stride, and behind her on the step he did not turn his head to follow her, or if he turned it she did not see it, because she did not look back. When she reached for the count of the lit windows at the next corner it was gone, and she did not start it again.

The close streets gave out onto the open part at the bottom. The light had finished while she was on his street; the open part was in the blue now, the far houses gone to their shapes, and a long way down it there was the one small held window that she took for the woman's, yellow and low against the rest. She stood at the corner a moment. The cold was all the way up off the ground now and into her feet, and the dark was down the rest of the way, the part of the day that fit her, and she had the folded sack still in her pocket and the meal still to get and the long way back to walk after she had it. She went on down toward the window, into the open, with the close street behind her that she had not looked back along.

The woman had the door open before Claire was up to it, the way she did on the days the meal came in, the lamp behind her in the room and the parceled sacks on the table at her back. She had Claire's ready in her hand. She did not make her wait the small wait she made the others wait while she squared the share, and she did not keep her at the door with the talk she kept the others with, the who-was-poorly and who-had-gone; she put the sack into Claire's hands and stood a little back from her while she did it, and spoke the once, low and level, the way you speak to a person you have decided not to startle. "Mind the cold going back." Her eyes went over Claire's face the once while she said it, the quick over-and-down of a look that already knew what it would find and was only seeing that it was still there, and then they came off her, easy, the way you let be a thing there is no use looking harder at. She drew back into the lamp.

Claire took the sack and said the thing you said and went up into the dark with it, and behind her the woman put the door to against the cold.
