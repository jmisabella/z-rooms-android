_Chapter 12_

Ruth had set the box on the floor by her chair and laid her hands flat over the gone-through stack, the worsened right one and the left, the way she laid them on a thing she had decided to leave where it was, and they had come to the part of the afternoon that was after the photographs and before the coat, the part that had got longer over the year, where Ruth did not get the next one up and Claire did not rule the date, and the south light held on the table between them and neither of them did anything in it for a while.

The room was warm with the held light the way Ruth's room got warm, the light coming in long across the table and across the boxes against the far wall and lying on the floor between them, and the cold was still down in the corners where the light had not got and would come up out of them when the light went off the table, and it had not gone yet. There was tea somewhere in the room that had been hot and was not. Claire had the notebook closed on her knee under her hand. She had written less than half a page of the afternoon down and she knew it and the page was closed on the knowing of it.

Ruth was looking at her. She had been looking at her since the box went down, the whole of the attention come up off the photographs and arriving and staying, the looking of a woman who had spent her life looking at people and had not worn it out with the eyes, only moved it back behind them, and she did not say what she was looking at. She had looked at Claire that way for over a year, across the gone-through stacks, the looking that noticed the thing Claire would rather it did not and did not name the thing, and there was nothing new in it that Claire could have pointed to, except that it stayed, and did not go back down to a photograph, because there was no photograph up.

It came up the way the count came up. Not a thing she decided. She was sitting in the warm with the notebook closed on her knee and the long light on the table and her sister's voice somewhere far back in her saying the plain thing it said, _I heard you the once,_ and the want came up under all of it the way it came up now, from the floor of her, unasked — the want for Ruth to have the thing, for the thing to be given, for the long after-part of the afternoon to be the easy one and not the one where the box went better than half full back onto the floor — and it came up and it went out toward Ruth across the table the way her attention went out toward a thing, and this time, where it would have turned, at the place it always turned, it did not turn. It went on toward her. It went to the doorway it went to and it did not stop at the doorway, and her hand, on the closed notebook on her knee, came up off the notebook.

She did not feel it decide to. It came up the way the breath had aimed itself in her sister's dark room, level, knowing the distance, not searching — she watched it go the way she had watched the other go, from somewhere back of it — and it crossed the warm bright air over the table toward where Ruth's hands lay flat on the squared photographs, and it opened. The fingers loosened off and the palm turned up and it lay open in the air over the table, open, offered, and it asked nothing a waking hand could ask and it asked anyway, the only way it had, by being open and waiting, for the one thing that would answer it, which was for Ruth to turn one of her own hands over from where they lay and lay it up into Claire's.

Ruth looked at the hand.

She did not startle. The looking did not change, or changed only in that it went from Claire's face down to the open hand and stayed there a beat the way it had stayed on Claire, taking it in, getting the whole of it into herself the way she got a photograph into herself before she said the thing about it, and what was in her face was not surprise, because she had known, she had known across the gone-through stacks for longer than Claire could have said, and this was only the thing she had known coming up at last into the room where it could be seen, the way a date came up out of her at last when she had been a while finding it. She looked at the open hand a beat past the looking. Then her hands moved.

They moved the way Helen's had moved at the cover, both of them, without waste, without hurry, knowing the thing they were for — the worsened one and the left — and she did not take Claire's hand the way you take a hand that is offered. She took it the way she took a photograph up off the stack now, between the side of the thumb and the side of the first finger, because the pad did not do it well anymore and the side of the thumb was the thing she had found that still did, and she brought her other hand under it, and she lifted the open hand up out of the air where it was offered and carried it the short way back across the table, and she set it down on the closed notebook on Claire's knee, where it had come up from, and she let it go. The palm closed as it came to rest on the notebook. The offer shut.

"No," Ruth said. There was no point on it. It was the voice she used about her own work, the flat exact voice that corrected a date and in the next breath said the date did not matter, and the no was said in it the way she said the names of the dead, a fact that had finished happening. "Not that. You can give me the rest of it. Not that."

Claire's hand was on the notebook where Ruth had set it. She felt the cold of her own fingers come into the back of it, or the warm of the room not yet in them, one or the other.

"I never put a face on anybody that wasn't doing what their face did," Ruth said. She was looking at Claire again, the hand business done and set aside the way she set a thing aside that she had decided to leave where it was. "People asked me to. More than you'd think. Take ten years off her, take the tired out of him, he never smiled like that a day in his life but smile him for me. And I could have. You can do it in the printing, some of it, and the rest you can do by what you let them stand in front of and how long you keep them, and I knew the tricks, I knew them better than anybody up here. And I didn't do it. Not because I was good. Because a picture of a thing that didn't happen isn't a picture of anything. It's a nice lie you paid for, and you'd know it every time you looked at it, underneath, even if you couldn't say what you knew." She got the next breath the way she got it now, with the work of it showing a little. "I'd not have one of those of my own. I'm too old to start keeping the kind of thing I spent forty years not making."

She let it go the way she let a name go, and did not look to see whether it had landed, because she did not look to see whether the things she said landed; she said them and went on. She reached down to the box on the floor and squared it without taking anything out of it, the gone-through and the not-reached both, and laid her hands back over it.

"Get your coat before the cold comes up," she said. "It comes up the minute the light's off the table and I'll not be the one tells you you've gone blue." And then, in the same voice, not a different one, no weight moved onto it: "I'm not going anywhere between now and whenever you're next by. That hasn't changed. None of it's changed but the one thing, and the one thing was never going to be a thing I did, so."

Claire ruled the date at the top of a clean page for the next time and underlined it in the small clean way she ruled dates and closed the notebook on it and put it in the inside pocket of her coat against her ribs. Her hand did the ruling and the underlining and the clipping the way it did, and she watched it do them from back of it the way she had watched the other thing, and it did them right, the date and the line under it clean, and the page under the date was clean too, and the one before it, and the one before that, which she did not count.

"Next time," Ruth said. She had not got up. She sat in the last of the south light with the box better than half full beside her and her hands flat on it.

"Next time," Claire said.

"All right."

Claire put her coat on, and the cold was beginning to come up off the floor in the corners where the light had already gone, and Ruth was in the chair in the part of the light that was left, smaller in the chair than she had been a year ago and looking at Claire the way she looked, the whole of it arrived and staying, not saying the thing she saw, having said the only part of it she was going to say. Claire went out through the room and the front room and the door, and the latch took it shut behind her, and the street was in the blue already on this side of the houses, the light gone off the fronts and only the high air still holding it, and she stood on Ruth's step a moment with her hand on the gate and then she opened the gate and went through it, and there was the long walk back in the going light, and she did not count the steps of it, and a good deal of the dark to come down yet before she was home.
