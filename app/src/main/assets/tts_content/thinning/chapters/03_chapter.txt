_Chapter 3_

The light had come around to the table a while ago and was on the notebook now, across the open page of it, and Claire had been looking at the page in the light for longer than the looking needed. It was the last page she had written at Ruth's. The notes ran down it in the shorthand she used in a session, which was smaller and faster than her ordinary hand and which she could read without trouble as long as she read it soon. She had not read it soon. It had been longer than she usually let it go. She could still read it. The words were the words she had put down and she knew what they were, but a few of them had the quality of a thing said by someone else that she was being told she had said.

_Mrs. Halsey, before the bridge._ _Children look further away._ _Had not said anything either._

The pages of the account were beside the notebook on the table, squared to the edge of it, where they had been since the morning or the day before. The account was the larger book — not a book yet, a stack, the fair sheets she wrote the sessions onto once she had them in order, in her ordinary hand, in full sentences, the way they would be read by whoever read them. The stack was thick on the left side, where the worked sessions were, and the sheets on the right side were empty and squared and waiting. She kept the two things separate because the notebook went with her, against her ribs, into rooms where she was working, and the account stayed on the table; and because the work of the one was not the work of the other. The notebook was for getting it down. The account was for getting it right. She had let the distance between the two grow over the last while in a way she had been aware of without measuring.

She picked up the first pen.

The light through the window came in low and long and held the way it held, the same angle across the floor and up the leg of the table that it had held for most of the afternoon, the angle that did not move the way light was supposed to move and then moved all at once when the day finally went. It was not going yet. There was a good deal of the afternoon still in the room. She had time to do the session before the light went. The session was not a long one. She had the notes and she knew what they said and the doing of it was only the doing of it.

She read down the notebook page again. The teacher who had stood off to the side for thirty years and let herself be in the picture once, at the end, and had cried while it was taken, very quietly, and Ruth had developed the film and seen it and said nothing and given her the picture with the others. Claire had written that down more or less as Ruth had said it. She had underlined _had not said anything either._ She looked at the underlining. She had made it in the session and she remembered making it and she could not now have said what she had meant the line under it to hold.

She set the pen down on the empty sheet without writing anything on the sheet.

The room was small and she knew where everything in it was without looking. The table was under the window for the light. The chair she was in was the one she worked in. The other chair was against the wall and had a coat over the back of it that was not the coat she wore out. The stove was cold; she had not lit it; the cold of the long night had not come down into the rooms yet but it would, the way it came down every night, from the corners first and then the floor. There was nothing on the stove. There was a cup on the table from earlier that had had tea in it and now had the ring of the tea in the bottom of it, dried. She had not moved it.

She picked the pen up again.

The first sentence of a session was the one that took the time. Once the first sentence was down the rest came along behind it, because the rest was only the carrying-over of what was in the notebook into the longer hand, and that was work but it was not the kind of work that stopped you. The first sentence was the one where you decided what the session was. She had done it many times. She knew how it went. _On the third of the month I sat with Ruth at the worktable and she showed me photographs from the years she took the school pictures._ Something like that. A date, a place, the two of them at the table, the thing that had been in front of them. Then the teacher, then the girl with the hands, then the light in the rooms that changed not because of the rooms.

She did not write it.

She had the sentence and she did not write it, and she sat with the pen on the sheet and the sheet stayed empty under the pen, and after a while she became aware that her hand had not moved and that she had been looking at the place on the sheet where the first word would go without the word arriving there. She moved the pen off the sheet. She had been counting, she realized, the worked sheets in the stack on the left while she did not write — she had counted them once already at some point in the afternoon and had got a number and the number was the same number and she put it out of her mind, because it was a number that did not need keeping and because keeping it was a thing her mind did while the rest of her did not do the thing in front of her.

She got up.

She did not get up to go anywhere. There was nowhere in the rooms to go. She stood by the table for a moment with the pen still in her hand and then she put the pen down on the sheet again and went to the window and looked out of it. The street below was the street. There was the house across that had no one in it and the curtains in its lower window that were the curtains that had been in it, and there was the angle of the light along the fronts of the houses that ran down the street, and there was no one on the street, which at this hour was not strange. It was past the hour. It was past the hour she would have set out at, on a day she was going, by a good while — she could read that off the light without thinking about it, the light was the light of after, not the light of the walk — and she had not set out, and the not-setting-out had not had the shape of a decision in it anywhere that she could find. The day had simply got to where it was with her in the rooms.

She stood at the window. The light was beginning, now, to do the thing it did, to thin at the top of itself, to start the going that once it started did not take long. She watched it begin.

She thought, without going toward it, of her sister's face. It came up the way it had been coming up — not a thought of Helen, not anything she was thinking _about_ Helen, only the face as it had been in the greenhouse in the indoor light, older in that light than in the day, turned a little toward her over the tray, and the soil along the jaw that Helen did not know was there and that Claire had not told her about. The face came up and Claire's attention went to it and then her attention was somewhere else, on the window, on the cold coming up through the floor of the rooms now from the corners, and the face was not there anymore. She did not bring it back. She had not put it down so much as turned, the way you turn from a room you are not going into, and it was behind her.

She went back to the table.

The notebook was open where she had left it. She turned the page. The next page had the date she had ruled at the top of it and underlined, in the small clean way she ruled dates, and nothing under the date. She had ruled it for the session she had meant to have the week she had not gone. She turned past it. The page after had no date on it. The pages after that had no dates on them. She turned through them slowly, the blank pages, the pages that were the shape the next sessions would have if the next sessions were had, and they went on under her hand, blank and ruled-ready and waiting in the way paper waits, which is to say not at all, paper does not wait, it only stays where it is and is blank until it isn't. She turned through to the back of the notebook and there was nothing in any of them and she let the notebook fall closed.

Ruth's hands were getting worse. Ruth had not said so. Ruth had got up slowly the other day — not the other day, the last day, the day in the notebook, the day of the school pictures, which was further back now than _the other day_ would mean. The knuckles of the right hand were larger than they had been. Claire had noticed it visits ago and had not written it down anywhere, because it was not the kind of thing the account was for, and because writing it down would have been keeping it the way she kept the numbers she did not need, and she did not want to keep it.

She opened the notebook again to the last written page and read the notes once more in the light that was now going faster. The teacher who had cried. The girl with the hands clasped in front of her that someone had told her to clasp, the one Ruth saw when she closed her eyes, out of the thousands, that one. _I don't know why._ Ruth had said _I don't know why_ and Claire had not written that part down, had only written the girl, and now she could not have the _I don't know why_ on the sheet because it was not in the notebook, it was only in her, and the account was made from the notebook and not from her. That was the discipline of it. You wrote what was got down. What was not got down was gone, and the getting-down was the whole of the work, and the work was sitting in front of her got-down and waiting to be carried over and she was not carrying it over.

She picked up the pen.

The light was nearly off the table now. It had gone up the wall the way it went, the long band of it climbing and narrowing as the sun got to where it got to over the valley, and the table was in the lower light now, and the sheet under the pen was grey rather than white, and the words on the notebook page were still readable but only just, and would not be readable much longer, and the thing to do, the thing she knew to do, the thing that any evening's work required and that she had done on a hundred evenings, was to get up and cross to the shelf and put on the lamp, and bring the light back to the table, and do the session while the notes could still be read.

She sat with the pen on the sheet.

The light went up the wall and off it. The room came down slowly into the blue the rooms came down into, and then past the blue, the corners first. The notebook page on the table was a pale shape with no words on it that could be read now. The sheet under her hand was the same pale shape, empty. Her hand was on the sheet and the pen was in her hand and she did not get up, and the cold came up out of the floor and into the room, and she sat in it, and the dark came on the rest of the way and she was in the rooms in the dark with the work in front of her that she had not done, and she was not troubled by the dark, and she did not move.
