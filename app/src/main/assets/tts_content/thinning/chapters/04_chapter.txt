_Chapter 4_

He had come back from the lower place while there was still light in the yard. Claire had watched him come up the path from the kitchen window, where she was standing with a cup she was not drinking, and had watched Helen go to the door and stand in it without going out to him, and then he was in, and the part of the evening that had been the two sisters was over.

He had a part with him, a flat iron thing wrapped in cloth that he set on the bench by the door without unwrapping, the way a man sets down a thing he has carried far enough that the carrying is the part he is done with. There was the lower-place smell on him, oil and cold metal and the particular dust of a building that other people kept. His hands had the work in the lines of them. He washed them at the sink and the water ran grey off them and then less grey and then he dried them and that was the work put away for the night.

"It's seized worse than they said," he told Helen. "I'll have to go back."

"When."

"Day after. Maybe the day after that." He looked at Claire then, not unkindly, the way you look at a thing in a room you had not been told would be in the room. "You walked over."

"Yes."

"She wouldn't have taken the cart," Helen said, from the stove.

"No," Bran said. "She wouldn't."

They ate at the table in the back. There was bread, which was the bread from the cloth on the counter, a day past being made and good still, and there was something Helen had kept warm that had been the same thing the night before and would be the same thing, Claire understood without being told, the night after. It was the food the week had. They ate it without saying much about it. Bran ate the way a man eats who has worked, head down, steady, and was finished before either of them and sat with his hands flat on the table not waiting for anyone, just done.

It was settled without being decided that Claire would stay. The dark was already all the way down by the time the table was cleared, and the walk back was the long walk, and Helen did not offer the cart a second time, and Claire did not bring it up, and that was the staying arranged. Helen made up the small room off the back of the house that had a bed in it and a chair and the boxes that did not fit in the worktable room. She put a second blanket on the bed without being asked and said the cold came up in there worse than the rest of the house and Claire said all right.

The house went down into night the way a house does, in stages, the small sounds of it stopping one at a time. Claire lay in the made-up bed under the two blankets and did not sleep. She had not expected to. The cold came up the way Helen had said it would, from the floor first and then the corners, and lay along the outside of the blankets, and Claire lay inside them and was awake in the way she was awake at this hour, which was the most herself she was in a day now, the dark being the part of it that fit her.

Out the small window the yard was not entirely dark. The pale light was on in the greenhouses, the low light Helen kept through the long nights for the temperature work, and it came across the packed dirt of the yard and reached the house and put a thin grey shape of the window on the wall opposite the bed. Claire watched it for a while. It did not move the way the day's light moved. It was a made light and it stayed where it was put.

She got up.

She did not get up to go anywhere in particular. She wanted water, was the nearest thing to a reason, and the kitchen was where the water was, and so she went toward the kitchen, in the dark, in her socks, with her hand along the wall of the short passage that ran from the back room past the other door to the front of the house. The other door was the door to the room Helen and Bran were in. It stood part open. Claire would have gone past it the way she went past anything, without quite stopping, except that she heard the breathing change.

She stopped in the passage with her hand flat on the wall.

Bran's breathing had had, since the house went quiet, the long even rhythm of a man asleep after work, the breath going down into him and coming back up and asking nothing. Now it changed. It did not break. It did not catch the way a breath catches when a man is surfacing toward waking. It evened further, and as it evened it took on a thing the sleeping breath had not had, that Claire heard before she had any word to put to it. It aimed. The breath went out of him and it went out toward something. A sleeping breath goes nowhere. This one had a direction in it, and the direction was Helen.

By the grey light off the yard Claire could see the both of them. Helen was on the near side, and she was awake. Claire knew it the way you know it across a room — the stillness was the wrong kind for sleep, a stillness being kept and not a stillness fallen into. Helen lay on her back with her eyes open and her hands at her sides on the cover, and she was not surprised, and she was waiting the way a person waits who has done the waiting many times and does not expect it to be long.

Bran's arm came up off the cover.

It crossed the space between them and it did not search. She watched it not search. A sleeping arm that reaches gropes — it goes out vague and open-fingered and lands where it lands and the man it belongs to never knows. This arm did not grope. It went across the dark the way a hand goes to a thing the eye is already on, level, and certain of the distance, and it arrived at Helen's near hand where it lay on the cover, and it arrived there because it had known where she was. The breath had known. And his face, turned a little on the pillow toward her, had not changed at all. It was the face of a man entirely asleep — slack, the jaw gone down, the eyes moving somewhere under the lids after a thing that was not in the room — and below that gone face the arm was doing this without him, doing it with some part of him that had got up and crossed the dark while the rest of him stayed sleeping, a part that could see in a room where the man could not.

The hand came down over hers. And then it opened. The fingers loosened off and the palm turned up under her palm and lay there, open, offered, and it asked nothing in any way a waking hand could ask, because it had no words in it and no waking in it, and it asked anyway, the only way it had, by lying open against her and waiting. It held open and it waited the way the breath had waited, for the one thing that would answer it, which was for her to turn her own hand over and lay her palm down into his.

Helen did not turn her hand.

She lay a moment with his open hand on hers and her face by the grey light gave away nothing Claire could read. Then her hands moved, both of them, the way they moved at the trays — without waste, without hurry, knowing the thing they were for. She took his hand in both of hers, not the way you take a hand that is offered but the way you take a thing that has been set down where it should not be, and she lifted it up off her own and carried it back across the space it had come, and she laid it down on his chest over his heart where it rose and fell with him, and she let it go. The palm closed as it came to rest. The offer shut. It had cost her, from the outside, no more than it costs to lift a sleeping child's arm off you in the night so the two of you can go on lying there — and she had done it like that, exactly like that, like a thing done so many nights it no longer woke her to do it.

Bran's breath let go of its aim. It spread back out flat into the rhythm of sleep and went nowhere again. The arm lay on his chest and did not move. Helen lay back down the small distance she had risen and put her hands at her sides where they had been, and was still, and the room was a room again, and the made light from the greenhouses lay across the floor of it and did not move.

Claire stood in the passage.

She had begun, somewhere she could not have found, to count. She did not know what she was counting. The seconds the hand had stayed open, maybe — there had been a number of them and she had it, and it was a number that needed no keeping and that she had kept. Her own hand was flat on the wall where she had put it when she stopped, and she felt it there now, the cold of the wall come into the palm of it, and her attention went to it, and to the open hand it had just watched, and to the small carrying-away that had answered the open hand, and from the carrying-away to the windowsill in Helen's kitchen, the four of them in front of the house she did not remember, the two who were the age they had been and no older — and it got that far and no further. It went toward the thing the way you go toward a doorway, and at the doorway it turned. She did not go in. She did not bring it back.

She did not get the water.

She went back along the passage with her hand on the wall, into the small cold room where the second blanket was, and she did not lie down. She stood by the bed. The thin grey window-shape was still on the wall opposite. Out beyond it the pale light held in the greenhouses, the light Helen made against the long dark and kept burning through it, the only light in the yard, and under it the things in the trays were doing in the night what they did, slowly, in conditions that were not enough and that Helen worked to make into enough, and the night had a great deal of itself left to go.
