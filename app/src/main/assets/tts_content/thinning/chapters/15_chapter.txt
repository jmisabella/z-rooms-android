_Chapter 15_

Helen was at the far end of the house, down in the low light, at the temperature work — the thing she did to hold the warm against the cold through the long middle of the night. Claire sat at the near end of the bench with the enameled cup gone cool between her hands and had not drunk from it. The warm of the house had got into her a while back, the face and then the back of the neck and then the hands, and she had let that go unmarked, so that the one thing left for her to keep was the cold where it came in low under the door against the side of her foot, the single cold thing the made warm had not reached. She kept it closely. She kept things closely now.

The light down the house was the made light, the low pale light Helen kept burning through the long nights, set where it was set and staying there — not the day's light, that came up the glass and held its angle a while and went, but a made thing, that lay where Helen had put it along the staging and the near beds and the length of the floor and did not move and would not. Past where it reached, at the bright end by the door, the glass was black, and behind the black was the yard, and behind the yard the other dark, and the cold was on the far side of all of it, all the way up, pressing on the panes, not coming but already come, standing the whole length of the night and waiting on a gap to come in by. Claire's attention sat in the made light without effort. It was a light a person could keep hold of — it stayed where it was put and asked nothing of the keeping — and the cold stood off past the end of it.

She had come down in the dark, at the hour she was up for, and the thermos had been out on the bench when she came in and the cup beside it, the same enameled cup, set out — except that it was the night now, the deep of it, and not the morning, and Helen had set it for the night because the night was when Claire came, and had said nothing about the setting of it or the hour.

"It'll hold tonight," Helen said, down the house, not stopping the work. "It's a hard cold but it came on slow, and a slow one you can get the warm up under and hold. It's the ones that drop all at once that take things, before you're ahead of them. This one'll sit where it is till the turn." She did the thing she did to the near heater, and the glow of it changed in the low light and held. "Bran's in. Asleep this long while — he's the near work in the morning and takes his sleep when the rest of us are up, which is the most of why I do the night on my own, and have done. The thing at the lower place is holding. He says it's holding."

"He said that the last time."

"He did. And it held a while and then it didn't, and he'll go again. But it's holding now, and he's in, and asleep, and that's tonight."

She went on down the house to the far heater. Claire heard her at it — the small sounds of the tending of it — and looked down the house for her. Helen was past where the made light got to, a shape against the glow, bent to the work and then straightening the small distance she straightened, and she put the back of her wrist to her hair; there'd have been soil on her hands. The dark had her face. The glow at that end changed and held the way the near one had, and the voice came back up the house full and sure when she said a thing about the cold; and Claire's looking went down after the voice and got as far as the shape and the work the shape was doing, and the dark past the light had the rest of her, and she did not go down into it after her.

The heaters cut in against the cold at their own rate through the night, the near one and the far, and she had the count of the answerings without having begun it. It was not the count of the wood giving as it took the cold, the way the wood had given in her own rooms through the dark — that had been the cold taking and the wood yielding to the taking, a thing given up by the ones. This was the made thing answering, cutting in against the cold when the cold came on a degree, holding the line that Helen held; and the count came up by ones, each one whole and plain and arriving in her, the near and the far, while the rest of her sat in the warm and let the cup go the rest of the way cold in her hands.

"You're warm through," Helen said. It was not a question. The cold was held off her where she stood at the near end, and she said the thing she said about the going-back. "You'll want to start before the turn. The cold lifts a little when the night turns and that's the time to be on the road in, before the lift goes off it and the morning cold comes up behind it, which is a worse cold than the night's for all it's the morning's. Drink that if you're going to. It's stone cold but it's wet."

"In a minute," Claire said.

"All right."

She went back down the house. The made light held where it was put. The heaters cut in against the cold, the near one and then the far, and the count of the answerings came up by the ones, each one whole and plain and arriving in her, the made things holding the line that Helen held; and her sister was a voice down the house, full and sure, saying the true things she said about the cold and the turn, and a shape at the work past where the light got to. Claire kept the count. She did not start back, and the warm held.
