_Chapter 19_

Helen had the made light on over the bench and was sowing the year's saved seed into the flats, and Claire sat at the near end of the staging with the enameled cup gone cool in her hands and watched the flats go across. The day past the glass was a poor one and short — a low grey that came up late and never got bright, that held a while at its grey and would go early — and it was no light to do the fine work by; so Helen had the made light run along above the bench and full on the work, the low pale light she kept for the long nights, brought round to the day because the day would give her nothing the seed wanted. Under it the empty flats stood in their row, the dark medium broken fine and struck off level in the cells, and she set the seed down into them as she went, and the sown cells went across behind her hand, and a flat filled and she set it by and drew an empty one up.

It was the same made light that lay along the house through the nights, that stayed where it was put and asked nothing of the keeping; only she had it on by day now, close over the bench, because the day past the glass did not come to much and gave the white fronts of the houses below no shadow and the seed no seeing. The heaters were on under it. The warm of the house had got into Claire a while back, the face and then the neck and then the hands, and she had let it go unmarked, so that the one thing left for her to keep was the cold — the cold that stood off past the made light at the dark end of the house, over the bed Helen had cleared at the year's end, the bare run of it still open and broken fine and waiting on the year, with the cold standing the length of it where the made light did not reach.

"It's early for it," Helen said. She did not stop the work. "You'd not start them this far down the year if you had the choosing of it. There's no light yet to bring them on and won't be for weeks. But the light comes up so late now and goes so soon that by the time it's worth the starting the year's half gone from you, and you've nothing up and ready when it turns. So you steal the weeks off the front. You start them under the made light and they sit slow in the warm till the day's worth something, and then they're away ahead of where they'd have been." She sowed down the flat. "It costs, running the light by day. But it's that or stand the whole year behind, and behind's the worse of the two."

The seed was the seed she had saved off the year — Claire had watched her stand the fruit up for it on this same bench in the autumn, the soundest of a poor picking put by out of her own eating — and it had dried down to a dish of it at Helen's left hand, dark and small and dry, and she took from it as she came to need it, her hand knowing the place of it without her looking.

It was the closest work she did, the sowing, and the made light was full on it — full on her hands an arm's length off where they went over the flat — and of all her work it was the work that most asked to be watched: the lifting of the one seed off the dish, and the setting of it down into the cell, and the small press of the fingertip after to seat it, the whole of it in the ends of her fingers and nowhere else. And the light had it, and Claire had it there before her, close. And the hands did not come. What came was the cell sown and the next sown, the flat going across, the dish going down at her hand; and the shape of her bent over the bench in the made light; and the voice, when she said a thing about the seed, full and plain and sure of it.

"Bran ran the line out for the light," Helen said. "Along to the bench, so I'd have it down close over the work and not be carrying the lamp and setting it and shifting it every flat. He's the evenings for that kind of thing now, being home. He did it one night last week and never said — I came down and there it was." She set seed down the cells. "So he's home, and the light's run out to the bench, and I sow by it."

The dish came down at her left hand, the saved seed in it dark and dry, and she took from it without looking; and the sown flats stood by in their row in the made light, the cells level and dark and the seed down in them where it would lie through the cold weeks with nothing showing, waiting on a light that was not in the year yet; and the made light lay full and even on all of it, the dish and the broken medium and the flats going by, close and staying where it was put. The warm of the house held around it. The cold stood off at the dark end over the cleared bed and did not come in.

She worked the seed along the bench toward the near end as she filled the flats, the dish coming with her, a foot nearer with each one she finished, the way she came up a row; and she got within the arm's length of Claire and then nearer than that, near enough that the small dry sound of each seed going down and being pressed into its cell came up under the voice — and the hands that made the sound, lit the whole length of them now, did the work and did not come.

"They'll show in their own time and not before," Helen said. "Three weeks, four, if the warm holds them. There's no hurrying seed — you give it the warm and the wet and the made light, and that's the whole of what's yours to give it, and the rest is the seed's own and the cold's. I'll know by the turn of the next month which of the flats took." She drew the last empty flat up. "Most years you'd sow more than this and have the choosing later of what you kept. I'd not the seed for more than this, off the year there was. So it's this, and I'll grow up what comes of it and no more, because there's nothing behind it to grow."

The dish came down to the last of the seed and then there was none of it, the dark of the medium showing through at the bottom where the seed had lain, and Helen sowed the last few down the cells of the last flat and there were cells left over at the end of it unsown, and she did not fill them, because there was nothing to fill them with. The count had run out with the seed. It had nowhere left to go and it stopped, and Claire did not look for the number it had got to. Helen set the last flat by with the others in the row, the sown cells and the few bare ones at the end of it, and the made light lay full and even along the whole row of them, the flats standing in it with the seed down in the dark of the cells where it would lie through the weeks with nothing showing. The cold stood off at the dark end of the house over the cleared bed and did not come in. Claire sat on at the near end of the staging with the cold cup in her hands, and her sister down the bench was the shape bent over the last of it and the voice, full and sure, and the seed was in.
