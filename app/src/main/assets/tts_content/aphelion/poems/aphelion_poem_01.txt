By the Design

The phrase was in our mouths before our teeth.
We learned it with the morning, with the light,
with everything the world provided freely—
before we thought to ask who gave that right.

By the design. We said it before eating.
We said it in the doorways, before thought—
the daily small inheritance of language,
passed down through all the lives the years had brought

back to the ones who first composed the phrase,
who pressed the meaning in with cold intention,
who knew exactly what they were constructing:
a truth that did not need to draw attention

to itself. That needed only use.
It needed only morning after morning,
the phrase before the meal, the young receiving
it as breath, as world—a gift requiring no warning,

no examination. We said the name for home.
We said it to the meal, the light, the dead.
We gave it to our children with the day,
and did not look inside the word we said.

The word meant farthest distance. Meant the cold
extreme, the absolute remove, the turn
from which the ones who named it had intended
to come back.

Not locked—no door was shut, no question barred.
The shape of what could be asked had been made small
across the generations, slow, unmarred

by any single act of suppression.

Truth does not need concealment. It needs only
the cover of the commonplace, the veil
of repetition thick enough to muffle
the meaning into something worn and pale

and daily. Shapeless with the years of saying.
It waits inside the word you say like breath,
before you are awake enough to hear it—
below the morning, patient underneath.

It was already in your mouth this morning.
It has been there for years.
