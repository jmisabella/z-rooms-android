CHAPTER THREE


A signal had been received from outside the ship eleven months ago. Classified immediately. In the eleven days that followed, quiet adjustments across multiple domains of the ship's administration. She had spent those eleven months working in the archive where those traces lived, and she had not known they were there.


--


In the Stacks, Cassian was already at his workspace — the third consecutive day of the Continuance audit, which evidently ran longer than she had assumed it would. She registered his presence without comment, set Davan on the intake queue, and descended to the deep archive.

She had decided in the night what she needed to look for.

The classification code class was not in the reference schema she maintained. If she could identify where the code class originated — what category it belonged to, what it was designed to mark — she would know more about the nature of the absent record than the record itself would have told her. A code was a label. Labels had origins. And the Stacks held everything, or nearly everything, because the Stacks were redundant by design.

The foundational classification schema went back eleven generations. She read it now in the sections most likely to contain an expansion notation — the administrative revisions, the code-class additions, the amendments that had accumulated as the ship's bureaucratic needs evolved over time — with the patience of someone who trusted that the answer was present if she followed the thread.

She found it in an annotation layer from several generations back.

The code class belonged to a category of records concerning external communications intercepts.

She read it twice. Then she read the annotation language around it, which was dry administrative prose, entirely unremarkable, the language of a bureaucratic system expanding to include a new function. It did not say anything dramatic. It said that this code class was established to handle records involving transmissions received from outside the ship, and that such records would be administered under Continuance retention protocols.

She pulled the five documents again and read them with this as the frame.

The demographic record — population projections, resource modeling — contained the phrase terminal phase parameters in its methodology section. She had read it as standard modeling language, an end-date convention. She read it again now. Terminal phase. The projection had been built with a terminus in its calculations — a point at which the model resolved into a final state. The kind of endpoint you modeled for if you had information about an actual endpoint.

The medical inventory abstract contained an authorization for increased stores. The kind of increase you prepared for if you were anticipating conditions that did not currently exist — a period of elevated demand, a disruption to normal function, something the projections told you was coming.

She did not pursue the interpretation further than the evidence warranted. The evidence warranted this much: eleven months ago, something from outside the ship reached them. The Continuance classified it instantly. In the eleven days that followed, five documents across five separate domains were filed, all pointing back to that absent center. The documents showed quiet adjustments — projections revised, supplies increased, models updated with new parameters.

There was a destination. The signal had told them something about the destination. And the Continuance had known for eleven months, and the Common still said by the design every morning, and the clinic still attributed disrupted sleep to cycle irregularity, and no one had been told.


--


Cassian came down from the upper level in the mid-afternoon, the physical records audit apparently at a pause. He set something on the side table, glanced over the main floor. "How is the work?" he said.

"Steady." She was sealing the deep archive layer back into its housing, which carried no visible significance.

He stood for a moment. Then: "You know that some of the older Continuance code classes aren't in the standard reference schema." He said it lightly. "If you come across something outside your schema during routine review, it's worth routing through me before you spend time on it. Those old classes tend to generate administrative questions that need seniority clearance to resolve cleanly."

She looked at him. "I'll keep that in mind."

He nodded. Said something brief about the audit timeline. Went back up.

She stayed where she was until she was certain her face was doing nothing.

He might have come down with that note for any number of reasons. It was entirely possible that this was exactly that — a senior archivist noting something relevant before she spent unnecessary time on a dead end. Warm, collegial, appropriate.

The timing was not something she could account for with certainty.

She finished the remaining work and closed the systems and went to find her mother.


--


Lisel was moving when Calla arrived — had been moving, by the look of it. There was a quality of contained energy in her that settled when she saw who it was, and she opened the door the rest of the way and did not say you look tired, which she always said. She poured water instead and let the small heating element work.

"Four patients this week," she said, once they were sitting. She held the cup in both hands. "Similar complaints. Disturbed sleep. Difficulty holding concentration. Two of them reported visual disturbances — not dramatic, peripheral. Lights in the wrong place. Shadows." She used the medical worker's careful language, the precision of someone who had not yet decided what they were describing. "There was a communication from administration some months ago. Sleep cycle irregularity."

"Is that what you think it is?"

Lisel looked at the cup. "I've seen cycle disruption produce strange effects. It's a reasonable explanation." She was quiet for a moment. "Four in one week is not unusual in itself."

"No," Calla said.

They sat. The heating element clicked off. Outside in the corridor someone passed, footsteps receding toward the far end of the tier.

"You have something on your mind," Lisel said. Not a question.

"A discrepancy in the archive." She said it plainly, which was mostly true, and which was all she was prepared to say.

Her mother held the look for a moment — that measured maternal attention, the one Calla had never been entirely successful at deflecting — and then released it. There would be other opportunities.

"Come again soon," she said, when Calla rose to leave.

"In a couple of days."


--


At home she sat in the quiet and held what she knew. She would go back down tomorrow.
