
Chapter 1 — Prya  
  
He had set the lighting to its second configuration four hours ago, when Prya’s responses shifted into a cadence he recognized — not the early halting of Phase One, not the flat procedural processing that came with the first hours of a new sensorium, but a more settled quality that meant she was beginning to inhabit the configuration rather than report on it. He had adjusted the overhead fifteen degrees warmer. He had brought the ambient level up slightly, to approximate afternoon. Small decisions, procedural, the kind that wouldn’t appear in any log because they weren’t protocol — they were craft, accumulated across eight hundred calibrations, and they were the difference between a transfer that found the new sensorium bearable and one that arrived at it already exhausted.  
  
Prya was twenty, approximately. A singer before her Transition, though her file said musician and did not specify. She had chosen a standard multipurpose configuration, which was common for transfers her age — the ones who hadn’t yet settled into the professional specialization that made a narrower configuration worth the trade-offs. She had been talking, for the last several minutes, about what her new hearing was like.  
  
Iven noted the description. It was good — specific, reaching, the kind of observation that suggested the integration was proceeding well. Nineteen hours in. The lighting was right. Prya’s voice through the interface had taken on, over the last three hours, a quality that was recognizably her own rather than the flatter cadence that characterized the early processing.  
  
She stopped.  
  
Not a pause. The difference was legible, after thirty-six years, in the first half-second: a pause had a shape, an intention to resume, and the transfer’s attention stayed in the room. This was not that. Prya went still. The interface registered the cessation of output, and then there was only the silence that Iven had built the room for — the absence of ventilation, the drive vibration that lived below hearing, and the faint electrical hum of the panel between them.  
  
Iven sat. He did not adjust the lighting. He did not speak. There was nothing to adjust and nothing to say. The transfer had oriented toward something inside the new sensorium that Iven could not perceive and would not be able to perceive, because it was not a sound or a sight or a piece of data but a kind of threshold — the specific moment where the new consciousness met a category that had belonged to the old one and could not locate it. He had watched it happen, in this room or rooms like it, more than eight hundred times. It lasted, on average, between seven and twenty minutes. It was in every file. It appeared in every report in the same standard language: brief orientation event, hour fourteen to twenty-two, no intervention required.  
  
He sat and he watched the panel and he waited.  
  
The room did what it was designed to do, which was: nothing. The silence held. The lighting held. The air, unstirred by ventilation, held the faint warmth of two bodies in a small space, though one of those bodies was no longer strictly a body — it was a configuration, a new architecture of consciousness housed in hardware that did not breathe or radiate heat but that the room held anyway, because the room did not distinguish. Iven had sometimes thought, during these silences, about what the transfer was orienting toward — what the threshold felt like from the inside, whether it was a wall or a gap or something the old vocabulary had no category for. He did not think about it now. He had learned, years ago, that the thinking was not useful. The waiting was the work.  
  
Eleven minutes. He did not count them on the display. He counted them in the quality of his own stillness — the way his breathing settled, the way the room’s silence became a pressure and then leveled into something he could hold. His hands were still on the armrests of the chair. His back was straight. He had not shifted his weight.  
  
She came back.  
  
It was visible before the interface registered output: a shift in the pattern, something resuming that had been suspended, and then Prya’s voice again, slightly different — not damaged, not diminished, but lighter, as if one thing that had been present before was not present now.  
  
“I’m sorry,” she said. “I was trying to — there was a word. For what the layering sounds like. I was reaching for it and it was right there, and then I wasn’t reaching anymore, and now I can’t remember which word it was.” A pause. “I’m sorry.”  
  
“What you’re noticing is normal,” Iven said. His voice was steady and warm, the register he used in these moments, which was not performed — it was the voice he would use to say it to anyone, because he meant it. “The orientation you experienced is part of the integration process. Most transfers report something similar around this stage. It doesn’t indicate a problem.”  
  
“Okay,” she said. She was quiet for a moment. “It felt like it should have been a word I knew.”  
  
“Yes,” Iven said. “That’s also normal.”  
  
The calibration proceeded. Prya’s responses in the remaining hours were good — measured, increasingly fluent, the new sensorium becoming something she spoke from rather than something she spoke about. Iven adjusted the lighting twice more, small shifts, tracking her cadence. He filed his notes at standard intervals. Around hour twenty-three she made a joke about the silence in the room and he smiled, because the timing was hers, still hers, and the joke worked. By hour twenty-six she was asking about the operational schedule and when she would be cleared for full integration with the cohort. He told her. She thanked him.  
  
The calibration closed at hour twenty-eight. Prya’s handoff to her operational supervisor was clean. The Completion Report took Iven thirty minutes and he filed it before end of shift. Everything in the standard language. Brief orientation event, hour nineteen, eleven minutes, no intervention required. Transfer expressed temporary difficulty locating a specific term during Phase Two integration. Within normal parameters.  
  
He closed the file. He sat in Calibration Room Six.  
  
The room was empty now. The interface panel had powered down, the lighting returned to its neutral default. The chair across from Iven was the same chair that had been there for every calibration he had conducted in this room, and it held nothing of the eight hundred and fourteen people who had sat in it — not their words, not the specific quality of their voices arriving through the interface for the first time, not the silences. The room was clean and empty and standard.  
  
Iven sat. He did not think about Prya, exactly. He did not think about anything, exactly. He sat in the room where the calibration had been and he let the silence be a different silence than the one he had built for the work — not a professional instrument now, just: the quiet of a room with one person in it who had finished something and was not yet ready to stand.  
  
Ten minutes. Then he stood. He turned off the overhead. He left.  
  
  
—————  
  
  
The corridor from the calibration wing to the residential sector was four hundred meters, more or less, depending on which junction you took at the service hub. Iven took the same route every evening. The air changed three times along it: the clean processed quality of the calibration wing giving way to the common-area circulation — slightly warmer, carrying an undernote that shifted depending on the reclamation cycle — and then, in the mid-section, a stretch of maybe thirty meters where the temperature dropped. A different air loop. Not a malfunction, just an artifact of the ship’s thermal architecture that no one had prioritized correcting. Regular walkers knew it. Iven passed through it the way he passed through it every evening, registering the cold on his face and hands without altering his pace.  
  
The ventilation was at its evening frequency — a subtly lower register than the daytime cycle, something most people did not notice until it was pointed out to them and then could not stop hearing. Iven had stopped noticing and started noticing again so many times across thirty-six years that the sound had become a kind of clock, not of the hour but of how long he had been aboard. He walked. The corridor’s evening lighting had shifted to its cooler spectrum, the overhead panels dimming in the slow gradient the ship used to simulate dusk, though there was no horizon to darken and no sun to set and the effect, after decades, registered less as an imitation of anything planetary and more as the ship’s own particular way of saying: the day is finished.  
  
Two people passed him going the other direction — a couple, speaking quietly, carrying the comfortable cadence of a conversation that had been going on for a while and would continue after he was out of earshot. He did not look at them. He was not thinking about anything in particular. He was walking home, the way he walked home after every calibration, and the corridor was the corridor, and the evening was the evening, and the work was behind him.  
  
He reached the residential sector. The door to the apartment was closed. When he opened it, the air that met him was different — not the processed common-area circulation but the smaller, warmer atmosphere of two rooms and a kitchen area that had been holding the smell of food for perhaps an hour. The stew. He could identify it before he was fully through the door: the specific combination of reconstituted protein and the herbs Hanne grew in her section, the thyme and the dried fennel she had been working with this rotation.  
  
Hanne was at the stove. She turned when he came in, not all the way, just enough to register him.  
  
“Long one?” she said.  
  
“Twenty-eight hours.” He set his bag on the chair by the door, the way he always did, and went to wash his hands. “Good transfer. A young woman, a singer.”  
  
“Did she sing for you?”  
  
“No. She described what her new hearing sounded like. She was good at it.”  
  
Hanne made a sound that was not quite a laugh — an acknowledgment, warm, carrying the specific quality of her attention landing on something she found interesting without needing to pursue it further. She served the stew. They sat at the table across from each other, the garden journal between them, closed, the ceramic bowl in front of Hanne — slightly lopsided, made twelve years ago during the brief period when she had been interested in ceramics, outlasting the interest by a decade and becoming, through use, so specifically hers that Iven could not imagine the table without it.  
  
They ate. The stew was the same stew it always was, keyed to the harvest rotation in Sector Four — every eight days, when the leaf crops came in. Hanne had been making it for longer than Iven could specifically remember, long enough that the taste had stopped being a thing he noticed and become instead a kind of temporal marker, the way the ventilation frequencies marked evening or the cold section marked the midpoint of the corridor. He did not count how many remained.  
  
“The scheduling office confirmed today,” Hanne said. She said it the way she said most things: directly, without preamble, in the middle of the meal rather than before or after it. “Eleven weeks. They’ve assigned the date.”  
  
Iven set his spoon down. Not dramatically. He set it down because he had heard what she said and his hand needed to not be doing something else.  
  
“I’ve requested you,” she said. “As my Calibration Technician. They approved it.”  
  
There was a specific silence in the apartment that was different from the silence in a calibration room and different from the silence of the corridor. It was the silence of two people who had been in the same rooms for thirty-four years and who knew what the other was about to say and who waited, sometimes, not because the answer was uncertain but because the moment before the answer was a kind of room itself, and they were both in it.  
  
“Yes,” Iven said.  
  
Hanne nodded. She picked up her spoon. She ate another bite of the stew. After a moment she said, “The basil is doing something interesting this week. The third-generation graft took in a way I didn’t expect. I want to see what it does before —“ She stopped, not because she could not finish the sentence but because she had decided, somewhere in the middle of it, that she did not need to. She took another bite. “It’s good,” she said, meaning the basil or the stew or something else entirely.  
  
They finished dinner. Hanne washed the bowls. Iven dried them and put them in the cabinet, except for the ceramic one, which went back on the table because that was where it lived. The lighting fixture in the ceiling above them flickered once, the same small flicker it had produced for four years, and neither of them looked up.  
  
Later, in the dark, Iven lay on his back and listened to the ventilation system shift into its nighttime frequency. The transition took eleven minutes — he had never timed it, but he knew, the way you know the length of a silence in a calibration room without checking the display. Beside him Hanne’s breathing was steady, already sleep, the specific rhythm he had been hearing for thirty-four years and could have identified in a room of a hundred strangers breathing.  
  
Eleven weeks.  
  
He lay still. The ventilation settled into its lower register. The viewport above the bed showed the inner hull — not stars, because their apartment faced inward, but the gentle slow progression of the ship’s running lights at their nighttime rate, which was slower and dimmer and which Hanne had once said she preferred to stars, because stars did not know you were looking at them but the ship’s lights were made for exactly this: for someone lying in the dark, watching.  
  
He did not sleep for a long time.
