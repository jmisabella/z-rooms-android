Chapter 5 — The Cold Section and Rall  
  
The response arrived the next morning.  
  
Hanne had spent the previous evening copying out the basil section of the garden journal — not the whole section, she said, just the cultivar work, the fourteen years of grafting notes, the records she had kept of each cross and its outcome. She had worked at the terminal on the kitchen table with the journal open beside her, transcribing her handwriting into the Navigator-class messaging format, which required plain text, no diagrams, no margin annotations. The format did not accommodate the small drawings she had made in the journal’s margins — the leaf shapes, the branching patterns she tracked from generation to generation — and Iven had watched her decide, for each one, whether to describe it in words or to leave it out. She had worked for two hours. She had not asked for help.  
  
Iven had gone to bed before she finished. In the morning, when he came to the table, she was already up, eating, the terminal dark beside her. She had sent it, she said. Last night, after he went to sleep.  
  
He poured himself water and sat down and ate. Midway through the meal the terminal chimed once — the specific low tone of the Navigator-class communication queue, which was distinct from the sector scheduling notifications and the maintenance alerts and every other sound the terminal made, and which Iven had heard exactly once before, in this apartment, six weeks ago, when Teo’s first response had arrived.  
  
Hanne set down her spoon and turned the terminal toward her. The gesture was unhurried. She opened the message. Iven watched her read it.  
  
*Received. The cultivar documentation will be preserved in accessible format. Thank you for sending it.*  
  
She read it once. She did not read it again. She turned the terminal back to its idle position and picked up her spoon.  
  
“Good,” she said.  
  
She continued eating. Iven watched her across the table — her face, her hands, the steady motion of the spoon. There was nothing visible in her expression that he could identify as disappointment or relief or the careful neutrality of someone who had received precisely what she expected. She was eating her breakfast. She was a woman who had sent something to her son and received an answer and was now finishing her meal.  
  
He understood, watching her, that the reunion had never been what she was reaching for. Not the first message, with its careful disclosure of the diagnosis and the Navigator-class request. Not this one, with its two hours of transcription, the decisions about which drawings to describe and which to leave out, the work of translating her handwriting into a format the system could carry. The reach was the thing. The motion of sending was complete in itself — the same motion she brought to the basil row and the garden journal and the cooking and the way she placed her shoes by the door, not lined up exactly but close together. Teo’s response confirmed only that the reach had arrived. It did not need to bring anything back.  
  
Hanne finished her breakfast. She rinsed her bowl and set it beside the ceramic one on the counter and went to the door and put on her shoes and left for Sector Four. Iven sat at the table for a few minutes after she left, in the apartment that held the specific quiet of a room someone had just departed — the same ordinary quiet it held every morning — and then he cleared his own bowl and went to work.  
  
—————  
  
He went to see Rall three days later, on the rest-day afternoon, carrying the book.  
  
The book did not need repair. It was the old translation, the one he had been rereading for most of his adult life, and the binding was sound — Rall had rebound it himself perhaps eight years ago, replacing the original adhesive spine with a stitched one, and the work had held. Iven carried it anyway. He had always carried it when he went to Rall’s, the way one might bring a small object to someone’s home that justified the visit by giving the hands something to hold.  
  
Rall’s shop was in the service corridor behind Sector Two, a narrow workspace that had originally been assigned as auxiliary storage and had been converted, with permission that Rall described as tacit, sometime in his first decade aboard. The space was perhaps four meters by three. He had built shelves along two walls from surplus structural composite, stained with something that gave them the approximate warmth of wood, though no one aboard had seen actual wood in their working lives. The shelves held books in various states of disassembly: spines detached, pages fanned and weighted for flattening, covers laid out with their linings peeled back for re-adhesion. There was a worktable under the room’s single lighting panel, which Rall had supplemented with a secondary lamp of his own construction that cast a close, warm circle on the work surface. The air in the shop was different from the rest of the ship — substrate adhesive, paper dust, the faint chemical note of the binding solutions Rall mixed from maintenance supplies and whatever materials Sector Three’s fabrication lab could be persuaded to spare.  
  
Rall was at the worktable when Iven arrived, working on a technical manual whose cover had separated cleanly from the text block. He looked up and nodded and continued working. Iven sat in the chair by the door — the only chair, besides Rall’s stool — and set the book on his knee.  
  
They had known each other for twenty-three years. Rall had come aboard twelve years before Iven, had been the ship’s only book repair specialist for most of that time — a role that did not exist in the crew manifest but that everyone who owned a physical book understood. He was sixty-one. He had declined Transition twice, both times past the recommended window, and was now past the age at which the renewal protocols could guarantee a clean mapping. He had never explained why he declined, and Iven had never asked, and this was one of the things that made the friendship what it was.  
  
“The old translation,” Rall said, without looking up. He had recognized the book by its cover, or by the fact that it was the only book Iven ever brought.  
  
“It doesn’t need anything.”  
  
“I know.” Rall set down the tool he was using — a narrow blade for separating adhesive from paper — and examined the manual’s spine. “You’re here because you want to sit in a room that isn’t your apartment and isn’t the calibration wing.”  
  
Iven did not deny it. Rall picked up a different tool, something with a flat, flexible edge, and began working adhesive residue from the manual’s spine in small, patient strokes. The motion was practiced and exact, the kind of precision that came from decades of the same work done well, and Iven watched it the way he sometimes watched Hanne’s hands in the hydroponics bay in the early years of their marriage — the comfort of seeing someone do the thing they were specifically good at.  
  
For a while there was only the sound of the work — the quiet scraping, the occasional shift of weighted pages on the shelves, the underlying hum of the ship’s ventilation that was present everywhere and that you heard most clearly in small, quiet rooms. Iven turned the book over in his hands. The cover had taken on the specific softness of a thing held many times, the edges of the pages worn to a texture that was almost cloth.  
  
“I’ve been looking at some of the older calibrations,” Iven said. He said it to the book in his hands, not to Rall. “Going back through them. Records I hadn’t looked at carefully before.”  
  
Rall did not stop working. “Finding things.”  
  
“Things I should have seen.” A pause. “It’s hard.”  
  
Rall set the manual down and looked at Iven. His hands were still, resting on the edge of the worktable, stained faintly with adhesive at the fingertips. He had the quality of attention that people who work with small, precise things develop over time — not intense, not searching, just steady.  
  
“It usually is,” Rall said. “When you go back.”  
  
He did not say anything else about it. He picked up the blade again and returned to the spine, and the sentence sat in the room between them the way sentences sometimes did in Rall’s shop — placed, not expanded, left to hold whatever weight it carried without being asked to hold more. Iven did not know whether Rall meant calibrations specifically or something else or everything. He did not need to know. The sentence was enough.  
  
They talked about other things. Rall had a commission from one of the archive staff — a nineteenth-century astronomical reference that had been brought aboard by the original crew and had survived eighty years of circulated handling with the predictable results. He described the damage with the mild, absorbed interest he brought to everything in the shop: the foxing on the plate illustrations, the way the original binding thread had held in some signatures and failed in others, the specific challenge of sourcing paper that would age compatibly with the existing stock. Iven listened. He asked about the plates. Rall showed him one — a stellar cartography grid drawn by hand, the lines so fine they seemed to have been laid down by someone who believed precision was a form of devotion. They looked at it together for a moment without commenting on it.  
  
Rall made tea from the small heating element beside the worktable. Ship-issue blend, the same as everywhere, but the water from the shop’s auxiliary line had a slightly different mineral quality that Rall had pointed out once years ago and that Iven had never been able to untaste since. They drank the tea. Rall described a binding technique he had been adapting from a manual that was itself in need of repair, and the recursion of this pleased him in a quiet way. Iven sat in the chair by the door and held his tea and did not think about Hanne or Teo or the sought-word catalogue or any of the things that occupied the rest of his hours. He thought about paper and adhesive and the quality of the light in Rall’s shop and nothing else, and the nothing else was the point.  
  
When Iven stood to leave, he felt it — a lightness he had not anticipated and could not entirely account for. Something in the quality of having sat in a room where the work was visible and specific and patient and had nothing to do with him.  
  
Rall glanced at the book under Iven’s arm.  
  
“Let me see it,” he said.  
  
Iven handed it over. Rall opened it and examined the binding — the binding he had done himself, which was perfectly sound, which they both knew — and ran his thumb along the inner spine and turned a few pages and closed it and handed it back.  
  
“It’s fine,” he said.  
  
“I know.”  
  
“Come back when it isn’t.” A pause. “Come back before that.”  
  
Iven nodded. He left the shop and turned into the main corridor toward home, carrying the book that did not need repair and would not need repair for years, and the air changed from substrate adhesive and paper dust to the ship’s standard recycled atmosphere, and the corridor widened, and the lighting shifted from the close warm circle of Rall’s secondary lamp to the flat distributed panels of the residential stretch.  
  
—————  
  
The cold section was four minutes from his apartment. He had walked through it thousands of times in thirty-six years — the point in the corridor where the air loop from the environmental systems crossed a junction with a secondary circulation feed, and the temperature dropped perhaps two degrees, and the air took on a different quality, slightly drier, slightly sharper. It lasted for perhaps thirty meters. Everyone who walked the corridor regularly knew it was there. No one had ever filed a maintenance request because the cold was not a malfunction. It was a feature of the way the systems intersected, and it was known, and it was minor, and it was specific, and it had been there for longer than anyone currently aboard.  
  
Iven stopped.  
  
He had not planned to stop. He was walking at his usual pace, carrying the book, still holding the remnant of whatever had lightened in Rall’s shop, and he reached the cold section and felt the air change on his face and he stopped.  
  
He stood in the middle of the corridor. The cold touched his face and his hands and the skin at the back of his neck. He registered it as a specific small pleasure — a thing the ship did without intending to, a known imperfection that had become, over years of walking through it, a kind of landmark in the body. He had passed through it without slowing a thousand times. He had never stood in it.  
  
A woman going the other way — someone he did not recognize, different section, carrying a bag from the commissary — slowed as she passed him.  
  
“Are you all right?” she said. Quietly, the tone of someone who was asking out of courtesy rather than alarm.  
  
“Yes,” Iven said.  
  
She nodded and kept walking. Her footsteps receded down the corridor. Iven stood in the cold for a few more seconds. He was not reaching for any meaning in it. He was not connecting it to anything. The cold was the cold. It was on his face, and he was standing in it, and that was all.  
  
He walked on.  
  
—————  
  
The apartment was warm when he arrived — not in temperature, the ship kept everything regulated, but in the quality of Hanne being there. She was at the table with the garden journal open, writing in the slow deliberate hand she used for it, the pen moving in the searching way he had been watching for thirty-four years and had only recently learned to see. The ceramic bowl was beside her elbow, holding a few small leaves — the kind she sometimes brought home from Sector Four, not for any reason, just because she had been handling them and had carried them out without thinking about it.  
  
She looked up when he came in.  
  
“How is Rall?” she said.  
  
Iven set the book on the shelf and his bag on the chair by the door, in the order he always did.  
  
“The same,” he said.  
  
Hanne smiled. It was not a large smile, not directed at him exactly — more at the answer itself, as though the same were a thing she was glad to hear confirmed, a piece of the ship’s constancy that was still where she had last known it to be.  
  
She returned to the journal. Iven went to the kitchen area and poured a glass of water and stood drinking it, watching her write. The light fixture in the ceiling flickered once and settled back into its steady glow.  
  
He sat down across from her. The journal was between them on the table, and her pen was moving, and he watched the motion of it — the reaching quality, the lateral search, each word arriving as though she were finding it rather than producing it. He had watched this for most of his life without knowing what to call it, and he did not know what to call it now, only that it was the thing that made her writing specifically hers, and that in five weeks it would undergo a process he had administered eight hundred times and had never once been able to prevent from doing exactly what it did.  
  
“There’s the grain salad,” Hanne said, without looking up. “On the counter.”  
  
He served himself and sat back down. They ate and wrote and sat in the apartment together as the lighting shifted along its evening gradient, and outside the viewport the running lights moved at their steady nighttime rate, and the journal’s pages turned, and somewhere four minutes down the corridor the cold section held its two-degree difference in the air, and Iven knew it was there the way he knew the fixture would flicker and the stew would come again in three days and the weeks would pass and the thing he could see in Hanne’s handwriting would go, and he sat with her, and he did not say any of this, and the evening closed around them.
