Epilogue  
  
The lighting was still at Phase Three standard.  
  
Iven sat in the chair he always sat in — offset to the left, slightly behind the calibration chair, which was empty now. The interface housing was dark. He had turned off the ventilation at the start of Phase One that morning, as he always did, and had not turned it back on, and in the stillness the drive vibration was the only thing below the silence, and the drive vibration was below hearing, so the room was quiet in the way calibration rooms were quiet between sessions.  
  
He had completed the calibration forty minutes ago. A standard outcome. He had filed the Completion Report in the standard language, handed the transfer to the operational supervisor, and come back into the room, because he always came back into the room. The ten-minute accounting. The practice he had maintained for thirty-six years, in every room he had worked in, after every calibration — sitting alone in the emptied space, in the specific silence of a room that had held something and was now holding nothing.  
  
This was Room Four.  
  
Three weeks ago he had prepared this room for Hanne. Set the Phase One lighting to warm, the secondary panel raised four percent. Turned off the ventilation. Sat in this chair. Her consciousness had instantiated at 11:07, and the calibration had proceeded for fifty-eight hours and fourteen minutes, and the freeze had come at hour nineteen, and he had counted four hundred and eighty-seven heartbeats, and she had come back, and he had said the sentence, and the reaching had ended, and he had filed the report and turned off the lights and left. And the next morning the room had been what it was now: a room between sessions. Holding nothing from before.  
  
Today he had set the lights for a different transfer and a different calibration had proceeded and the reaching had ended and the sentence had been said and the room had held it the same way it held everything. The room did not know the difference between today’s calibration and Hanne’s. It did not distinguish between the sentence said to a stranger and the sentence said to his wife. The not-distinguishing was the condition of the rooms. Iven had built his entire practice on it — the rooms held what happened in them without carrying it, and the not-carrying was what made the work possible, because someone had to sit in the clean silence afterward and be the one who carried it out the door.  
  
He sat.  
  
He was not thinking about the transfer he had just completed. He was not thinking about Hanne. He was sitting in the silence, which was the same silence it had been after Prya and after Orell and after the archivist and after Hanne and after every calibration he had ever completed, and the silence held him the way it always had, and for ten minutes that was all there was — the room, the silence, and the man sitting in it.  
  
Ten minutes passed.  
  
He stood. He picked up his bag from where it rested against the wall. He turned off the lights.  
  
The room went dark. The calibration chair, the interface housing, the walls — the same dark the room held every night between sessions. Tomorrow the lights would come on and the ventilation would go off and someone would sit in the calibration chair and the reaching would begin and the reaching would end and the sentence would be said and the room would hold it and the room would let it go. That was what the rooms did. That was what they had always done.  
  
Iven stepped into the corridor. The door closed behind him.
