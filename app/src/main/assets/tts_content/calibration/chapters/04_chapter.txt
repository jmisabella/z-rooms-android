Chapter 4 — The Fourth Reading  
  
The man’s name was Orell, and he was fifty-three, and he had worked in secondary water reclamation for the better part of twenty-four years. He had chosen Transition on the standard timeline — the facility had matched him to a Residential-class configuration, and he had accepted it without requesting modifications, and his pre-Transition cognitive interview had been unremarkable in all the ways that made Iven’s work straightforward. He was responding well.  
  
Phase Two had been running for sixteen hours. Iven sat in the chair he always sat in, in Room Six, where the light had been preset to the warm-spectrum configuration he used for mid-stage work. Orell’s new consciousness was settling into its operational register with the steady, unhurried quality that Iven had learned, across eight hundred calibrations, to associate with a good outcome. The voice through the interface was calm. The orientation responses were clean. Every parameter Iven would note in the report was within range.  
  
He had opened Sael’s files eleven days ago. Not all at once — he had started with the sought-word catalogue, late one evening after Hanne had gone to bed, and had stopped after forty entries and closed the reader and sat for a while in the quiet of the apartment. The next evening he read another sixty. The evening after that he went through the comparison transcripts, all forty of them, one after another, until the kitchen terminal’s clock read past midnight and the lighting in the apartment had finished its full transition to the nighttime spectrum without his noticing.  
  
He had not discussed the files with Sael. They had passed each other twice in the corridor since that afternoon in Room Seven, and both times the exchange had been exactly what it would have been before — a brief nod, a word about scheduling, Sael carrying her book. She had not asked whether he had read the material. She would not ask.  
  
Now Iven was watching Orell move through the late hours of Phase Two, and the parameters were all clean, and the voice was steady, and everything about the calibration was proceeding well, and he was seeing something he had seen eight hundred times before.  
  
Hour eighteen passed. Orell described the difference between processed water and source water in a memory from his apprenticeship — a sensory distinction he had trained himself to detect by taste, years ago, a skill his new configuration would not need but that his pre-Transition cognition was still organized around. He was reaching for something specific about the memory. Not the fact of it but the quality — the way his tongue had learned to read the mineral variation, “the way that reading had been a form of attention that was,” he said, and then paused, and then said, “almost like listening."  
  
Iven noted the time. 18:41.  
  
The conversation continued. Orell talked about the reclamation bays, the sound of the secondary filters, a colleague who had retired. The voice was steady. The pauses were within range.  
  
At 19:07, Orell was describing what the filtration system sounded like when it cycled between loads — a specific low hum he said he’d always found —  
  
And then he stopped.  
  
The silence in Room Six was the particular silence Iven kept there — ventilation off, the interface’s ambient noise suppressed to its minimum threshold, so that the only sound in the room was whatever the transfer produced and whatever Iven produced and nothing else. In that silence, Orell’s stillness had a quality that Iven had registered, across thirty-six years, as a standard orientation pause. *Duration variable. Transfer remains responsive to direct query but does not initiate. Resolution spontaneous.* He had written those words, or words very like them, hundreds of times.  
  
He was watching it now and seeing the catalogue. Sael’s six hundred and eighty-two entries, indexed by hour and duration and by the word or concept the transfer had been reaching toward when the freeze began. The atlas of what Phase Two did to the motion of seeking — not to the knowledge, not to the vocabulary, but to the specific quality of reaching for something that was not yet in your hand.  
  
Orell was still. His new consciousness oriented toward something Iven could not perceive and would never be able to perceive, because whatever the transfer attended to in those seconds existed in a register that the calibration interface did not measure and the completion reports did not describe and the standard language did not have a notation for.  
  
Iven counted his own heartbeats. He did this during every freeze, had done it for years — not as a timing method, the interface logged precise durations, but as a way of remaining present in the silence without filling it. He had never thought about why he did it. He counted two hundred and sixty-three beats.  
  
Orell came back. There was a small shift in the voice — not a change, exactly, but a resettling, as though the consciousness had picked up a thread it had briefly set down.  
  
“I was trying to think of the word for —” he paused. “I don’t know what the word was. I’m sorry. That’s strange.”  
  
“What you’re noticing is normal,” Iven said. He said it the way he always said it: warm, steady, with the particular professional care that the sentence required. It was not a lie. It was normal. That was precisely the thing.  
  
The calibration continued. Orell moved through the remaining hours of Phase Two and into the early hours of Phase Three, and his voice settled into the configuration that the report would describe as operational integration, and by hour twenty-six his language had the clean efficient quality that meant the process had done what it was designed to do, and Iven completed the calibration at hour thirty-one and filed the handoff and wrote the report.  
  
He used the standard language. He wrote: *orientation pause, 19:07, duration 4.2 minutes, spontaneous resolution.* He wrote: *voice calibration stabilized within expected parameters by hour twenty-four.* He wrote the sentences he had written eight hundred times in the same forms with the same field headings and submitted the report and logged the completion.  
He used the standard language. He wrote: *orientation pause, 19:07, duration 4.2 minutes, spontaneous resolution.* He wrote: *voice calibration stabilized within expected parameters by hour twenty-four.* He wrote the sentences he had written eight hundred times in the same forms with the same field headings and submitted the report and logged the completion.  
  
Then he sat in the empty room. Six minutes. Ten minutes. The lights were still at the preset he had chosen for Orell, who was no longer here. The chair across from him, where no one sat, was the same chair. The silence, which was now the silence of a completed calibration rather than the silence of an active one, had a different weight, though the room did not know this and the interface did not measure it.  
  
He picked up his bag. He turned off the lights. He left.  
  
—————  
  
The corridor was quiet at this hour — past the second shift change, the ship settling into its overnight configuration. The lights along the ceiling ran at their reduced rate, the warm amber that Iven had walked through thousands of times on his way home from late calibrations. He passed through the cold section without stopping. The air on his face registered as it always did — a small specific change, a different loop, a colder note — and he kept walking.  
  
The apartment was dark except for the kitchen’s low nighttime illumination. Hanne’s shoes were by the door, placed together with the particular care she had always taken with them — not lined up exactly, but close together, as though they were keeping each other company. The ceramic bowl was on the table. The garden journal was beside it, closed. The light fixture in the ceiling was off, which meant Hanne had gone to bed at least an hour ago, because she always left it on until she went to the bedroom, and the fixture did not turn itself off.  
  
Iven set his bag on the chair by the door. He did not turn on the main light. He sat down at the small terminal in the kitchen and entered his personal credentials and navigated to a file he had first opened seven years ago and had opened three times since.  
  
Renn’s Completion Report on Teo Marek. *Transfer age 22. Pre-Transition occupation: ship-systems engineer, Level 3. Requested configuration: Navigator-class. Transition voluntary.*  
  
He had read it for the first time two days after Teo’s calibration was complete, when the report appeared in the family-access log as a standard notification. He had read it a second time three months later, in the evening, after a calibration of his own that had gone badly — a young woman whose Phase Two had extended past the normal window, who had required additional stabilization, who had eventually integrated but with a flatness in the voice that Iven had noted in the report and then thought about for several days afterward. He had read the report a third time a year ago, on the anniversary of Teo’s Transition, sitting at this same terminal while Hanne slept in the next room.  
  
Each time the document had said the same things.  
  
*Phase One initiated at 06:00, standard protocol. Transfer displayed high engagement during opening calibration sequence. Cognitive orientation responses within expected parameters. Transfer expressed particular interest in navigational sensorium capabilities, consistent with pre-Transition interview.*  
  
*Phase Two integration proceeded at above-average rate. Transfer maintained consistent verbal engagement through hours 8-16. Orientation pause noted at hour 17, duration 6.8 minutes, spontaneous resolution. Transfer reported brief difficulty locating a specific term related to spatial engineering concepts. Assured of normalcy. Integration resumed without incident.*  
  
*Voice calibration stabilized within expected parameters by hour 22. Transfer demonstrated full operational fluency by hour 28. Completion criteria met at hour 34.*  
  
Iven read the report. He read it the way he had always read it, from the beginning, following the standard sections in their standard order. Phase One. Phase Two. Phase Three. Completion. Handoff. Each section in the language the forms provided, each observation slotted into the notation the system required.  
  
*Orientation pause noted at hour 17, duration 6.8 minutes, spontaneous resolution.*  
  
He sat with this sentence for a long time.  
  
Six point eight minutes. Approximately four hundred and eight seconds. Teo — who had been twenty-two, who had wanted to see the ship at scales biology could not reach, who had asked his parents’ permission and received it and walked into the Transition facility on a morning in the third week of the rotation cycle — had gone still at hour seventeen of his Phase Two. He had oriented toward something the interface did not measure. He had stayed there for six point eight minutes. He had come back. He had been unable to find a word.  
  
And Renn — who was competent, who was steady, who had been Iven’s colleague for thirteen years — had written *orientation pause*. Had written *spontaneous resolution*. Had written the sentence that came next: *Transfer reported brief difficulty locating a specific term related to spatial engineering concepts. Assured of normalcy. Integration resumed without incident.*  
And Renn — who was competent, who was steady, who had been Iven’s colleague for thirteen years — had written *orientation pause*. Had written *spontaneous resolution*. Had written the sentence that came next: *Transfer reported brief difficulty locating a specific term related to spatial engineering concepts. Assured of normalcy. Integration resumed without incident.*  
  
*Assured of normalcy*. Iven recognized it — his own sentence, in the clinical register the forms required rather than the voice he used when he spoke it aloud. Renn had said it to Teo the way Iven had said it to Prya and to Orell and to every transfer who came back from the freeze not knowing what they had been reaching for.  
  
*Voice calibration stabilized within expected parameters by hour 22.*  
  
He read this sentence, too. He had read it three times before and understood it to mean that Teo’s new consciousness had settled into its operational register, that the Navigator-class configuration had integrated successfully, that his son was — in the specific, technical sense the report intended — doing well. The sentence still meant that. It also meant that somewhere between hour seventeen and hour twenty-two, something in the quality of Teo’s voice had changed from the register he had spoken in for twenty-two years into something the report called stabilized, and that this stabilization was the same smoothing Iven had watched happen to Orell and to Prya and to eight hundred others, and that the standard language called it *expected*, and the standard language was correct, and the standard language did not have a field for what it cost.  
  
He closed the report. The terminal’s display went to its idle state — the small clock, the ambient system notifications, the steady green indicator of the ship’s navigational status, which meant, among other things, that the Navigator-class network was operational, which meant, among other things, that Teo was somewhere in that network, functioning, integrated, stable.  
  
Iven opened his notebook. Not the notebook he used for calibration notes — a smaller one, older, the cover worn at the corners, the kind he had carried since he was a student and had never stopped carrying despite having no professional use for it. He picked up a pen. He wrote, in his own handwriting, a single line:  
  
*I have known and have not looked.*  
  
He closed the notebook. He set the pen down beside it. He sat at the terminal for another minute, in the dark kitchen, in the apartment where his wife was sleeping and where the garden journal lay closed on the table and where the ceramic bowl sat lopsided and empty and where the light fixture in the ceiling would have flickered if he had turned it on, but he had not turned it on.  
  
He went to bed. Hanne was on her side, facing the viewport, her breathing the slow even pattern of deep sleep. He lay down beside her and looked at the inner hull through the viewport — the running lights at their nighttime rate, the steady dim pulse that Hanne had once told him she preferred to stars because stars did not know you were looking at them and the running lights did not know either but at least they were the ship’s own.  
  
He did not wake her. He lay still. After a while he closed his eyes.
