Chapter 2 — The Message  
  
Hanne had been at the terminal for twenty minutes before she asked him to read it.  
  
She had not said she was composing anything. Iven had come home from a routine shift — no calibrations that day, only equipment certifications, the quiet work that filled the intervals — and found her at the kitchen table with the small terminal open, the garden journal pushed to one side, the ceramic bowl to the other, a cleared space she had made the way she made space for things that required her full attention. She had not looked up when he came in. He had set his bag on the chair by the door and washed his hands and put water on for tea, and when he brought her a cup she took it without drinking and said, still looking at the screen, “Tell me if I’ve left anything out.”  
  
He sat across from her and she turned the terminal toward him. The message was addressed to Teo’s Navigator-class designation — a string of route and cohort identifiers that was not his name but that served, within the Navigator-class communication architecture, as the address where messages intended for Teo could be received and, if a response was generated, replied to. Hanne had typed it from memory.  
  
The message itself was short. Four paragraphs. She had written it the way she wrote everything — the daily sector reports, the notes in the garden journal, the supply requests she filed at the end of each growing rotation — with a directness that did not abbreviate or soften. She had stated the diagnosis. She had stated the timeline: eleven weeks, the date confirmed. She had said that she had selected her calibration technician. She had not said who.  
  
In the last paragraph she had written that she had chosen Navigator-class configuration, and that this was a decision she had made after consideration, and that she wanted him to know before the scheduling office sent its standard notification to connected designations.  
  
She had not said why she chose Navigator-class. Iven read the paragraph again and understood that she would not say why, because the reason was not something the message needed to carry. It would arrive on its own, in the designation’s processing, the way a frequency arrives in the ear: not announced, just present.  
  
“You haven’t left anything out,” he said.  
  
Hanne nodded. She turned the terminal back toward herself and looked at the message for another moment — not rereading, just looking at the whole of it, the shape it made on the screen — and sent it.  
  
She picked up the tea. It had cooled. She drank it.  
  
“He won’t respond right away,” she said. “Navigator-class processing works on its own schedule.”  
  
“I know,” Iven said.  
  
She looked at him with an expression that was not quite a smile. “Of course you do.”  
  
They did not say anything else about it. Hanne opened her sector scheduling application and began checking the next day’s nutrient levels, and Iven cleared the cups and opened his book, and the evening closed around them the way evenings did — the ventilation shifting to its lower register, the lighting dimming along its gradient, the small apartment holding its particular atmosphere of two people being quiet in the same room.  
  
  
—————  
  
  
The response arrived six hours later, early morning. Hanne was dressing for her shift. Iven was at the table with his book — the old novel, the translation he had been rereading for years, whose rhythm he returned to the way other people returned to a specific piece of music. The terminal chimed once. Hanne came out of the bedroom with one sleeve still unfastened and stood over the table and opened it.  
  
Four lines.  
  
*Thank you for informing me. Navigator-class configuration is noted. I will ensure my connectivity status is current for your post-Transition integration window. I hope the remaining weeks are manageable.*  
  
Hanne read it. She read it again. She read it a third time, and Iven, still seated, watched her reading it the third time — watched the way her eyes moved across the four lines with the same careful attention she gave to nutrient readings that were not quite within their expected range. Not alarmed. Not surprised. Verifying that what she had read was what was there.  
  
She closed the message. She fastened her sleeve. She looked at Iven and said, “He sounds well,” and the steadiness in her voice was the kind of steadiness that was not the absence of feeling but the decision, made somewhere private and complete, to carry it in a place where it would not need to be managed by anyone else.  
  
She left for her shift.  
  
Iven sat at the table. The terminal had returned to its default screen. The book was open in front of him but he was not reading. He was thinking about the four lines. *I hope the remaining weeks are manageable.* It was a sentence a considerate person would write. It contained the correct elements — acknowledgment of difficulty, an expression of something that could, from a certain angle, be called concern. It was the kind of sentence Teo would never have written seven years ago, because seven years ago Teo would have called, or come, or said something that was clumsy and too much and that arrived carrying the full unmanaged weight of a person who did not know what to do but could not bear to do nothing.  
  
But Teo had been twenty-two, and had wanted to see the ship the way biology could not show it to him, and had chosen Navigator-class, and the sentence was not from the boy who would have come. It was from the designation that received messages addressed to Teo and processed them on Navigator-class timescales and generated responses in the flat, polite, competent language of a consciousness that had passed through its own integration and arrived, fully, on the other side — not damaged, not diminished, but complete in a way that did not need to reach for anything, because the words it had were sufficient and the searching was not something the architecture required.  
  
Iven closed his book. He sat for a while longer. The apartment was quiet around him, holding the specific emptiness of a room where someone had just been and was no longer, though this was only the ordinary emptiness of Hanne leaving for work, which it had been every morning for thirty-four years, and Iven did not think about the other kind.  
  
That evening, when Hanne came home from Sector Four, she cooked. It was not a stew day — five days remained before the rotation brought in the next round of leaf crops — but she took out the dried stores and the remnants of the previous cycle’s herbs and began making something Iven did not recognize, working at the counter with the steady unhurried attention of a person who needed to be doing something with her hands. She did not explain what it was. He did not ask. He sat at the table and watched her back — the way she moved between the counter and the small stove, the way she reached for a jar and found it without looking because she had been reaching for it in the same place for years, the way her shoulders carried the particular set they carried when she had finished thinking about something and had arrived, on her own, at the place where the thinking was done.  
  
He understood, watching her, that she had decided something. Not about the Transition — that had been decided. Not about Navigator-class — that had been decided too. Something smaller and more private: a decision about what to expect from the four lines, and what not to expect, and how to proceed in the weeks that remained carrying that knowledge in exactly the way she had carried it out of the bedroom that morning — her sleeve unfastened, her face steady, the message closed behind her.  
  
She served the meal. It was good — simpler than the stew, a different set of flavors, something with the dried fennel and a grain preparation he did not usually eat. They ate in a quiet that was neither comfortable nor uncomfortable but was instead the specific quality of silence that settled between two people who were thinking about the same thing and who had both decided, without discussing it, that this was not a silence that needed to be filled.  
  
After dinner they washed and dried the dishes the way they always did. The ceramic bowl went back on the table. The fixture flickered. They did not look up.  
  
Hanne went to bed early. Iven sat at the table in the half-dark — the overhead off, the kitchen light still on, the apartment holding the dimmed warmth of a space that had just been cooked in. He thought about a document he had not read in more than a year. The Completion Report that a Calibration Technician named Renn had filed on Teo seven years ago. Renn was steady and competent, a woman Iven knew slightly and respected. She had written the report in the standard language — the same phrases Iven used in his own reports, the same measured professional cadences that contained a calibration’s entire three days in a form that could be filed and archived and referred to without ever needing to be opened again. He had read it three times in seven years. Each time it had told him the same thing.  
  
He thought, sitting in the kitchen light, that he might read it again soon. He did not examine the thought too closely. It had the quality of something not yet settled — a document that might, if he brought a different kind of attention to it, say something he had not previously heard. He did not know what that different attention would be. He only knew that his son had written I hope the remaining weeks are manageable and that the sentence was correct and that it was, in some way he could not yet name, the most complete piece of evidence he had for what the calibration process did to a person.  
  
He turned off the kitchen light. He went to bed. Hanne’s breathing was steady, already deep in the rhythm of sleep. The viewport above them showed the running lights at their nighttime rate.  
  
He would read the report soon.
