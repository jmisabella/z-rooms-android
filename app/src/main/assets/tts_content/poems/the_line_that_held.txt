*The Line That Held*


I don't remember who dialed.
Only that the line was open
at an hour no one calls for small reasons,
and I was the kind of open
I had only ever been
when borrowed from myself.

A thousand miles between us,
a childhood of them,
the ordinary distance of cousins
who grow up in separate weather.

And still — the line.
Phone line. Lifeline. Bloodline.
One word for the thing
that refused to go slack
when everything else did.


She already knew the name
I had only said once,
to my mother,
in a kitchen that no longer exists.
Word travels the long way
through a family —
through aunts, through rooms,
through the small careful mouths
of people who love you
and don't know what to do with the loving.
It arrived at her door already held.

After the years
that folded into a shape
I still can't read —
after the kitchen, after the room that went still,
after the order of grief
went loose in my hands —
the line was still the line.


This was the whole weather turning,
the long arrangement
of everyone who ever loved me
reaching through one another
to reach me.
