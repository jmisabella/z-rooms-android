# Chapter 3: The Chamber

Three days left, and Reed was learning what it meant to say goodbye to a body that wasn't dying.

The preference form sat on the datapad in Reed's quarters, waiting for final submission. Standard multipurpose operational configuration. Middle of the road. Not massive like Navigator-class, not microscopic like Microtechnician. Just functional. Adaptable. The kind of choice made by someone who couldn't bring themselves to choose anything at all.

Reed pressed their thumb to the submission field. The form processed with a soft chime. Done.

The quarters were the same as they'd always been. Small, efficient, slightly too cold. Reed had never accumulated much—personal items weren't encouraged when space was limited and everyone knew they'd be Transitioning eventually. But there were a few things. A small decorative stone Kess had given them years ago. A drawing Mira had made during a boring orientation session, all geometric patterns and precise lines. A photograph of the three of them, taken before any of them really understood what Transition meant.

Reed picked up the photograph. In it, Reed's mother's hand rested on Reed's shoulder—the same hand that used to press cool fingers to Reed's forehead when Reed was sick, checking temperature without saying anything, humming that melody. Reed studied the hand now. Memorized the shape of it, knowing they were already too late. Reed had been practicing forgetting for weeks now, every sensation slipping a little faster each time they tried to hold it.

A soft knock at the door. Kess and Mira came through, wearing expressions of careful solemnity.

"Farewell ceremony starts in twenty minutes," Kess said. "Thought we'd walk over together."

"Ready?" Mira asked.

"No," Reed said. "But I don't think that matters."

---

The ceremony was held in one of the larger common areas, decorated with nothing. No streamers, no celebration. Just rows of seats and a small platform where an Operator stood, its configuration almost human-shaped, almost comforting, almost right.

Fifteen trainees sat in the front rows—all of them scheduled for Transition within the next week. Reed, Kess, and Mira sat together. Around them, Operators of various configurations. A few recent Transfers, still adjusting to their new forms. The youngest children, watching with wide eyes.

"We gather today to acknowledge those approaching their Transition," the Operator on the platform said. Its voice was warm, practiced, carrying just the right tone. "This is not an ending, but a continuation. Your consciousness will persist. Your identity will remain. Only the substrate changes."

Reed's hands gripped the edge of their seat.

"Every Operator in this room has made this journey. Every one of us is still here, still ourselves, still part of this community."

When it was time, the fifteen trainees came forward and stood in a line. Reed's heart hammered against their ribs—one of the last times it would feel like this, fast and erratic and human.

"Do you have anything you wish to leave behind?" the Operator asked.

Kess stepped forward first, holding out the small decorative stone. "For whoever needs it next." Others followed—a drawing, a handwritten note, a small precision tool. Reed reached into their pocket, pulled out the photograph. The three of them, younger, smiling, unaware.

"For memory," Reed said quietly.

"And what will you carry forward?"

The traditional response was "Consciousness. Identity. Self."

"I don't know," Reed said instead.

The silence stretched. The Operator's sensors focused on Reed with uncomfortable intensity.

"You will carry forward," the Operator said finally, "what makes you you."

Reed didn't respond.

---

Afterward there was food—the same flavorless nutrition as always, presented on slightly nicer plates. Reed ate slowly.

"This isn't a funeral," Kess said, sitting down beside Reed. "We're going to see each other in three days."

"Will we?" Reed asked. "Will you still be Kess when you're the size of a building? Will Mira still be Mira when they're a thousand copies of themselves?"

Kess's expression tightened. "Yes. Because consciousness persists. Because identity isn't about having hands or eyes or a heartbeat."

They sat without talking. Around them the gathering dispersed, Operators drifting back to duties, trainees returning to their quarters for their final nights in biological form.

---

Two days. One day. Then: morning.

Reed didn't sleep the last night. They lay in bed, feeling the mattress beneath them, the weight of the standard-issue blanket, the temperature of the air. When the wake cycle began, Reed rose slowly, washed their face with water that felt cold, then lukewarm. Looked in the mirror—one of the few mirrors on the ship. Soon they wouldn't have a face to check.

The message came through the comm: "Group Seven, please proceed to Transition facility. Time slot begins in thirty minutes."

Reed dressed in the standard transition garments—simple, designed to be removed easily by medical staff—and stepped into the corridor. Fourteen other trainees were already gathering, all wearing the same expression of controlled anxiety.

They walked through familiar corridors that suddenly felt foreign. Reed looked at everything like it was the last time—because it was.

They turned a corner and the facility came into view. Large. Sterile. No windows. Just smooth walls and a single entrance where medical Operators waited with datapads. Inside: machinery. Soft voices giving instructions. The low hum of systems processing.

Then Reed saw it.

Through an open doorway on the left: rows of preservation tanks stretching back farther than Reed could see. Identical cylindrical chambers, each about two meters tall, connected to ceiling-mounted systems by thick cables and tubing. Through the transparent sections, fluid—pale blue, slowly circulating. And suspended in that fluid, connected to feeding tubes and monitoring equipment, were brains.

Human brains. Each tagged with a designation: "Substrate 4,782." "Substrate 8,391." "Tank 9,127." Numbers, not names. Each one pulsing slightly with the rhythm of maintained life.

Reed stopped walking.

"Reed, this way," the medical Operator said, guiding Reed away from the doorway.

Reed let themselves be led. Behind them, a maintenance Operator made a note on a datapad: "Tank 5,447 — nutrient circulation pump needs replacement."

Just infrastructure.

The preparation room was small. A medical table in the center. Monitoring equipment surrounding it. Restraints built into the table's surface—gentle-looking but clearly meant to prevent movement.

"Please lie down," the Operator said.

Reed didn't move.

"Reed, please lie down. We're on a schedule."

Reed lay down. The surface was cold against their back. The Operator began attaching sensors to Reed's temples, chest, hands.

"The process is straightforward," the Operator explained. "A mild sedative to relax the biological substrate. Your consciousness will remain active throughout. The transfer takes approximately thirty seconds."

Reed's hands gripped the edges of the table.

"Wait," Reed said. "Wait, I need—"

"Apprehension is normal. Try to breathe slowly."

A hiss. Something cool spreading through Reed's bloodstream. The panic was still there, but distant. Observable rather than overwhelming. The restraints engaged—soft straps across wrists, ankles, chest. Not painful. Just firm.

Above the table, polished metal reflected Reed's face. The last time Reed would see themselves like this. Brown eyes. Face gone pale. Lips moving soundlessly.

*I am Reed. I am still Reed. I will always be Reed.*

"Beginning transfer in three... two... one."
