# Chapter 2: The Operators

Sixteen days left, and Reed was learning to look away.

The maintenance corridor stretched ahead, lit by strips of white light that made everything feel exposed. Reed's assignment for the morning cycle: assist the Operators with routine calibration work on the environmental systems. Assist. As if Reed could do anything the Operators couldn't do better, faster, with appendages that could reach into spaces that shouldn't exist.

Three Operators were already at work when Reed arrived. One had extended something like arms into an open maintenance panel, joints bending in directions that made Reed's stomach tighten. Another had compressed itself to fit inside a narrow access shaft, its form rippling as it moved deeper into the wall. The third stood near the main console, its proportions almost human-sized, almost recognizable, except for the way its limbs seemed to occupy more space than they should.

"Good morning," that one said. "You'll be working with Operator Twelve-Seven today."

Operator Twelve-Seven emerged from the access shaft in a way that shouldn't have been possible given the opening's dimensions, unfolding in stages. Its configuration was lean, multi-jointed, with too many articulation points along what might have been a spine. It turned toward Reed, and though it had nothing that resembled eyes, Reed felt seen.

"Thermal regulation needs checking," Twelve-Seven said. "You'll monitor the readouts. I'll handle the adjustments."

They worked in silence. Reed stood at the console, watching numbers shift as Twelve-Seven moved through the system, its form visible through transparent access panels as it navigated impossible spaces. The efficiency was remarkable. Every movement precise, every adjustment immediate. No wasted motion. No hesitation.

Too perfect.

Reed glanced down the corridor where the other two Operators worked. They moved in synchronization, reaching for tools at the exact same moment, adjusting their positions with identical timing. No communication that Reed could hear. No signals exchanged. Just perfect, seamless coordination.

"Temperature variance in section nine is higher than optimal," Reed said, reading from the display.

"Adjusting now," Twelve-Seven replied. Through the panel, Reed watched it extend something thin and precise into the wall's infrastructure. The temperature reading corrected instantly.

"How do you know where to—" Reed started.

"Where to reach?" Twelve-Seven finished. "The systems are standardized. Once you know one ship, you know them all." A pause. "Besides, I've been doing this for a long time."

Another Operator arrived, its configuration towering and angular, with limbs that seemed to bend backward at what Reed took for elbows. Without warning, its proportions shifted—limbs extended, joints reconfigured—casual as rolling up a sleeve.

Reed looked away, focused on the console.

"You get used to it," Twelve-Seven said quietly. "The configurations, I mean. Once you've been through Transfer, changing configuration becomes as natural as breathing used to be."

*Used to be.*

Reed's hands stilled on the console.

"I remember—" Twelve-Seven continued, then stopped. A long pause. When it spoke again, the voice was different, more careful. "The training materials cover configuration flexibility. You'll understand after Transition."

But that wasn't what it had been about to say.

The work continued. Reed called out values, tried to make themselves useful. Tried not to watch too closely when the Operators moved.

"Hand me the calibration tool," Twelve-Seven said.

Reed found the cylindrical device with sensor arrays and held it out.

Twelve-Seven took it with something that couldn't be called fingers, holding the tool delicately despite the alien nature of its grip. "Thanks, Marcus."

Reed froze. "My name is Reed."

Twelve-Seven went completely still. The other Operators paused in their work, that too-perfect synchronization extending even to their stopping.

"Of course," Twelve-Seven said. "Reed. I apologize. A slip of the tongue."

But Reed had heard the certainty in that first name. *Marcus.* Said like Twelve-Seven knew them, remembered them, expected them to respond to it. Said like a name from muscle memory, from habit, from having said it many times before.

"Marcus was..." Reed started.

"A common name," Twelve-Seven said quickly. "I must have been thinking of someone else. The Transition can sometimes muddle recent memories and old ones. Nothing to worry about."

Marcus wasn't a common name. Reed had never met another Marcus on the ship. And Twelve-Seven's voice had carried the inflection of familiarity, not confusion.

How old was Twelve-Seven? How many trainees had it worked with over the years? How many names did it remember, filed away in whatever served as its memory, fragments of people who had gone through Transfer and become Operators themselves?

"We're finished here," one of the other Operators said. "Efficiency is optimal. Good work."

They left as smoothly as they'd worked, their movements coordinated in a way that spoke of consciousness beyond the individual. Twelve-Seven was the last to go, pausing at the corridor junction.

"You'll do fine," it said to Reed. "After Transfer. Everyone does."

Then it was gone.

Reed stood alone in the corridor, hands shaking, staring at the console's readouts without seeing them.

---

The observation deck was usually empty during the mid-cycle.

Most of the trainees avoided it. Too quiet, maybe. Too much empty space. Too stark a reminder that they were on a ship in the middle of nothing, traveling to somewhere none of them could clearly remember learning about, for reasons that had something to do with the Necessity but nothing to do with choice.

Reed came here specifically for the emptiness.

The viewport stretched across the entire forward wall, showing the star field beyond. Thousands of points of light, distant and cold. And between them, occasionally, the dark silhouettes of Operators working on the ship's exterior.

Reed watched one now, its form outlined against the void. Massive out there, configuration adapted for the work, with appendages that seemed to sprout from no central body, reaching and grasping and manipulating in ways that made Reed's depth perception fail. It moved from one section of the hull to another without any visible means of propulsion, as if space itself was something it could navigate like walking.

Two Operators worked together with the same perfect coordination Reed had seen in the maintenance corridor. No communication. No visible signals. Just seamless cooperation, as if they shared one mind distributed across two configurations.

Maybe they did.

Reed pressed their forehead against the viewport, felt the cold of it against their skin. Kess used to come here too, Reed remembered—not for the emptiness, but for the opposite reason. Kess said the walls felt like they were closing in and only the stars felt wide enough to breathe.

"Beautiful, isn't it?"

Reed jerked back. An Operator stood a few meters away, its configuration humanoid-sized, though with proportions that suggested it could change that at will. Reed hadn't heard it enter.

"The view," the Operator clarified. "I've always thought it was beautiful."

"You come here often?" Reed's voice came out smaller than intended.

"I used to." The Operator moved to the viewport, standing beside Reed. Close enough that Reed could see the fine details of its configuration—the seamless surface that had no visible joints but moved with perfect articulation anyway. "Before. When I could feel the temperature through skin. When I could feel the vibration of the ship through my feet. It was... different then."

Reed's breath caught.

*Before. When I could feel the temperature through skin.*

The Operator seemed to realize what it had said. Its form went very still.

"You were..." Reed started.

"I've served for several cycles," the Operator said, its tone carefully neutral now. "One's memory sometimes conflates different periods of service. The sensory experience of configuration can create false memories of limitation."

"You said 'before,'" Reed pressed. "Before what?"

"Before is relative." The Operator stepped back from the viewport. "I should return to my duties. Enjoy the view, trainee."

"Wait—"

But the Operator was already leaving, its movement smooth and deliberate. The door slid shut, leaving Reed alone with the stars.

*When I could feel the temperature through skin.*

Not 'if I could.' Not 'when one has skin.' *When I could.*

Past tense. Personal. Specific.

Reed looked back at the viewport. The Operators continued their work, dark shapes against darker space. How many of them remembered being human? How many still thought in terms of 'before' and 'after'? How many stood here, in their off-cycles—if they had off-cycles, if they needed rest at all—and remembered what it felt like to be cold?

Reed pressed their hand against the wall, felt the vibration travel through their palm, up their arm, into their chest. Out there the Operators moved across the hull, each one a 'before' that never got spoken.

Sixteen days.

---

Three days later, Reed needed answers.

The library terminal was in one of the quieter sections of the ship, a small room with a few database access points. Reed sat at one of the terminals and pulled up the search interface. The screen glowed in the dim light, waiting.

Reed typed: *Transition process details*

The results populated instantly. Clinical documents, training materials, consciousness continuity studies. Reed opened the first one.

*The Transition process ensures seamless consciousness transfer from biological substrate to operational configuration. Neural patterns are mapped with 99.97% fidelity, maintaining continuity of self while enabling enhanced capabilities. The preservation process safeguards biological substrate for optimal longevity and resource efficiency.*

The same language from every training session. Smooth. Reassuring. Empty.

Reed tried another search: *Why is Transition necessary*

More results. More clinical language.

*Operational efficiency requires enhanced capabilities beyond biological limitations. The Necessity mandates optimal resource utilization for ship survival and mission continuity.*

The Necessity. Always capitalized. Always vague.

Reed leaned back, frustration building. Every document said the same thing in slightly different words. Consciousness continuity. Operational efficiency. The Necessity. Resource optimization. But none of them explained what it *felt* like. None of them addressed whether you were still you afterward.

Reed's fingers hovered over the keyboard, then typed: *Preservation substrate maintenance*

They weren't sure why they searched for that. Just a feeling. A hunch from the earlier conversation with Operator Twelve-Seven, from the medical examination's focus on brain health, from the way everything treated the body as temporary.

The results were different this time. Technical documents. Maintenance protocols. System logs.

Reed opened one at random.

*Cycle 4,891, Day 156: Tank 4,782 - nutrient flush required. Circulation pump replacement scheduled. Substrate neural activity nominal.*

Reed stared at the text.

*Tank. Substrate. Neural activity.*

They opened another log.

*Cycle 4,891, Day 157: Tank 3,391 - replaced circulation pump. Temperature regulation adjusted. Substrate 3,391 showing optimal preservation metrics.*

Another.

*Cycle 4,891, Day 159: Tanks 8,000-8,100 - routine neural monitoring complete. All substrates within acceptable parameters. Tank 8,047 flagged for enhanced nutrient supplementation.*

These weren't abstract technical documents. These were maintenance records. Regular, routine maintenance of thousands of tanks containing substrates with neural activity.

*Substrates.* That's what the medical Operator had called Reed's body. The temporary interface. The biological substrate.

Reed scrolled through the logs. Hundreds of entries. Thousands. Different cycle numbers going back years, all with the same routine maintenance tasks.

Nutrient flush.
Temperature regulation.
Circulation pump maintenance.
Neural activity monitoring.

All of it describing tanks. Numbered tanks. Tens of thousands of them.

Reed followed cross-references in the maintenance logs and found an inventory document.

*Preservation Facility Status Report - Cycle 4,891*
*Total active preservation tanks: 47,392*
*Substrates in optimal condition: 47,385*
*Substrates requiring maintenance: 7*
*Substrates scheduled for recycling: 12*

Forty-seven thousand tanks.

The realization hit like a physical blow. The bodies didn't come along during Transition. The consciousness was transferred, yes, but the brain stayed behind. Removed from the body. Placed in preservation. Kept alive in tanks. Maintained like any other ship system.

All the people who had gone through Transition. Their brains were still here. Still alive. Still generating neural activity. But separated from consciousness, from awareness, from everything that made them *them*.

Or were they? Was consciousness really transferred, or just copied? Was the brain in the tank still aware somehow, trapped without sensory input? Or was it just biological machinery, running on automatic while the real person existed somewhere else in machine form?

Reed didn't know. Couldn't know.

But in thirteen days, their brain would be removed from their body. Placed in a tank. Given a number. Maintained with routine nutrient flushes and circulation pump replacements.

The terminal screen held its glow, steady and indifferent. Maintenance logs scrolled past—endless entries of routine procedures on substrates that used to be people.

Forty-seven thousand tanks pulsed with life that had no name.

Soon there would be forty-seven thousand and one.
