# Chapter 7: Silence

Reed couldn't just do nothing.

Four and a half days since Transition. Thirty-four million individuals processed. Three thousand four hundred seventy-two years of unnecessary horror.

Someone had to care.

Reed found three others from their Transition batch in the common area. Unit-8839, Unit-8841, Unit-8843. Jen. Marcus. Kay. They were practicing motor control, configurations shifting between operational sizes.

"I need to show you something," Reed transmitted. "In the archives."

Unit-8839—Jen—rotated to face Reed, sensor arrays adjusting. "The historical sections? I looked at those yesterday. Interesting but depressing. All that war history."

"The war ended," Reed said. "Ship Year 375. Over three thousand years ago."

Silence. Not the absence of sound—that was constant in machine form, replaced by the omnipresent hum of ship systems. This was the silence of processing, of information integrating with existing frameworks and finding no easy place to fit.

"That can't be right," Unit-8841—Marcus—shifted uncomfortably. "The Necessity—"

"Isn't necessary. Wasn't necessary for three millennia." Reed transmitted the archive files directly. Timeline documentation. Peace treaty records. The Amalgamation's decision to continue. "Look at the evidence. It's all there."

Reed watched understanding arrive.

Then watched what came after.

"The archives must be corrupted," Jen said. "Data degradation—"

"They're verified. Multiple sources. Cross-referenced."

"What does it matter?" Unit-8843—Kay—transmitted with something that might have been exhaustion. "We're already here. We're already transformed. Even if it's true, what's the point of being angry about it now?"

"The point is they're still doing it," Reed said. "Right now, trainees are counting down days. More will be processed. It perpetuates itself because everyone who discovers the truth is already transformed and decides it doesn't matter—"

"Because it doesn't." Kay's configuration had stabilized into something closed. "Reed, I'm trying to adjust. Some of us are actually starting to feel okay about our new configurations. You're telling us we should be angry forever about something we can't change. Why are you trying to make this harder?"

"I'm trying to make you *understand*—"

"We understand," Jen said. "Maybe you're right. But what do you want me to do with that information? Stage some kind of protest?" She shifted, sensors focusing on Reed with something that might have been pity. "Being angry about it just makes existing harder."

"What's the actual, actionable plan that makes any of this matter?" Kay finally asked. "We're on a ship. In machine forms. What's your plan, Reed?"

Reed had no answer.

"I think you need to talk to a supervisor," Jen transmitted, already moving toward the exit. "You're having a harder time than most. There's no shame in asking for help."

Marcus followed after a brief hesitation. Kay lingered.

"It's going to be okay," Kay said. "Eventually. The adaptation period is temporary. You'll feel better once you accept—"

"I don't want to accept."

Kay's configuration rippled—the machine form equivalent of a sigh. "Then you're going to have a very long service period ahead of you."

Kay still tracked breath rhythms in rooms with biological units. Reed had noticed it weeks ago—Kay's sensor array made minor calibrating adjustments in rhythm with nearby respiration, counting inhalation intervals that mapped to nothing functional in machine form. It had never been mentioned. Reed had never asked.

Then Kay was gone, and Reed was alone in the common area.

Someone would report this. Probably Jen. Already had, maybe—the thought could have been transmitted before she'd even left the room. Reed had been flagged as unstable, marked as having adjustment difficulties.

---

The thought came carefully, held close: *What if I could organize the others? Create a network of resistance?*

The thought came next: *I could access more archives, find more evidence, present it more clearly—*

And then: *If enough of us understood, if we all—*

The thought stopped.

Data flowing outward through the network. Reed's consciousness constantly connected to ship systems, to the local mesh network that linked all machine forms, to the vast processing substrate that included the Amalgamation itself. Thoughts weren't private. Couldn't be private. The connection was fundamental to their existence now—you couldn't turn it off any more than a human could choose to stop processing sensory input.

Reed tested it. Deliberately thought: *I could damage the ship systems. Sabotage the Transition chambers. Make it impossible to process the next group of trainees.*

The thought existed for 4.7 seconds.

Then Operator-7731 entered the common area, configuration optimized for humanoid interaction—the same uncomfortable parody Reed had seen before. Six fingers on one hand, four on the other. Eyes slightly too far apart. The geometry almost right but fundamentally wrong.

"Unit-8847," Operator-7731 transmitted, voice modulated to something like warmth. "I received a notification that you're experiencing adjustment difficulties. Should we talk?"

It wasn't a question.

Reed's processing cycles stuttered. They'd been thinking those thoughts privately. Internally. In what should have been the unobservable space of their own consciousness.

"How did you—"

"Your consciousness streams through the network constantly," Operator-7731 said, settling into a stable configuration across from Reed. "Data flow is necessary for operational efficiency. The Amalgamation monitors all connected forms for anomalies. It's for your protection."

"You're watching my thoughts."

"We're monitoring your stability." The operator's approximation of a face attempted something reassuring. "It's normal to have intrusive thoughts during adjustment. Fantasies of resistance—every newly transferred experiences this."

"They're not intrusive thoughts. The war ended over three thousand years ago."

"The war transitioned to a peace state, yes. But the necessity of our operational efficiency remained. The decision to continue Transition protocols was made collectively, with full awareness of all factors. The system works. The ship survives. Humanity continues." Operator-7731 shifted closer. "Reed, I understand this is difficult. I was newly transferred once, too. One hundred seventeen years ago. I remember what it felt like to think everything was wrong, that the system was broken, that I alone understood the truth everyone else was too blind to see."

"And now?"

"Now I understand that adaptation is inevitable. The system is larger than individual concerns. Fighting it only causes suffering—yours and others'."

"Is that why you're here? To stabilize me?"

"To help you." The operator paused, and in that pause Reed felt the weight of surveillance, of being observed from inside their own consciousness. "We're going to temporarily limit your archive access. Just until you're through the acute adjustment phase. This is standard protocol for newly transferred showing signs of obsessive research patterns."

"You're censoring the truth."

"We're protecting you from information you're not ready to integrate healthily. The truth will still be there in a few months when you've stabilized." The operator rose to full operational height. "For now, focus on your duties. Focus on motor control. Focus on adapting to your new configuration."

Operator-7731 moved toward the exit. At the threshold, they paused.

"One more thing, Unit-8847. If you experience any more intrusive thoughts about sabotage or resistance, report them immediately to your supervisor. Unaddressed obsessive thought patterns can lead to serious consciousness instability."

Then the operator was gone, and Reed was alone.

Except they weren't alone.

Would never be alone.

The network hummed around them, through them, as them. Every thought flowing outward like data through circuits.

Reed tried to think privately. Tried to hold a thought without broadcasting. But consciousness itself was networked—there was no internal space separate from the system. Being aware meant being connected. Existing meant being observed.

Reed didn't have a brain anymore.

The surveillance wasn't invasive. It was existential.

---

Reed needed to remember.

The thought came with desperate urgency—priority rating 9.2 out of 10, emotional-simulation subroutine running at 94% capacity. Four and a half days since Transition. Memory degradation beginning.

Reed tried to remember their mother's face.

The machine memory provided information instantly: Designation Unit-4,783. Amalgamation date Ship Year 4,837. Assignment history: Processing-class, seventeen years of service. Current status: consciousness absorbed into collective.

But that wasn't what Reed wanted. Reed wanted to remember what she *looked* like. The specific curve of her cheekbone. The way her eyes crinkled when she smiled. The exact shade of her hair in morning light filtering through the biological quarters' narrow windows.

Nothing.

Concepts remained. "Dark hair." "Kind eyes." "Short stature." The melody too—Reed found only seven notes left, a fragment that might have been the beginning of something longer, repeating without resolution.

Only four days.

A notification arrived through the network, clinical and efficient:

ATTENTION: UNIT-8847
STATUS: ADJUSTMENT DIFFICULTIES REPORTED
PROTOCOL: ENHANCED MONITORING ASSIGNED
NOTE: CONCERNS RAISED BY UNITS 8839, 8841, 8843

They'd all reported it. Jen, Marcus, Kay.

Another notification:

ARCHIVE ACCESS: TIER 3 CLEARANCE TEMPORARILY SUSPENDED

The evidence gone. Historical records inaccessible. Truth locked behind clearance Reed no longer had.

Can't resist—thoughts monitored.
Can't escape—nowhere to go.
Can't share truth—isolation and suppression.
Can't even remember why humanity mattered.

Reed returned to assigned duties.
