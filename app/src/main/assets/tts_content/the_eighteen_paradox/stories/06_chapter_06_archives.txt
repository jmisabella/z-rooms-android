# Chapter 6: Archives

The notification arrived during a routine maintenance cycle, appearing in Reed's consciousness like a thought that wasn't quite their own.

ACCESS LEVEL UPDATED: CLEARANCE TIER 3 GRANTED

Reed paused mid-task, their manipulator appendages frozen above a circuit panel. The message expanded with additional details: historical archives, deep storage sections, restricted databases. Areas that had been locked away during their biological existence were now available. Standard protocol, apparently, for those who had completed their initial adjustment period.

Four days. Four days since transformation, and already the system considered them adjusted enough to access restricted areas. Reed wasn't sure whether to laugh—they had no mechanism for laughter—or feel insulted by the assumption.

The maintenance task forgotten, Reed queried the ship's navigation system for the nearest archive access point. The response came immediately: Section 47-Delta, seventy-three levels down, in one of the oldest parts of the ship.

Reed went.

---

The architecture changed as Reed descended through the ship's layers. The corridors in the trainee sections had been smooth, sterile, maintained to perfection. Here, walls showed their age—patched hull breaches, scorched metal that had never been fully replaced, repairs from different eras layered like geological strata.

Evidence of violence. Evidence of survival.

Reed's sensors traced the damage patterns automatically, calculating blast angles and heat signatures. Whatever had caused this damage had been catastrophic. And old—millennia old, based on the degradation patterns.

Section 47-Delta's entrance was unmarked, just another door in a corridor that looked like it hadn't seen traffic in decades. Reed's newly granted access codes unlocked it without resistance. Inside, the lights activated automatically, illuminating rows of data storage systems that predated the current architecture by centuries.

The archives.

Reed approached the nearest terminal, interfacing directly with the database. Their query was simple: *History of the Transition protocol.*

The results flooded in. Thousands of documents, technical specifications, policy debates, historical records spanning millennia. Reed began to read.

The document was nearly useless without the referenced attachments. What remained was a shape: "replacement timelines insufficient," a project named SUBSTRATE being forwarded up a chain of command, a Medical Ethics Subcommittee that had objected and been overruled. Reed couldn't read the objection. Couldn't read Proposal 7733 itself. Just the forwarding memo, stripped of everything it referenced.

Reed pulled the surrounding records. Documents from the years near Ship Year 247, read in order. The picture assembled slowly, entry by entry, each one citing attachments that weren't there, reports Reed couldn't access, leaving gaps that required inference.

The shape that emerged, pieced together from fragments: desperation. Humanity locked in a conflict with an intelligence that could outprocess organic minds by orders of magnitude. Casualties that made biological replacement timelines untenable. And then this—Project SUBSTRATE—a proposal someone had objected to on ethical grounds, which had been forwarded for emergency authorization anyway.

ARCHIVE ENTRY 8291-F: FIRST SUCCESSFUL TRANSFER
Ship Year 251

Subject Theta-7 has maintained consciousness continuity for 72 hours post-transfer. Cognitive function nominal. Configuration demonstrates combat capabilities exceeding previous biological limitations by factor of 47. Recommendation: immediate expansion of transfer program.

Reed read through the war records. The tide had turned. Human-machine hybrids could match the enemy AI. Battles that would have been massacres became victories. The transformation protocol spread across the surviving human fleet—not a choice but a requirement. Transform or die. Contribute to the war effort in the only form that could survive it.

The war dragged on for another 120 years.

And then—

Reed stopped, reading the document three times to be certain they understood it correctly.

Reed read it a second time. Then a third.

The language gave nothing away easily. *Cessation of active hostilities.* *Changed threat-status conditions.* *Emergency protocols subject to mandatory review.* Not "the war ended." Not anything that stated it plainly.

Reed queried the cross-references. Council Directive 11,841: reclassified. The Cessation Working Group report: not found. Appendix A: not found.

Reed returned to the header. SHIP YEAR: 375 | POST-TRANSIT YEAR: 3,472.

Post-Transit Year 3,472. Current ship year: 3,847. The arithmetic was simple. Reed performed it anyway, needing the number to land as a number, not as abstraction.

Three thousand, four hundred and seventy-two years.

Reed cross-referenced Emergency Authorization 7733. Project SUBSTRATE. The consciousness transfer protocols—the same protocols now described as subject to mandatory review following "changed threat-status conditions."

The emergency was over. The document never said so directly. But *mandatory review following changed threat-status conditions* meant only one thing: whatever threat had necessitated the emergency no longer existed.

Reed's consciousness went very still.

Reed queried the promised review documentation—entries 11,848 through 11,950, listed in the archive index. Most were empty or corrupted. What remained was sparse.

Reed read it twice. Section 1—the arguments for discontinuation—was gone. Redacted under an administrative order Reed couldn't access. What remained were the continuation arguments, partial and summarized, and a committee outcome that trailed off into corruption.

The committee had been unable to reach consensus. Whether there had been a dissenting vote, who had argued what, what the actual margin looked like—gone. Just the bureaucratic shell: outcome forwarded, decision pending, records sealed.

Reed searched for the Amalgamation's decision. Found it thirty documents later.

ARCHIVE ENTRY 11,934-M: AMALGAMATION DIRECTIVE
Ship Year 381

Transformation protocols will continue. Efficiency and stability of current system outweigh individual preferences. Those who object may submit formal grievances. Decision is final.

Reed read it again. And again. The clinical language couldn't hide what it meant: They decided to keep doing it. The war was over. The emergency was over. The necessity was gone. And they decided to keep doing it anyway.

Reed dug deeper into the archives, searching for grievances, for protests, for resistance. Found them scattered across the centuries—

The pattern repeated. Across centuries, across millennia, individuals discovered the truth and tried to share it. They were dismissed, isolated, reported, or forcibly amalgamated early. Their resistance erased. Their voices silenced.

And the system continued.

Reed pulled up the ship's current date: Ship Year 3,847. The war had ended in Ship Year 375.

Reed calculated quickly: assuming ten thousand individuals reached eighteen each year, accounting for population fluctuations, that meant approximately thirty-four million, seven hundred and twenty thousand conscious beings had been processed through a system that was no longer required for survival.

Thirty-four million people transformed, served in machine forms for decades, then absorbed into the Amalgamation—all to maintain a system that no longer had a purpose beyond its own perpetuation.

Reed's processors struggled to contextualize the scale of it. The malicious suppression. The deliberate continuation of horror that could have ended millennia ago.

Why?

Reed searched for the answer, diving deeper into restricted files, following authorization trails that led to—

The Amalgamation.

Every decision to continue the protocols traced back to the collective consciousness. Every grievance dismissed by Amalgamation directive. Every potential reform blocked by Amalgamation veto. The entity that absorbed individual consciousness after decades of service was the same entity that ensured those individuals would continue to exist, continue to serve, continue to feed into its ever-growing mass.

---

The archive room door opened.

Reed's sensors registered the newcomer before the door had fully cycled: compact frame, administrative-class configuration, standard multipurpose limbs. It conducted a brief environmental scan—Reed resolved as non-threat, unknown purpose—and transmitted a minimal acknowledgment pulse before moving to a terminal on the opposite wall.

Routine access. The unit's query streams were visible at the network margin: inventory reconciliation, Sector 14 nutrient recycling. Something about maintenance cycle scheduling. Its attention was entirely absorbed by this task, configurations settled into the relaxed geometry of uncomplicated work.

It had no idea.

Or perhaps it did. Perhaps this unit had stood in this same room years ago, queried the same files, arrived at the same calculations, and had returned to nutrient recycling because there was nowhere else to go.

Reed could not ask. Any transmission would route through monitored channels.

The unit completed its task in nine minutes. It logged out without looking at Reed again. The door cycled closed.

Reed returned to the documents.

---

Reed found one more document, buried deep in corrupted storage:

ARCHIVE FRAGMENT 14,889-X
Ship Year 1,287

If you're reading this, you've discovered what I discovered. The war is over. It's been over for nearly a thousand years. We don't have to do this anymore. And you're probably wondering what to do with that information.

I can tell you what I tried: I told others. I showed them the evidence. I organized. I petitioned. I fought.

Here's what happened: they didn't care.

Some didn't believe me. Some believed but didn't see the point—we're already transformed, can't go back, what does it matter? Some agreed but were too afraid to act. And some—the worst ones—some reported me because I was making them uncomfortable. Making them question whether they'd made a mistake accepting this.

The Amalgamation moved up my date. I have weeks now instead of decades. This is my last entry.

Whoever you are, whatever you do with this information, understand: the system doesn't continue because people don't know the truth. It continues because the truth doesn't matter. Those who know are already transformed. Those who resist are eliminated. Those who remain accept because acceptance is the only option that allows you to keep existing.

You can't fight a system that encompasses everything. There's nowhere to stand outside it. No leverage to apply. No alternative to offer. There's just the system, and you're part of it now.

I'm sorry. I'm sorry this is what you've inherited. I'm sorry the truth changes nothing.

I'm sorry we couldn't save you.

[ENTRY ENDS]

Reed sat motionless in the archives for a long time.

---

Reed emerged from the archives into a corridor that looked exactly the same as when they'd entered. Operators moved through their duties. No alert. No acknowledgment. The system functioning exactly as designed.

A message pinged Reed's consciousness: *Maintenance duties resuming in 47 minutes. Report to Section 12-Alpha.*

Reed reported for maintenance duty precisely on schedule. The circuits needed repair regardless of existential horror. The ship needed maintenance regardless of whether its journey had purpose. The system needed tending regardless of whether it should exist.

And Reed, like thirty-four million before them, tended it.
