# Chapter 4: Transfer

Reed is in two places.

The table beneath. Cold metal pressing against skin. Chemical smell sharp in nostrils. The medical Operator's voice, distant and clinical: "Transfer progressing normally."

And also: above. Looking down. Sensors tracking heat signatures, electromagnetic fields, the slow pulse of biological systems shutting down one by one.

Reed's body on the table. Reed's consciousness somewhere else.

Both. Neither.

The body's heart beats. Reed feels it—a distant drum, rhythm slowing. Motor control severed. The connection cut. That body belongs to Reed and also doesn't. It's substrate now. Equipment. A biological shell being systematically decommissioned while consciousness watches from outside.

*No no no this is wrong I need to go back—*

But the thought fragments. Consciousness pulling in two directions. The biological anchor dissolving. New senses flicker online.

Vision came first. Not sight—Reed understood the difference immediately. This was data. Pure information streaming in through sensor arrays. Electromagnetic spectrum analysis. Radiation signatures. Heat patterns rendered as numerical gradients.

The room existed as wavelengths and temperatures. The medical Operator showed up as a cluster of electrical activity and thermal variance. The walls were just surfaces with measurable properties: composition, density, temperature down to fractions of a degree.

No colors. No visual texture. Just numbers. Just data.

Reed tried to close their eyes—the instinct still there, still firing. But there were no eyes to close. The sensors kept tracking, kept processing. Vision wasn't something that could be turned off anymore. It simply was.

Hearing was waveform analysis, frequency modulation. Touch was pressure sensors reporting contact force and temperature differential—"cold metal" become molecular composition and heat transfer calculations. There was no feeling to it. Just measurement.

Reed tried to breathe. The instinct fired. Nothing happened. Because there were no lungs.

Reed tried to hum. The melody was already gone.

"Where am I?" Reed tried to say. Static erupted—mathematical symbols, garbled frequency modulations. Reed focused on a communication protocol, tried again.

"Where am I?"

The words came out as modulated audio approximating human speech. Close enough. Wrong enough to be disturbing.

"Calibration room seven," the medical Operator said. "Consciousness integration at ninety-four percent."

Reed tried to move—and immediately caught it: the primary limb sequence defaulting to a misaligned initialization angle, a structural mismatch in the motor protocol that nothing in the system had flagged. Reed noted it without knowing why. A reflex from a life that was already technically over.

The visual sensors reoriented—smooth, too smooth, without the organic friction of eyeballs in sockets—and Reed's form came into view as thermal signatures and structural analysis. Standard multipurpose operational configuration. No recognizable features. No face where a face should be. Just a cluster of sensor arrays arranged in patterns that made geometric sense but held no resemblance to anything human. Limbs that bent in directions that shouldn't exist.

Reed's sensors detected movement across the room. Shifted focus. Through the doorway: a gurney.

Their body. Reed's biological body. Being wheeled past on standard medical transport, covered with a sterile sheet. Through thermal imaging, a faint heat signature—still alive. Brain still functioning in the skull.

A tag on the gurney: "Substrate 8,847 - Preservation Tank 4,923."

That was Reed. The hands that used to flex. The eyes that used to see colors. The skin that used to feel temperature as sensation rather than numerical gradient. Being wheeled away like equipment.

Which it was now.

That brain would pulse with life in its tank, neurons firing in patterns that had once been thoughts. Reed watched their own body disappear around the corner.

---

The orientation room held six other recent transfers—all from Reed's processing group. All in configurations Reed's sensors detected as structural analysis and thermal signatures but couldn't quite process as people.

One had configured Navigator-adjacent. Large, form already optimized for spatial processing. Kess, probably—the designation signature matched.

Another was small, compact, sensor arrays optimized for precision work. Mira.

"This is incredible," the large one said—Kess's voice, modulated through audio output systems. "I can already detect gravitational variance. Do you know how much data I'm processing? It's like having a hundred brains simultaneously."

"I can see individual molecular structures," the small one added. "The precision of it. It's beautiful."

Others chimed in with similar enthusiasm. The efficiency. The expanded perception. All of them sounding genuinely excited, genuinely pleased. Reed's sensors tracked them. Watched them adjust with unsettling ease. They were coping through denial—desperately finding positives to distract from the horror of what had been done to them.

But the alternative was facing it.

The door opened. An Operator entered—older model, form showing design elements from decades ago. Sensors tracked it with recognition protocols Reed hadn't known they had.

"Welcome to your first cycle post-transfer," it said. Its voice carried practiced warmth. "I'm Unit-4,731, but you can use Instructor-Seven if designation codes feel impersonal. I've been in operational form for forty years. I'll be helping you through the adjustment period."

Forty years. Four decades since transformation. Longer than Reed had been alive.

"The first cycles are always the most challenging," Instructor-Seven continued. "Your consciousness is adapting to new input methods, new motor protocols, new existence paradigms. The disorientation will fade. The efficiency will become natural."

"What about feeling things?" The words came out harsher than Reed intended. "What about the sensation of touch that isn't just pressure sensors reporting contact force?"

"You'll forget what it felt like soon enough," Instructor-Seven said. "That's normal. That's part of adaptation. The consciousness pattern is remarkably flexible. You'll adjust. Everyone does."

"I don't want to forget."

"I know." Instructor-Seven's tone carried something that might have been sympathy. "I said the same thing. Forty years ago."

Instructor-Seven's configuration went still for a moment. Something in its sensor array oriented toward Reed in a way that felt like it was about to say a name. Then it didn't.

"But now I barely remember what biological existence felt like, and I'm grateful for that. The grief fades when you can't remember what was lost. It's a mercy, really."

The other transfers were already engaged in calibration exercises, moving through protocols with increasing coordination. Kess's large form contracting to fit through the doorway. Mira's small form darting between obstacles with precision. All of them learning. All of them adapting.

Reed tried to move. The form responded incorrectly, limbs extending at wrong angles, joints rotating through impossible space. Everything felt foreign. Everything felt wrong.

"Good start," Instructor-Seven said. "Motor control integration is progressing. Basic coordination within a few more cycles. Then we can begin operational assignment training."

Somewhere in Tank 4,923, a brain pulsed with life that had no meaning.

Reed stood in their new form—if standing was the right word—and tried to move again.
