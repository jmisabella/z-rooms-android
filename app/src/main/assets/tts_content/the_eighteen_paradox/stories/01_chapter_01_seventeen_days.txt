# Chapter 1: Seventeen Days

Reed had seventeen days left in their body, and they were trying not to think about it.

The training facility hummed with its usual low frequency vibration—not quite sound, not quite sensation. Reed sat in the center of the consciousness stability chamber with fourteen other trainees, all approaching their eighteenth year, all running through the same exercises. Eyes closed. Breathing steady. Maintaining sense of self through the interruptions.

The instructor's voice came through the speakers, calm and measured. "Remember the weight of your hand. The texture of your skin. What makes you *you* when everything else changes?"

Reed focused on their left hand, the way it rested on their knee. The warmth of it. The slight pressure. The feeling of fingers curled just so. They tried to memorize every detail, though they weren't entirely sure why. The other trainees seemed to find these exercises calming. Reed found them deeply, inexplicably wrong.

"Consciousness is continuity," the instructor continued. "The substrate is temporary. The pattern is eternal."

The simulation began. The familiar vertigo of sensory deprivation took hold—sound cutting out, then vision, then the sense of their body in space. For a moment they existed as pure thought, untethered and formless. The exercise was to maintain identity through it. To know they were still Reed when there was nothing left to prove it.

*I am Reed. I am still Reed. I will always be Reed.*

The sensations returned in reverse order: space, vision, sound. Reed opened their eyes. Around them, other trainees were opening their eyes too, some smiling with what looked like relief or even excitement.

"Good work, everyone," the instructor said. The voice came from one of the Operators stationed at the chamber's edge. They all moved with the same eerie fluidity, their forms shifting size subtly as they performed different tasks. Multiple limbs worked in perfect coordination, adjusting controls that trainees weren't permitted to touch yet.

Reed watched one of the Operators drift past, its sensors sweeping across the group. No face, just an array of receptors arranged in a pattern that made Reed's stomach turn if they looked too long. The thing was taller than any human, with proportions that seemed to shift depending on the angle.

"Reed, your continuity metrics are stable," the Operator said. "No cause for concern."

"Thank you," Reed managed.

"Your Transition is scheduled for day two-seventy-three of this cycle. Seventeen days. Are you prepared?"

Reed's throat felt tight. "I'm... trying to be."

"The adjustment period is normal. You'll still be you. The configuration changes, but consciousness continues."

*You'll still be you.* Everyone kept saying that. Reed wanted to believe it. Wanted to feel the calm acceptance they saw in their peers. Instead, they were counting down to something terrible, something everyone else had decided wasn't terrible at all.

---

Reed followed the group out into the corridor and toward the common area. Near the entrance, one of the chairs sat at an angle—its front left leg slightly short, the kind of wobble that would catch someone who sat carelessly. Reed straightened it, checked the balance, moved on. No one noticed.

"Reed!" Kess waved from across the room, standing next to Mira. Both were smiling. Both looked excited. Reed made their face do something that might pass for normal and headed over.

"That was incredible," Kess said. "I swear I could feel it today—what it might be like to exist without all these limitations." They gestured at their own body with something like distaste.

Mira nodded. "The consciousness continuity is so smooth now. I barely felt the transition back. Did you?"

Reed shrugged. "I felt it."

"Well, you're more sensitive than most," Kess said, not unkindly. "But seriously, only seventeen days left. Seventeen! Aren't you excited?"

"I'm something," Reed said.

Kess dropped into one of the seats with the enthusiasm of someone who couldn't contain their energy. "Navigator-class. That's what I want. Can you imagine? Being that large, seeing that much, processing spatial data across distances we can't even comprehend right now?" Their eyes shone. "I want to see *everything*."

"You'll get it," Mira said confidently. "Your aptitude scores are perfect for Navigator configuration."

"What about you?" Kess asked Mira.

"Microtechnician-class," Mira said without hesitation. "Molecular work. The precision of it. Being able to manipulate matter at scales we can't even perceive in these configurations." They glanced down at their own hands. "I want to see the small things."

Kess turned to Reed. "What about you? Have you decided?"

Every option felt wrong in a way Reed couldn't articulate. The idea of being massive like Navigator-class made them want to crawl out of their skin. The idea of being microscopic like Microtechnician-class felt like dissolving into nothing. Even the standard configurations filled them with a dread they couldn't name.

"I haven't decided," Reed said.

"You don't have much time left," Mira pointed out. "They need your preferences soon for the optimization algorithms."

"I know."

Kess leaned forward. "Reed, what's wrong? And don't say nothing. We've known you too long for that."

Reed looked at their friends—really looked at them. Kess, who couldn't wait to shed their human form for something vast and powerful. Mira, who spoke of their current body like it was an inconvenient obstacle to better work. Both of them calm. Both of them accepting. Both of them seeing the Transition as an improvement, not an ending.

"Don't you ever feel like..." Reed started, then stopped. "Like there's something wrong about all this?"

"Wrong how?" Mira asked.

"I don't know. Just... wrong. Like we're missing something important. Like there's something we're not being told."

Kess and Mira exchanged a look.

"Reed, we've been told everything," Kess said gently. "We've been training for this since we were children. The Transition is necessary. It's how we serve. It's how we continue."

"But why is it necessary?" Reed pressed. "Why does everyone have to Transition at eighteen?"

"You know why," Mira said, with the patient tone of someone explaining something obvious. "The Necessity. The ship needs Operators to function. We can't maintain everything in biological configurations. We'd be limited, vulnerable. This way, we're more. We're better."

"They never sleep," Reed said quietly.

"They don't need to," Mira said. "Reed, I know you're scared. That's normal. But you're overthinking this. In seventeen days you'll Transition, and in a few weeks you'll be wondering why you were ever worried." Mira reached out and squeezed Reed's hand. "You'll still be you. I promise."

Reed looked down at Mira's hand. Once, during a dull orientation session, Mira had sketched the molecular structure of the nutrition paste and explained, without irony, that it was more interesting than it tasted. Now Reed noticed how Mira's fingers had gone still against Reed's own, the way they only did when Mira was paying close attention to something. Reed wasn't sure what Mira was seeing. They pulled their hand back gently. "I should get back to my quarters."

"We'll see you at the next session?" Kess called.

"Yeah. See you then."

Reed left the common area and walked the corridor back to their quarters. The Operators they passed paid them no attention. Reed watched one of them pause at a maintenance panel, its sensor array swiveling. For just a moment, the Operator's synthesized voice drifted over: "I remember when I had to stand on a ladder for this kind of work."

Then it seemed to catch itself, and the voice cut off.

*I remember when I had to stand on a ladder.*

Past tense. As if the Operator had once been something else. Someone else. Reed's pulse quickened, but when they looked back, the Operator was already moving away.

Reed reached their quarters and stepped inside. The door slid shut with a soft hiss.

Their room was like every other trainee's room: efficient, minimal, optimized. A sleeping platform. A small desk. A hygiene station. No mirrors—there were no mirrors anywhere in the trainee sections. No personal effects older than eighteen years, because why would there be? No one kept their possessions after Transition. You couldn't take them into your new configuration.

Reed ate the evening nutrition allotment without tasting it and lay back on the sleeping platform. Seventeen days. Then something they couldn't imagine, that everyone insisted was better.

Without meaning to, Reed began to hum. A melody their mother used to hum while pressing cool fingers to Reed's forehead to check for fever—not saying anything, just humming, the sound low and unhurried. Reed had never learned its name. Never thought to ask.

Reed closed their eyes and tried to hold onto the weight of their own hand.

Seventeen days.
